⏱ Critical Deadlines at a Glance

  • 10 business days before closing: Submit completed questionnaire + offering statement/prospectus to DTC's Underwriting Dept. via UW SOURCE or UWC.
  • 7 business days before closing: Provide complete CUSIP INFORMATION (numbers, interest rates, final maturities) in writing.
  • 10 business days after closing: Transfer Agent must submit Agent Confirmation via email to DTC's Underwriting Department.
  • 1 business day before closing: Deliver signed Letter of Representations (if BEO) and physical securities by 12:00 noon ET.
  • Corporate issues: CUSIP info due upon pricing (not a fixed pre-closing deadline).
Note: Per the June 2026 OA, these timeframes are "standards and not deadlines" — DTC may extend any timeframe in its sole discretion. However, do not rely on extensions; plan for the standard timelines.
To make a new issue of securities DTC eligible, the completed questionnaire and a copy of the offering statement/prospectus (preliminary or final) must be submitted to DTC's Underwriting Dept. at least 10 business days prior to the issue's closing date. (With respect to corporate issues, upon pricing of the issue.)

If complete CUSIP INFORMATION (CUSIP numbers, interest rates, and final maturities) is not included with the questionnaire, please provide to DTC in writing at least seven business days prior to the closing date.

Agent Confirmation: The Transfer Agent must submit an Agent Confirmation via email to DTC's Underwriting Department within ten (10) business days after the Closing Date. This confirms the agent has executed an Operational Arrangements Agent Letter and is prepared to perform its duties.

Electronic submission: DTC now accepts submissions through the UW SOURCE or Underwriting Central (UWC) platforms. Paper submissions are still accepted but electronic is preferred.

Red Flags: DTC's Discretionary Powers

Absolute Discretion on Counsel

DTC reserves absolute discretion to approve or reject the counsel providing a legal opinion. Even a well-known firm can be rejected if DTC determines it lacks sufficient securities expertise or has a conflict of interest.

Deadline Extensions Are Discretionary

The OA states all timeframes are "standards and not deadlines." DTC may extend deadlines, but never count on it. Missing the 10-day submission window can delay or derail a closing.

Indemnity Letters for Restricted Issuers

Issuers with ownership restrictions (REITs, NOL preservation provisions, maritime issuers) must provide an indemnity letter protecting DTC and Participants. DTC may require this even if not explicitly listed in the standard questionnaire.

OFAC Sanctions Compliance

All issuers, agents, and underwriters must agree not to conduct any transaction through DTC that violates OFAC sanctions. This is a hard stop — DTC will deny eligibility if sanctions concerns exist.

Certificate Sizing >$500M

If any single maturity exceeds $500 million, the issuer must provide multiple certificates (one per $500M tranche). A single oversized certificate will be rejected.

Foreign Issuer Legal Opinions

For non-U.S. issuers, DTC may require a foreign legal opinion confirming that DTC's rights as nominee are enforceable under local law. This can add significant time and cost.

DTC Can Discontinue Services Anytime

DTC reserves the right to discontinue providing depository services for any security at any time by giving reasonable notice. This is not merely a theoretical risk — DTC has exercised this right for securities with persistent operational failures, compliance violations, or issuer insolvency.

Non-U.S. Currency Requires Special Rider

Securities denominated in or paying interest in non-USD currencies require a distinct LOR rider separate from the UK/Ireland rider. This is often overlooked for euro-denominated bonds from non-UK issuers. The rider addresses FX conversion mechanics and payment timing.

Municipal Serial Maturities BEO

Scenario A: State of Franklin General Obligation Bonds, Series 2025A

The Offering: $250 million GO bond with serial maturities from 2026–2035, issued as Book-Entry-Only. Lead underwriter is a DTC Participant. Transfer Agent is U.S. Bank.

Questionnaire Walkthrough:

  • Issue Type: Check "Municipal." Do NOT check "Limited Underwriting" because principal exceeds $1 million.
  • Article 8 Security: Yes — municipal bonds are clearly securities under NY UCC Article 8.
  • UK Entity: No — issuer is a U.S. state.
  • Registration: Municipal securities are exempt from SEC registration under Rule 3(a)(2). Check "Exempt under Rule 3(a)(2)."
  • CUSIP Table: You need 10 rows (one per serial maturity). Each gets its own CUSIP, interest rate, and maturity date. Type code = "S" for Serial.
  • BEO: Yes — no certificates for investors. A Letter of Representations is required. Check if the State of Franklin already has a Blanket LOR on file with DTC. If yes, no new LOR needed.
  • Put/Tender: No — standard GO bonds without optional redemption features.
  • Agent Confirmation: U.S. Bank (Transfer Agent) must submit confirmation via email within 10 business days after closing.
Key Takeaway: Serial maturities multiply your CUSIP workload. Each maturity is a separate security in DTC's system. Verify the issuer's BLOR status before closing to avoid last-minute LOR execution.
Corporate Rule 144A Non-Investment Grade

Scenario B: Acme Corp. 8.500% Senior Notes due 2030 (Rule 144A)

The Offering: $500 million high-yield notes sold only to QIBs under Rule 144A. Distinct CUSIP from any registered notes. Lead underwriter is a DTC Participant.

Questionnaire Walkthrough:

  • Issue Type: Check "Corporate."
  • Registration: Check "Eligible for resale under Rule 144A of the '33 Act."
  • CUSIP: Must be distinct from any registered Acme Corp. notes. The CUSIP table gets one row (term bond). Type code = "T" for Term.
  • LOR Requirements: Because this is Rule 144A, a rider to the LOR is required. The rider confirms that the securities are legally restricted and that Cede & Co. will be the registered owner of the restricted securities.
  • PORTAL: Historically required for non-IG 144A issues. Per current OA, only the distinct CUSIP and LOR rider are required. Private trading venues have replaced PORTAL.
  • Electronic Submission: Submit through UW SOURCE upon pricing. CUSIP info is due upon pricing (not 7 days pre-close).
  • Certificate Sizing: $500 million exactly — one certificate for the full amount is acceptable (threshold is exceeding $500M). If it were $500,000,001, you'd need two certificates.
Key Takeaway: Rule 144A issues trigger the most LOR complexity. Always confirm the CUSIP is distinct from registered securities of the same class. The LOR rider is easy to overlook but mandatory.
Corporate UK Entity SEC Registered

Scenario C: Thames Industries plc — SEC-Registered Notes

The Offering: UK-incorporated issuer doing a registered offering of $300 million notes under Form F-3. Lead underwriter is a DTC Participant. Transfer Agent is Computershare.

Questionnaire Walkthrough:

  • UK Entity: Check "Yes." This triggers the UK/Ireland LOR rider, which addresses conflicts of law and insolvency considerations.
  • Registration: Check "Registered with the SEC."
  • State of Incorporation: "United Kingdom" — this may trigger a request for a UK legal opinion confirming DTC's rights.
  • LOR: Standard Issuer LOR plus the UK/Ireland rider. Because this is registered (not 144A/Reg S), no additional restriction rider is needed.
  • Signatures: Because the issuer is non-U.S., original manual signatures are required on the LOR. Electronic signatures are NOT permitted for non-U.S. entities per the OA.
  • Agent Confirmation: Computershare must confirm within 10 business days after closing. Ensure they have an executed Operational Arrangements Agent Letter on file with DTC.
  • OFAC: The underwriter must confirm no OFAC sanctions apply to Thames Industries or its principals.
Key Takeaway: Foreign issuers add layers. The UK rider, potential foreign legal opinion, and original signature requirement can each add days to the timeline. Start the LOR process early.
Corporate UK Entity Rule 144A + Reg S BEO Put Feature

Scenario D: British Telecom plc — Dual 144A/Reg S Notes with Put Option

The Offering: £400 million notes issued by a UK-incorporated entity, eligible for resale under both Rule 144A to QIBs and Regulation S to non-U.S. persons. Book-Entry-Only with a 3-year put option. Lead underwriter is a DTC Participant. Transfer Agent is Euroclear Bank.

Questionnaire Walkthrough:

  • UK Entity: Check "Yes." This triggers the UK/Ireland LOR rider addressing conflicts of law, insolvency, and enforceability under UK law.
  • Article 8 Security: Yes — notes are clearly securities under NY UCC Article 8.
  • Registration: Check BOTH "Eligible for resale under Rule 144A of the '33 Act" AND "Eligible for resale under Regulation S of the '33 Act." This dual eligibility is increasingly common for global issuers.
  • BEO: Yes — Book-Entry-Only with no certificates available to investors. Requires LOR execution.
  • Put/Tender: Yes — the notes have a 3-year put option requiring a Tender Letter of Representations (Tender LOR) in addition to the standard Issuer LOR.
  • LOR Requirements: This is the most complex scenario requiring THREE LOR components: (1) Standard Issuer LOR, (2) UK/Ireland rider, and (3) Tender LOR. Because of the 144A eligibility, ensure the LOR rider addresses restricted securities and Cede & Co. ownership.
  • Signatures: Original manual signatures required on ALL LOR components because the issuer is non-U.S. Electronic signatures are not permitted.
  • CUSIP: Must be distinct from any registered British Telecom notes. The dual 144A/Reg S structure requires separation from any U.S. registered securities of the same class.
  • Currency: £ (GBP) denomination — may trigger a non-U.S. currency rider in addition to the UK rider. Verify with DTC if both riders are required for GBP-denominated UK issuer securities.
  • Agent Confirmation: Euroclear Bank must confirm within 10 business days after closing. Ensure they have an executed Operational Arrangements Agent Letter with DTC and understand BEO mechanics.
  • OFAC: Underwriter must confirm no OFAC sanctions apply to British Telecom or its principals.
Key Takeaway: This scenario represents maximum complexity — UK entity + dual 144A/Reg S + BEO + put feature + foreign currency. The combination of UK rider, potential currency rider, Tender LOR, and 144A restrictions means the LOR package becomes quite involved. Start the LOR process at least 3-4 weeks before closing, and verify with DTC whether both UK and currency riders are required for GBP-denominated issues.
Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

❌ Missing the Agent Confirmation

The Transfer Agent must submit an Agent Confirmation via email to DTC's Underwriting Department within 10 business days after the Closing Date. This confirms the agent has executed an Operational Arrangements Agent Letter with DTC and is prepared to perform its duties. This requirement was updated in the June 2026 OA (changed from pre-closing to post-closing timing).

❌ Wrong Certificate Size for Large Maturities

If a single maturity exceeds $500 million, you must provide multiple certificates (one per $500M tranche plus remainder). A single $750M certificate will be rejected. This applies to both BEO and certificated issues.

❌ Assuming a BLOR Covers a New Issuer Name

If the issuer's legal name on the offering document differs from the name on the existing Blanket LOR (e.g., due to a merger or reorganization), DTC will require a new LOR or legal opinion. Always verify the exact name match.

❌ Using the Same CUSIP for 144A and Registered Securities

DTC requires distinct CUSIPs for restricted and unrestricted securities of the same class. Using the same CUSIP will cause eligibility rejection and may create settlement failures.

❌ Forgetting the Indemnity Letter for REITs / NOL Issuers

Issuers with ownership restrictions (e.g., REITs preserving their tax status, or companies with NOL carryforwards) must provide an indemnity letter. DTC will not process eligibility without it, and it's not explicitly requested on the standard questionnaire.

❌ Electronic Signature for Non-U.S. Entities

While the June 2026 OA permits electronic signatures in many cases, non-U.S. entities and legal opinions still require original manual signatures. Using DocuSign for a UK issuer's LOR will result in rejection.

Other Eligibility Paths

The standard new-issue questionnaire is not the only path to DTC eligibility. These alternative paths apply to specific situations.

Older Issue Eligibility

For already-issued and outstanding securities (not new underwritten offerings), DTC uses a distinct process:

  • Submit an Older Eligibility Questionnaire (different form)
  • Present physical certificates for the outstanding securities
  • Provide an Agent Attestation form
  • Submit through UW SOURCE as an "Eligibility Only" request
This path is common for secondary market listings, private placements becoming tradable, or legacy securities seeking DTC access.

Custody-Only Services

Some securities qualify only for limited custody service, not full nominee registration or book-entry transfer:

  • Securities with transfer restrictions that prevent free book-entry movement
  • Non-fungible bulk securities (e.g., physical commodities, unique certificates)
  • Issues where DTC holds certificates but does not register Cede & Co. as nominee
Custody-only securities cannot be settled through DTC's book-entry system. They are held physically and transferred manually.

Money Market Instruments (MMI)

MMI are processed under dedicated DTC Rules with special form letters and a separate help line:

  • Commercial paper, banker's acceptances, and short-term notes
  • Dedicated customer help line: (866) 724-4402
  • Specialized MMI form letters instead of standard LORs
  • Processing under DTC Rules rather than Operational Arrangements
MMI eligibility is faster but has different documentation requirements. Always use the MMI-specific forms.

Segregation Account 100 (Seg 100)

For issuers with ownership restrictions (shipping, FCC, gaming licenses), DTC offers the Seg 100 compliance tool:

  • Allows Participants to track and restrict ownership for regulatory compliance
  • DTC does not monitor compliance — it provides the tool only
  • Requires an indemnity letter from the issuer
  • Commonly used by REITs, maritime issuers, and broadcast licensees
Seg 100 is not an eligibility path per se, but a post-eligibility compliance mechanism. The standard questionnaire still applies.

Retail Certificates of Deposit (E-CD)

Retail CDs use a dedicated Electronic Master Certificate BLOR:

  • Specialized form distinct from standard corporate or municipal LORs
  • Covers all CDs issued under a master certificate program
  • Requires bank issuer to maintain the master certificate at DTC
  • Individual CDs are issued in book-entry form without separate certificates
Bank issuers should contact DTC's Corporate Eligibility team (212-855-3782) for the E-CD BLOR template.

Unit Securities — Separation Rules

Unit securities (debt + equity/warrant combined) have special separation rules:

  • Voluntary separation: At closing, the underwriter may elect to separate the unit into its components, each receiving its own CUSIP
  • Mandatory separation: After closing, DTC may require separation if the components trade independently
  • Each component must independently qualify for DTC eligibility
  • The unit itself receives a CUSIP, plus each component receives its own
Coordinate with DTC Underwriting before closing if your offering includes units. The separation decision affects CUSIP assignment and settlement.

Study Guide & Section-by-Section Checklist

Before You Start (Issue Information)

  • Confirm issuer name matches organizational documents exactly
  • Verify BLOR status or prepare new LOR/ILOR
  • Request CUSIP assignment from CGS (S&P Global)
  • Determine if issue is BEO (triggers LOR requirement)
  • Check for put/tender features (triggers Tender LOR)
  • Confirm UK/Ireland status (triggers special LOR rider)

Contact Information Checklist

  • Lead Underwriter is a DTC Participant or has clearing Participant
  • Transfer Agent has executed OA Agent Letter with DTC
  • Transfer Agent will submit Agent Confirmation within 10 business days after closing
  • Paying Agent coordinated with DTC income collection system
  • Issuer's Counsel is independent, licensed, and experienced
  • All phone numbers are direct lines (not general desks)

Timing Checklist (Working Back from Closing)

  • 10 days pre-close: Questionnaire + docs via UW SOURCE/UWC
  • 7 days pre-close: CUSIP info in writing (municipal)
  • Upon pricing: CUSIP info (corporate)
  • 10 days post-close: Agent Confirmation from Transfer Agent
  • 1 day pre-close: LOR + securities delivery by noon ET
  • Closing day: DTC credits Participant account

Special Cases & Red Flags

  • UK/Ireland issuers: Special LOR rider + original signatures
  • Rule 144A: Distinct CUSIP + LOR rider
  • Reg S: Distinct CUSIP + distribution compliance period
  • 3(c)(7): All owners must be Qualified Purchasers
  • Foreign issuers: Additional legal opinions may be required
  • REITs/NOL issuers: Indemnity letter required
  • OFAC: Confirm no sanctions apply to issuer or principals
  • Non-USD currency: Non-U.S. currency LOR rider required (distinct from UK rider)
  • Correspondent relationships: Non-Participant submitters must have approved correspondent agreement

The Depository Trust Company (DTC)

Definition
The Depository Trust Company

The world's largest securities depository, holding over $100 trillion in assets. (DTC surpassed this milestone in June 2025; the exact figure moves quarter to quarter.) Founded in 1973 and headquartered in New York City, DTC is a subsidiary of The Depository Trust & Clearing Corporation (DTCC).

DTC provides custody and book-entry transfer services for eligible securities. When securities are "DTC-eligible," they can be held, transferred, and settled electronically through DTC's systems rather than via physical certificate delivery.

DTC's nominee, Cede & Co., is the registered owner of record for all securities held in DTC's depository. Beneficial owners hold their interests through DTC Participants (broker-dealers, banks, and other financial institutions).

DTC was created to solve the "paperwork crisis" of the late 1960s and early 1970s, when the surge in trading volume overwhelmed the manual, certificate-based settlement system.

Source: Securities Exchange Act of 1934, §17A(a); DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I. dtcc.com

The Depository Trust & Clearing Corporation (DTCC)

Parent Organization
DTCC

The parent company of DTC and other critical financial infrastructure organizations. A systemically important financial market utility overseen by the Federal Reserve, SEC, and CFTC.

DTCC's subsidiaries include:

  • DTC — Depository Trust Company (securities custody & book-entry)
  • NSCC — National Securities Clearing Corporation (equity trade clearing)
  • FICC — Fixed Income Clearing Corporation (bond clearing)
Source: 17 CFR §240; SEC Oversight. dtcc.com

What does "DTC Eligible" mean?

Eligibility Standard
DTC Eligible

A security that DTC has accepted for its book-entry delivery, settlement, and depository services, enabling electronic trading and settlement.

DTC eligibility is the gateway to the U.S. clearing and settlement infrastructure. Without it, trades would require manual handling of physical certificates, dramatically reducing liquidity and increasing counterparty risk.

Generally, issues eligible for DTC services are those issued in a transaction that: (i) has been registered with the SEC under the Securities Act of 1933; (ii) was exempt from registration under a Securities Act exemption that does not involve transfer or ownership restrictions; or (iii) permits resale pursuant to Rule 144A or Regulation S.

To be DTC-eligible, issues must generally: Be registered with the SEC or qualify for an exemption without transfer restrictions · Not have ownership restrictions conflicting with DTC holding as nominee · Have proper documentation (Letter of Representations) · Have valid CUSIP numbers · Meet special requirements for the security type
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.1. dtcc.com

UW SOURCE / Underwriting Central (UWC)

Electronic Submission Platform
UW SOURCE / UWC

DTC's online platforms for submitting eligibility requests, offering documents, and supporting materials. UW SOURCE (Securities Origination, Underwriting and Reliable Corporate Action Environment) and Underwriting Central (UWC) have replaced paper-based submission as the preferred method.

What Changes with Electronic Submission

  • Faster processing: Electronic submissions are routed directly into DTC's underwriting workflow, reducing manual data entry delays.
  • Document management: Offering statements, prospectuses, and legal opinions can be uploaded as PDFs rather than mailed or faxed.
  • Status tracking: Submitters can track the status of their eligibility request in real time.
  • Not mandatory: Paper submissions are still accepted, but electronic is strongly preferred and may process faster.
Access: UW SOURCE and UWC are available to DTC Participants and authorized users. Contact your DTC relationship manager or DTC's Client Services for access credentials.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.A.1 — Submission Requirements (June 2026). dtcc.com

Agent Confirmation

Updated Requirement (June 2026 OA)
Agent Confirmation

A written confirmation from the Transfer Agent (or other designated agent) submitted via email to DTC's Underwriting Department within ten (10) business days after the Closing Date, confirming the agent has executed an Operational Arrangements Agent Letter with DTC and is prepared to perform its duties.

Why This Matters

The Agent Confirmation requirement was updated in the June 2026 OA to provide post-closing timing (changed from 3 business days before closing). This ensures Transfer Agents are properly onboarded and contractually bound to DTC while providing flexibility for the closing process.

What the Transfer Agent Must Confirm

  • It has executed an Operational Arrangements Agent Letter with DTC.
  • It understands its responsibilities for the specific issue (income collection, transfers, corporate actions).
  • It will comply with DTC's instructions and timelines.
Action item: Confirm with your Transfer Agent that they have an active OA Agent Letter with DTC before the closing date. If they don't, the agent must execute one, which can take several days. The Agent Confirmation itself is due within 10 business days after closing.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.A — Eligibility Requirements (June 2026). dtcc.com

DTC Underwriting Department

Department
Underwriting Department

DTC's division responsible for reviewing and processing all new issue eligibility requests, evaluating whether proposed securities meet DTC's operational, legal, and risk criteria.

Key Deadlines for Submission

  • 10 business days before closing: Completed questionnaire + preliminary offering document (via UW SOURCE/UWC preferred)
  • 7 business days before closing: CUSIP numbers, principal amount, interest rate, maturity date
  • 10 business days after closing: Agent Confirmation from Transfer Agent
  • 1 business day before closing: Signed Letter of Representations
  • 12:00 p.m. ET, business day before closing: Physical certificates or FAST confirmation
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.2; NABL "Demystifying DTC" (2023), Appendix I. nabl.org

Closing Date

Transaction Milestone
Closing Date

The date on which the issuer delivers the securities to the underwriter and receives the proceeds of the offering. All DTC eligibility documentation must be completed before this date.

The 10-business-day submission deadline is measured backward from this date. For corporate issues, the CUSIP information trigger is "upon pricing" rather than a fixed pre-closing deadline, reflecting that corporate pricing often occurs closer to closing than in municipal offerings.

Critical: If the closing date changes (a "delayed closing"), notify DTC's Underwriting Department immediately. DTC may need to adjust its processing schedule, and the Letter of Representations may need to be re-dated. Per the OA, DTC may extend deadlines in its sole discretion, but never rely on this.
Source: DTC Eligibility Questionnaire instructions; DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.2. dtcc.com

CUSIP — Committee on Uniform Securities Identification Procedures

Identifier System
CUSIP Number

A unique 9-character alphanumeric identifier assigned to each security issue, enabling standardized tracking across the U.S. financial system. Administered by CUSIP Global Services (CGS), a division of S&P Global.

For DTC eligibility, complete CUSIP information must be provided according to specific timing requirements. For corporate issues, this is triggered upon pricing rather than a fixed pre-closing deadline.

CUSIP Format
9-character code (e.g. "037833100")

First 6 characters: Issuer code · Characters 7–8: Security type and issue identifier · Character 9: Check digit

CUSIP Assignment Procedures

DTC's operational arrangements specify detailed CUSIP assignment requirements:

  • Municipal Securities: CUSIP information due 7 business days before closing
  • Corporate Securities: CUSIP information due upon pricing (no fixed pre-closing deadline)
  • MMI Securities: Special CUSIP assignment for Money Market Instruments
  • Rapid Issuance: Expedited CUSIP assignment for certain programmatic issuances

Special CUSIP Requirements

Certain security types have special CUSIP assignment requirements:

  • Rule 144A Securities: Must use different CUSIP/CINS from unrestricted securities of same class
  • Regulation S Securities: Must use different CUSIP/CINS from U.S. registered securities
  • Dual 144A/Reg S: May require single or multiple CUSIPs depending on structure
  • Serial Maturities: Each maturity requires separate CUSIP (e.g., 10-year serial = 10 CUSIPs)
  • Unit Securities: Components may have separate CUSIPs from the unit itself

CUSIP Assignment Timing

  • Standard Assignment: Typically 2-3 business days after application to CUSIP Services
  • Expedited Assignment: Available for urgent issuances (may incur additional fees)
  • Program Assignment: Pre-assigned CUSIP ranges for frequent issuers
  • MMI Assignment: Special procedures for money market instruments

CUSIP Information Required

For DTC eligibility, the following CUSIP information must be provided:

  • Full CUSIP Number: Complete 9-character identifier
  • Security Description: Full name of security as it will appear on certificate
  • Issue Amount: Principal or par amount of the issue
  • Maturity Dates: For serial bonds, all maturity dates
  • Interest Rates: Coupon rates for each maturity/CUSIP
Operational Requirement: CUSIP information that is incomplete, incorrect, or late can result in eligibility denial or processing delays. For Rule 144A and Regulation S securities, failure to use distinct CUSIPs from registered securities is a common cause of rejection. See Operational Arrangements Section II.A.1
Assignment Tip: For complex issues with multiple maturities or features, create a CUSIP schedule early in the process. Verify CUSIP distinctness for restricted securities before submitting eligibility questionnaire.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.1.b. dtcc.com

Municipal Securities

Security Type
Municipal Securities

Debt obligations issued by states, cities, counties, and other governmental entities to fund public projects. Typically exempt from federal income tax under Section 103 of the Internal Revenue Code.

For DTC eligibility, municipal issues follow a distinct processing path from corporate issues. DTC offers a Limited Underwriting service for non-Book-Entry-Only municipal issues with a principal value of $1 million or less.

Source: DTC Operational Arrangements; SEC Form of Letter of Representations (Municipal Issues). sec.gov

Corporate Securities

Security Type
Corporate Securities

Debt (bonds, notes, debentures) and equity (common stock, preferred stock) issued by private and public corporations.

Corporate issues must comply with SEC registration requirements or qualify for an exemption. If issued under Rule 144A or Regulation S, additional riders to the Letter of Representations are required, and the securities must carry a distinct CUSIP from any registered securities of the same class.

For corporate issues, CUSIP information must be provided to DTC upon pricing of the issue (rather than at a fixed pre-closing deadline), reflecting that pricing often occurs close to closing.

Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.1; SEC Exhibit 4.4 (Becton Dickinson 144A/Reg S Notes). sec.gov

Limited Underwriting

DTC Service
Limited Underwriting

A streamlined DTC service for non-Book-Entry-Only municipal issues with a principal value of $1 million or less, reducing documentation and operational burden for smaller offerings.

Unlike full underwriting eligibility, limited underwriting does not require the same level of operational infrastructure, making it accessible to smaller issuers. Even under limited underwriting, the issuer must still submit the eligibility questionnaire and comply with DTC's basic eligibility criteria.

Source: DTC Eligibility Questionnaire Footnote 1; DTC Operational Arrangements. colonialstock.com

ABS — Asset-Backed Securities

Structured Product
Asset-Backed Securities (ABS)

Financial instruments collateralized by a pool of underlying assets — auto loans, credit card receivables, student loans, or mortgages. Cash flows from these assets service payments to ABS investors.

ABS are typically structured through a special purpose vehicle (SPV) or trust that isolates the assets from the originator's balance sheet. For DTC eligibility, ABS must meet the same general criteria as other securities: SEC registration or a qualifying exemption, and compliance with DTC's Operational Arrangements.

Source: SEC Regulation AB; DTC Operational Arrangements. dtcc.com

CMO — Collateralized Mortgage Obligation

Structured Product
Collateralized Mortgage Obligation (CMO)

A mortgage-backed security that pools mortgage loans and repackages them into tranches with varying risk profiles, maturities, and payment priorities. Created in 1983 by Freddie Mac and Salomon Brothers.

Each tranche receives principal and interest payments in a specific order (the "waterfall"), with senior tranches paid first and junior tranches absorbing prepayment risk. For DTC eligibility, CMOs must be issued by a qualified issuer (typically a government-sponsored enterprise or a REMIC trust).

Source: SEC Regulation AB; DTC Operational Arrangements. CMOs are governed by the Tax Reform Act of 1986 (REMIC provisions). dtcc.com

Article 8 of the NY UCC — "Security" Definition

Legal Framework
Article 8 — Investment Securities

The New York Uniform Commercial Code article governing investment securities. Defines what constitutes a "security" and provides the legal basis for the indirect holding system used by DTC.

Article 8 defines a "security" as an obligation of an issuer or a share, participation, or other interest in an issuer or in property or an enterprise of an issuer that:

  • Is represented by a security certificate in bearer or registered form, or transferable upon books maintained by or on behalf of the issuer;
  • Is one of a class or series of shares, participations, interests, or obligations; and
  • Is, or is of a type, dealt in or traded on securities exchanges or securities markets, or is a medium for investment.

This definition is critical because DTC's book-entry system operates under Article 8. If an instrument is not a "security" under Article 8, it cannot be held in DTC's depository system. New York is the governing jurisdiction because DTC is a New York corporation and the UCC Article 8 framework validates the indirect holding system (where Cede & Co. is the registered owner).

Practical guidance: Answer "Yes" for virtually all debt and equity instruments. Answer "No" only if the instrument does not meet the UCC Article 8 definition (rare).
Source: NY UCC Article 8 (1994 Revised Article 8); DTC Operational Arrangements. cornell.edu (UCC Article 8)

New York Uniform Commercial Code (NY UCC)

State Law
New York Uniform Commercial Code

New York's codification of the Uniform Commercial Code, the body of law governing commercial transactions. Article 8 (Investment Securities) provides the legal foundation for DTC's book-entry system.

Article 8 of the NY UCC was substantially revised in 1994 to accommodate the indirect holding system, where securities are held through intermediaries rather than directly by beneficial owners. Under this system, DTC's nominee (Cede & Co.) is the registered owner on the issuer's books, while beneficial owners hold their interests through DTC Participants. This is legally validated by NY UCC Section 8-503 and related provisions.

Source: NY UCC Article 8 (1994 Revised); DTC Operational Arrangements. cornell.edu (UCC Article 8)

United Kingdom Entity

Jurisdictional Classification
United Kingdom Entity

An issuer incorporated or organized under the laws of the United Kingdom. UK and Ireland issuers require a special rider to the Letter of Representations.

DTC requires a specific rider to the Letter of Representations for issuers organized under the laws of the United Kingdom or Ireland. This rider addresses unique legal and operational considerations, including potential conflicts of law issues and the applicability of UK/Irish insolvency laws.

The UK entity designation also affects how securities are cleared and settled, particularly for cross-border transactions involving Euroclear and Clearstream. A UK legal opinion confirming DTC's rights may also be required.

Important: For non-U.S. entities, original manual signatures are required on the LOR. Electronic signatures are not permitted.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.2 (LOR riders for UK/Ireland issuers). dtcc.com

Securities Act of 1933

Federal Statute
Securities Act of 1933

The federal law governing the initial issuance and registration of securities. Requires issuers to register offerings with the SEC and provide full disclosure through a prospectus. Often called the "'33 Act."

Enacted after the 1929 stock market crash, the Act requires that a registration statement be filed with the SEC before securities can be sold to the public. Several exemptions exist, including Rule 144A, Regulation S, and Rule 3(a)(2).

Source: Securities Act of 1933 (15 U.S.C. §77a et seq.); SEC Division of Corporation Finance. sec.gov

Investment Company Act of 1940

Federal Statute
Investment Company Act of 1940

The federal law regulating companies that engage primarily in investing, reinvesting, and trading in securities. Often called the "'40 Act." Provides exemptions for private funds under Sections 3(c)(1) and 3(c)(7).

Private investment funds (hedge funds, private equity) can avoid registration under:

  • Section 3(c)(1): Limits beneficial owners to 100 persons (or 250 for qualifying venture capital funds)
  • Section 3(c)(7): No ownership limit, but all owners must be "qualified purchasers" ($5M for individuals, $25M for entities)
Source: Investment Company Act of 1940 (15 U.S.C. §80a-1 et seq.); SEC Division of Investment Management. sec.gov

SEC Registered Securities

Registration Status
SEC Registered

Securities issued pursuant to an effective registration statement filed with the SEC under the Securities Act of 1933. The cleanest path to DTC eligibility — no transfer restrictions, single CUSIP, no special LOR riders required.

The registration process involves filing a registration statement (Form S-1, S-3, F-1, etc.), SEC review and comment, and declaration of effectiveness by the SEC before any sales can occur.

Source: Securities Act of 1933, Section 5; DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.1. dtcc.com

Rule 144A — Private Resale to QIBs

SEC Safe Harbor
Rule 144A

A safe harbor under the Securities Act of 1933 that permits the resale of restricted securities to Qualified Institutional Buyers (QIBs) — institutions that own and invest on a discretionary basis at least $100 million in securities of unaffiliated issuers — without SEC registration.

Key Requirements for DTC Eligibility

  • Securities must be Legally or Contractually Restricted at the time of initial registration in the name of Cede & Co.
  • Must carry a distinct CUSIP or CINS number different from any unrestricted securities of the same class.
  • A rider to the Letter of Representations is required.
  • For non-investment grade issues, private trading venues (successors to PORTAL) facilitate QIB trading. No separate "PORTAL eligibility" is required by DTC.
Source: SEC Rule 144A (17 CFR §230.144A); DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.1.b. sec.gov

Regulation S — Offshore Offerings

SEC Safe Harbor
Regulation S

A safe harbor under the Securities Act of 1933 for offers and sales of securities outside the United States. Designed to facilitate access to international capital markets without full SEC registration.

Distribution Compliance Period

Regulation S prohibits offers and sales to U.S. persons during the distribution compliance period. For debt securities, this is typically 40 days. For equity securities, the period is one year for non-reporting issuers or six months for reporting issuers. During this period, beneficial interests may only be held through Euroclear and Clearstream as DTC participants.

Restriction
U.S. Person Prohibition

During the compliance period, securities cannot be purchased or held by U.S. persons, except in limited circumstances. This includes direct purchases and indirect holdings through U.S. intermediaries.

Key Requirements for DTC Eligibility

  • Must have a distinct CUSIP or CINS number different from any registered securities of the same class.
  • Prior to expiration of the Distribution Compliance Period (40 days for debt, 1 year for equity), beneficial interests may only be held through Euroclear and Clearstream as DTC participants.
  • A rider to the Letter of Representations is required to address the restricted nature of Regulation S securities.
  • Dual 144A/Reg S: Many offerings combine Rule 144A and Regulation S eligibility. In these cases, the 144A LOR rider (addressing restricted securities and Cede & Co. ownership) typically covers both exemptions.
  • Global offerings: Regulation S is commonly used in global offerings where issuers want to reach both U.S. institutional investors (via 144A) and international investors (via Reg S).
Source: SEC Regulation S (17 CFR §230.901 et seq.); DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.1.b. sec.gov

Section 3(c)(7) of the Investment Company Act

Exemption
Section 3(c)(7)

An exemption from registration as an "investment company" for issuers whose outstanding securities are owned exclusively by "qualified purchasers" — individuals with $5M+ in investments, entities with $25M+.

Unlike Section 3(c)(1) (which caps beneficial owners at 100), Section 3(c)(7) has no ownership limit but restricts ownership to qualified purchasers. Popular for private equity funds, hedge funds, and other pooled investment vehicles targeting ultra-high-net-worth investors.

Source: Investment Company Act of 1940, §3(c)(7) (15 U.S.C. §80a-3(c)(7)); DTC Operational Arrangements. dtcc.com

OFAC Compliance — Sanctions Screening

Treasury Department
OFAC (Office of Foreign Assets Control)

U.S. Treasury agency that administers and enforces economic and trade sanctions based on U.S. foreign policy and national security goals. OFAC maintains the Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) list and other sanctions programs.

OFAC Requirements for DTC Eligibility

Underwriters must confirm that neither the issuer nor its principals (officers, directors, significant shareholders) appear on OFAC sanctions lists. This is a hard stop — DTC will deny eligibility if sanctions concerns exist.

Compliance Steps
OFAC Screening Process

Before submitting the eligibility questionnaire, underwriters should: (1) Screen the issuer's legal name against the SDN list, (2) Screen officers, directors, and 10%+ shareholders, (3) Check for matches against all OFAC sanctions programs (not just SDN list), (4) Document the screening process and results.

Red Flags

  • High-risk jurisdictions: Issuers from countries under comprehensive U.S. sanctions (e.g., Iran, North Korea, Syria, Russia)
  • Name variations: Issuers with names similar to sanctioned entities require additional due diligence
  • Complex ownership: Shell companies or opaque ownership structures may hide sanctioned individuals
  • Political exposure: Foreign government officials or their family members may be subject to sanctions
Source: U.S. Treasury OFAC; DTC Operational Arrangements. ofac.treasury.gov

Rule 3(a)(2) — Exempt Securities

Exemption
Rule 3(a)(2)

An exemption from SEC registration for certain categories of securities, including government obligations, bank securities, and short-term commercial paper.

Exempt categories include:

  • U.S. government or agency securities
  • State or municipal government securities
  • National bank or federal savings association securities
  • Short-term commercial paper (maturity ≤ 9 months)
  • Nonprofit religious, educational, or charitable organization securities

While exempt from SEC registration, these securities may still be DTC-eligible if they meet DTC's operational criteria. Standard eligibility documentation (questionnaire, Letter of Representations) is still required.

Source: Securities Act of 1933, §3(a)(2) (15 U.S.C. §77c(a)(2)); DTC Operational Arrangements. dtcc.com

State of Incorporation or Municipality

Jurisdictional Field
State of Incorporation / Municipality

The jurisdiction in which the issuer is legally organized, or for municipal securities, the issuing governmental body.

This determines applicable law, tax treatment, and whether special legal opinions are required:

  • Corporate: State or country of incorporation (e.g., Delaware, New York)
  • Municipal: Name of issuing municipality or state authority
  • Foreign issuer: Country of organization — triggers additional requirements including legal opinions confirming DTC's rights under foreign law
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.2 - Agent Requirements. dtcc.com

Issue Principal / Offering Amount

Offering Size
Principal / Offering Amount

The total dollar value (par amount) of the securities being offered.

For municipal securities using the Limited Underwriting service, the principal amount cannot exceed $1 million. For all other issue types, there is no principal limit for DTC eligibility.

Certificate sizing rule: If any single maturity exceeds $500 million, the issuer must provide multiple certificates: one for each $500M tranche plus one for the remainder. Each certificate is registered in the name of Cede & Co.

Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Sections I.A.3 and I.A.5; I.B.1.d.i. dtcc.com

Book-Entry-Only (BEO) Securities

Settlement Method
Book-Entry-Only (BEO)

Securities for which physical certificates are not available to investors. DTC, through its nominee Cede & Co., holds the entire balance either at DTC or through a FAST Agent.

For BEO securities, investors never receive physical certificates. Ownership is recorded only as a book-entry on the records of DTC Participants (broker-dealers, banks) who maintain accounts for beneficial owners.

If answer is "Yes": A Letter of Representations (LOR) is required — either a Blanket Issuer LOR (BLOR) covering all securities of the issuer, or an Issuer LOR (ILOR) for the specific issue. Must be on DTC's preprinted form and delivered no later than one business day prior to closing.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.B.1.a; DTC Underwriting FAQ. dtcclearning.com

Letter of Representations (LOR)

Legal Agreement
Letter of Representations (LOR)

A legal agreement between the issuer, its agent, and DTC that sets forth the operational terms under which DTC will provide depository and book-entry services for the securities.

There are two types:

  • Blanket Issuer LOR (BLOR): Covers all DTC-eligible securities of a particular issuer. Once on file, it does not expire and no new BLOR is needed for future issuances unless the issuer's name changes.
  • Issuer LOR (ILOR): Applicable only to a single, discrete issuance, such as trust issuances or special-purpose vehicles.

Required riders to the LOR are needed for: Rule 144A/Reg S issues, non-US currency securities, and UK/Ireland issuers. The LOR must be delivered to DTC no later than one business day prior to closing.

Electronic signatures: Permitted for U.S. entities in many circumstances per the June 2026 OA. Original manual signatures are still required for non-U.S. entities and for legal opinions.

Non-U.S. Currency Rider: If the security is denominated in or pays interest in a currency other than U.S. dollars, a separate currency rider is required in addition to any jurisdiction-specific riders. This addresses FX conversion mechanics and payment timing.

Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.2; SEC Form of Letter of Representations. sec.gov (ILOR) | sec.gov (BLOR)

Authorized Signature

Execution Requirement
Authorized Signature

The signature of a person with actual authority to bind the submitting entity (Underwriter, Financial Advisor, or Clearing DTC Participant) to the representations made on the questionnaire.

Electronic vs. Manual Signatures

  • Electronic signatures: Permitted in many circumstances for U.S. entities per the June 2026 Operational Arrangements.
  • Original manual signatures: Still required for non-U.S. entities and for any accompanying legal opinions.
  • Authority: The signer should hold a title or role (officer, partner, authorized agent) that reasonably establishes signing authority for the submitting entity.
Practice tip: Confirm signing authority in advance for non-U.S. submitters — sourcing an original wet-ink signature under time pressure close to closing is a common cause of delay.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.A.6.

PORTAL

Legacy Trading System
PORTAL

The NASD's Portal Alliance system, a trading platform historically used for non-investment-grade securities sold under Rule 144A that required Qualified Institutional Buyer (QIB) status to trade.

Current Status

  • Historical requirement: The original questionnaire required PORTAL eligibility for non-investment-grade Rule 144A resales.
  • Succeeded by private venues: PORTAL has been succeeded by private trading platforms for Rule 144A securities; issuers should confirm current venue requirements rather than applying to PORTAL directly.
  • Practical effect: A distinct CUSIP and LOR rider are still required for these issues even though the PORTAL application step itself is no longer the operative path.
Source: Historical NASD PORTAL rules; current market practice for Rule 144A resale platforms.

Then vs. Now: Footnote 2 (PORTAL)

Legacy Field — Changed Since 2000
PORTAL / NASD Footnote

The original 2000 form's footnote 2 read: "For non-investment grade issues eligible for resale under Rule 144A, PORTAL eligibility is required. Contact the NASD at (202) 728-8479 to apply for PORTAL."

What Changed

  • 2000: Non-investment-grade Rule 144A issues had to separately apply for PORTAL eligibility through the NASD by phone.
  • Today: PORTAL no longer exists as an active system. The NASD itself was absorbed into FINRA in 2007. Private trading platforms now serve this role.
  • What survived: The underlying substantive requirement — a distinct CUSIP and an LOR rider for these issues — is unchanged; only the specific venue and phone number are obsolete.
Practice tip: If you see PORTAL referenced in an older internal checklist or precedent document, treat it as a signal the checklist predates 2007 and should be refreshed.

Then vs. Now: Delivery Deadline

Updated Field — New Since 2000
12:00 Noon ET Cutoff

The 2000 form simply said to deliver securities to DTC "at least one business day before closing," with no time-of-day cutoff stated.

What Changed

  • 2000: No specific intraday cutoff was printed on the form itself (though DTC operationally had cutoff times internally).
  • Today: The June 2026 Operational Arrangements make the 12:00 noon ET cutoff explicit on the day-before-closing delivery requirement, for both physical certificates and FAST confirmations.

Tender Letter of Representations

Specialized Addendum
Tender Letter of Representations

A specialized addendum to the standard LOR required for certificated issues that contain put or tender features. Establishes the operational framework for processing tenders through DTC's book-entry system.

Membership Category

A broker-dealer, bank, or other financial institution that has been approved for membership in DTC and maintains an account with DTC. Participants are the direct holders of securities in DTC's book-entry system.

FAST — Fast Automated Securities Transfer

DTC Program
FAST (Fast Automated Securities Transfer)

DTC's program that allows securities to be transferred electronically between DTC and its FAST Agents without the physical movement of certificates.

Under FAST, physical certificates are immobilized at the FAST Agent's location or at DTC, and only book-entry movements occur. The FAST Agent must have on file with DTC a completed Operational Arrangements Agent Letter, a Balance Certificate Agreement, and an Operational Criteria for FAST Transfer Agent Processing agreement.

FAST dramatically reduces settlement risk, processing time, and operational costs. Most modern DTC-eligible issues are FAST-eligible. If securities are not FAST-eligible, physical certificates must be delivered to DTC at least one business day before closing, by 12:00 noon ET.

FAST Program Requirements

To participate in FAST, transfer agents must:

  • Execute an Operational Arrangements Agent Letter with DTC
  • Execute a Balance Certificate Agreement for FAST processing
  • Meet operational criteria for FAST Transfer Agent Processing
  • Maintain a single balance certificate registered to Cede & Co.
  • Process electronic deposits and withdrawals per DTC specifications

FRAC (Fully Restricted Automated Certificate)

FRAC is similar to FAST but for restricted securities. The transfer agent immobilizes restricted certificates at its location and processes book-entry movements for restricted transactions.

  • Used for securities with resale restrictions (Rule 144A, Reg S)
  • Requires additional compliance procedures
  • Transfer agent must verify restricted status before processing

DWAC (Deposit/Withdrawal at Custodian)

DWAC allows DTC participants to deposit and withdraw securities directly with a custodian rather than through physical movement.

  • Enables same-day settlement for eligible securities
  • Requires custodian to be a DTC participant
  • Used primarily for book-entry-only (BEO) securities
  • Reduces physical certificate handling requirements
Operational Requirement: Transfer agents must meet DTC's operational criteria and maintain required agreements to participate in FAST/FRAC/DWAC programs. Non-participation requires physical certificate delivery by 12:00 noon ET one business day before closing. See Operational Arrangements Section II.B.2

Cede & Co. — DTC's Nominee

Nominee Name
Cede & Co.

The registered nominee of The Depository Trust Company. Cede & Co. appears as the registered owner of record on the issuer's books for all securities held in DTC's depository.

Why Cede & Co. Exists

The indirect holding system — where intermediaries hold securities on behalf of beneficial owners — requires a single registered owner on the issuer's transfer records. Rather than registering millions of individual beneficial owners (which would be administratively impossible), DTC uses its nominee, Cede & Co., as the sole registered owner.

This structure was legally validated by the 1994 revisions to Article 8 of the NY UCC, which explicitly recognized the indirect holding system and the rights of beneficial owners through their intermediaries.

How the Chain Works

  1. Issuer's books: Show "Cede & Co." as the registered owner of 100% of the issue.
  2. DTC's records: Show which DTC Participants hold what positions.
  3. Participant's records: Show which beneficial owners (customers) hold what positions.
  4. Beneficial owner: Holds the economic interest but is not on the issuer's register.

Legal Rights

  • Cede & Co. holds legal title but has no beneficial interest. It cannot vote, receive payments for its own benefit, or exercise put/tender rights except as directed by DTC Participants.
  • DTC Participants (broker-dealers, banks) are the "direct participants" in DTC's system. They hold securities in their DTC account and allocate positions to their customers.
  • Beneficial owners (investors) hold their interests through their broker or bank. They receive proxy materials, income payments, and corporate action notices through their intermediary.

Practical Implications

  • Dividends & interest: The Paying Agent remits to DTC in the name of Cede & Co. DTC then allocates to Participants, who allocate to beneficial owners.
  • Proxy voting: Issuers send proxy materials to Cede & Co. DTC forwards to Participants, who forward to beneficial owners. Votes flow back up the chain.
  • Corporate actions: Tender offers, exchanges, and redemptions are processed through DTC's systems (ATOP, ASOP, etc.) with Cede & Co. as the formal counterparty.
  • Legal proceedings: In class actions or bankruptcies, notice to Cede & Co. constitutes notice to all beneficial holders under NY UCC §8-503.
Key point for issuers: When you sign a Letter of Representations, you agree to recognize Cede & Co. as the sole registered owner and to make all payments and send all notices through DTC. You do not deal directly with beneficial owners for book-entry securities.
Source: NY UCC Article 8 (1994 Revised), §8-503; DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.B.1.d; SEC Release No. 34-38067. cornell.edu (UCC Article 8) | dtcc.com

Closing Date — Form Field

Form Field
Closing Date

The scheduled date on which the issuer will deliver the securities to the underwriter and receive the offering proceeds. This is the anchor date from which all DTC deadlines are calculated.

How to Determine the Closing Date

  • Firm commitment underwriting: Closing is typically 3–5 business days after pricing, allowing time for document finalization and settlement.
  • Best efforts / continuous offerings: Closing may be a rolling date or the final date of the offering period.
  • Municipal bond closings: Often scheduled to align with project funding needs and may involve multiple closings ("draw-down" structures).
  • Rule 144A / Reg S: Closing may occur simultaneously with or shortly after the initial sale to QIBs or offshore purchasers.

What Happens on Closing Date

  1. Issuer delivers executed securities (or master certificate for BEO) to DTC or the designated DTC Participant.
  2. Underwriter wires offering proceeds to the issuer.
  3. DTC credits the securities to the Participant's account specified on the questionnaire.
  4. Securities become available for book-entry trading and settlement among DTC Participants.
Critical: If the closing date changes (a "delayed closing"), notify DTC's Underwriting Department immediately. DTC may need to adjust its processing schedule, and the Letter of Representations may need to be re-dated.

CUSIP Number — Column Explained

Column Field
CUSIP Number

The unique 9-character identifier for this specific security issue. Every distinct tranche, maturity, or series of an offering receives its own CUSIP.

Why This Matters for DTC

  • DTC uses CUSIP numbers as the primary key for all book-entry records. Without a valid CUSIP, DTC cannot create the security in its system.

Type of Issue — Column Explained

Column Field
Type of Issue

A single-letter code indicating the structural feature of the security. This helps DTC's systems apply the correct processing rules.

Valid Codes

  • C = Capital Appreciation — The security is issued at a discount and accretes to par at maturity. No periodic interest payments; the return is the difference between purchase price and redemption value. Common in municipal zero-coupon bonds.
  • P = Put — The security contains a put feature allowing holders to demand repayment before maturity on specified dates. Requires a Tender Letter of Representations if certificated.
  • S = Serial — The issue contains multiple maturity dates (serial maturities), with principal amortizing over time. Each serial maturity typically receives its own CUSIP and row in this table.
  • T = Term — The entire issue matures on a single date. The simplest structure; principal is repaid in full at maturity.

Combinations

Some securities may combine features (e.g., a serial bond with a put option). In such cases, list all applicable codes or describe the structure in the Issuer Name / Issue Description field. When in doubt, contact DTC's Underwriting Department before submission.

DTC Processing Impact

The type code determines which DTC processing modules are activated:

  • C (Capital Appreciation): DTC suppresses income payment processing until maturity.
  • P (Put): DTC enables tender/put processing workflows and requires coordination with the Tender Agent.
  • S (Serial): DTC sets up multiple maturity events and sinking fund schedules.
  • T (Term): Standard single-maturity processing.
Tip: If your issue does not fit neatly into one category (e.g., a VRDO with a mandatory tender), describe the structure in detail in your offering documents and contact DTC Underwriting for guidance on the correct code.
Source: DTC Eligibility Questionnaire; DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.C.2 — Unit Securities; Section VII — VRDOs. dtcc.com

Initial Offering Price (To the Public) — Column Explained

Column Field
Initial Offering Price (To the Public)

The price at which the security is first sold to investors, expressed as a percentage of par value (e.g., "99.50%" or "100.00%"). Also called the "public offering price" or "reoffering price."

What to Enter

  • Par pricing: "100.00%" or "$100.00" — the security is sold at face value.
  • Discount pricing: "99.25%" or "$99.25" — the security is sold below par; investors receive the difference as additional yield.
  • Premium pricing: "101.50%" or "$101.50" — the security is sold above par; typical for high-coupon issues in a low-rate environment.
  • Capital appreciation bonds: Enter the initial accreted value (e.g., "$45.00" for a bond that accretes to $100.00 at maturity).
  • Unit securities: Enter the combined offering price of the unit (debt + equity/warrant).

DTC Processing

DTC uses the initial offering price to establish a baseline market value for the security in its systems. This value is posted to Participants' accounts and serves as the starting point for:

  • Position valuation: Participants use this to report holdings values to beneficial owners.
  • Margin calculations: Clearing firms use DTC's posted values for margin and collateral purposes.
  • Corporate action processing: Exchange ratios and proration calculations may reference the offering price.

If no offering price is provided, DTC will post a price of $0.00 until the underwriter provides an initial indicative bid price.

Underwriter Obligation

Per DTC's Operational Arrangements, underwriters should provide DTC with an initial indicative bid price in U.S. dollars prior to the Closing Date. This is separate from the offering price but serves a similar function for secondary market tracking.

Formatting: Use percentage of par (preferred) or dollar price per $100 par. Be consistent across all CUSIPs in the same offering. Include two decimal places (e.g., "99.50" not "99.5").
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.A.5 — Market Values; DTC Fee Schedule. dtcc.com

Qualified Institutional Buyer (QIB)

Institutional Investor Category
QIB

An institution that owns and invests on a discretionary basis at least $100 million in securities of unaffiliated issuers. QIBs include banks, insurance companies, investment companies, and certain registered investment advisers.

Rule 144A Requirements

  • Safe harbor: Rule 144A permits resale of restricted securities to QIBs without SEC registration.
  • $100 million threshold: Must own and invest at least $100 million in non-affiliated securities (excludes affiliates' holdings).
  • Qualified purchasers: Similar concept under the Investment Company Act — entities with $5+ million in investments.

Types of QIBs

  • Banks: With at least $100 million in securities and audited financial statements.
  • Insurance companies: Meeting the $100 million threshold and state regulatory requirements.
  • Investment companies: Registered under the Investment Company Act of 1940.
  • Investment advisers: Registered with the SEC, managing at least $100 million for QIB clients.
  • Employee benefit plans: With at least $100 million in assets.
DTC Impact: For Rule 144A issues, QIB trading occurs on private trading venues (successors to PORTAL). DTC requires a distinct CUSIP and LOR rider for 144A-eligible securities, but no separate PORTAL eligibility verification.
Source: SEC Rule 144A (17 CFR 240.144A); Securities Act of 1933. sec.gov

Blanket Letter of Representations (BLOR)

DTC Agreement
BLOR

A Blanket Letter of Representations that covers all DTC-eligible securities of a particular issuer. Once executed and filed with DTC, it does not expire and applies to all future issuances by that issuer.

How BLOR Differs from ILOR

  • BLOR: Covers all securities of the issuer. Ongoing coverage. Does not expire unless issuer name changes.
  • ILOR: Issuer Letter of Representations for a specific issuance only. Single-issue coverage. May be used when BLOR unavailable.

BLOR Advantages

  • Efficiency: No need to execute new LOR for each issuance.
  • Speed: Reduces closing timeline — BLOR already on file.
  • Cost: Avoids repeated legal counsel fees for LOR execution.

When BLOR Doesn't Apply

  • Issuer name change requires new BLOR.
  • Change in corporate structure may require update.
  • DTC may request new BLOR if material circumstances change.
  • Some specialized securities (MMI, E-CD) use dedicated forms.
Verification: Before closing, verify with DTC whether the issuer has a BLOR on file. If not, an ILOR will be required. Check that the issuer name matches exactly — even minor discrepancies (e.g., "Inc." vs "Inc") can invalidate BLOR coverage.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.2; SEC Form of Letter of Representations. sec.gov (BLOR form)

Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT)

Corporate Structure
REIT

A company that owns, operates, or finances income-generating real estate. REITs are structured to avoid corporate income tax if they distribute at least 90% of taxable income to shareholders.

Ownership Restrictions

To maintain tax-advantaged status, REITs must comply with ownership concentration limits under the Internal Revenue Code:

  • 5-50 rule: No five shareholders can own more than 50% of the REIT's shares.
  • Diversity requirement: Must have at least 100 shareholders.
  • Asset tests: At least 75% of assets must be in real estate, cash, or government securities.
  • Income tests: At least 75% of income must come from real estate sources.

DTC Eligibility Impact

Because REITs have ownership restrictions to preserve tax status, DTC requires an indemnity letter from the issuer. This letter protects DTC and its Participants in case the REIT's ownership restrictions are violated through DTC's book-entry system.

Seg 100 Option: REITs may use DTC's Segregation Account 100 (Seg 100) tool to track ownership for compliance. However, DTC does not monitor compliance — Seg 100 is a tracking tool only. The indemnity letter remains required.
Source: Internal Revenue Code §856; DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.B.4. irs.gov | dtcc.com

Net Operating Loss (NOL) Carryforwards

Tax Attribute
NOL Carryforwards

Tax losses that a company can carry forward to offset future taxable income. NOLs are valuable tax assets that can be jeopardized if ownership changes trigger limitations under Section 382 of the Internal Revenue Code.

Section 382 Limitations

  • Ownership change: If one or more 5% shareholders increase their ownership by more than 50 percentage points over a 3-year period, an ownership change occurs.
  • NOL limitation: After an ownership change, annual NOL usage is limited to a formula based on the company's value.
  • Preservation concern: Companies with large NOLs may restrict ownership changes to preserve tax benefits.

DTC Eligibility Impact

Issuers with NOL preservation provisions often have ownership restrictions similar to REITs. DTC requires an indemnity letter because:

  • DTC's book-entry system could facilitate prohibited ownership concentrations.
  • The indemnity letter protects DTC if NOL limitations are inadvertently violated.
  • Ownership restrictions are not always visible in standard questionnaire responses.
Identification: When reviewing issuer materials, check for references to "Section 382," "ownership limitations," or "change of control provisions." These indicators suggest NOL preservation concerns and the need for an indemnity letter.
Source: Internal Revenue Code §382; DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.B.4. irs.gov

Indemnity Letter

Legal Protection Document
Indemnity Letter

A letter in which the issuer agrees to indemnify (compensate) DTC and its Participants for losses arising from the issuer's ownership restrictions or other special requirements. The letter protects DTC from liability when processing restricted securities.

When Required

DTC requires indemnity letters for issuers with ownership restrictions or special legal considerations:

  • REITs: Preserving tax-advantaged status requires ownership limits.
  • NOL issuers: Companies with net operating loss carryforwards subject to Section 382 limitations.
  • Maritime issuers: Subject to Jones Act ownership restrictions (U.S. citizenship requirements).
  • Broadcast licensees: FCC ownership restrictions apply.
  • Foreign issuers: May require indemnity for foreign legal opinion enforceability.

Content of Indemnity Letter

  • Indemnification clause: Issuer agrees to indemnify DTC and Participants for losses.
  • Ownership restriction acknowledgment: Issuer confirms restrictions exist.
  • DTC protection: DTC is held harmless for processing through book-entry system.
  • Counsel involvement: Typically drafted by issuer's counsel.
Not on Standard Questionnaire: The indemnity letter requirement is not explicitly listed on the DTC eligibility questionnaire. DTC may request it based on issuer type, industry, or review of offering documents. Always check with DTC if the issuer has ownership restrictions.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.B.4; DTC Underwriting FAQ. dtcc.com

Commercial Paper

Short-Term Debt Instrument
Commercial Paper

Unsecured, short-term debt instrument issued by corporations to meet short-term liabilities. Maturities typically range from 1 to 270 days. Commercial paper is usually issued at a discount from face value.

Characteristics

  • Unsecured: Not backed by collateral — relies on issuer's creditworthiness.
  • Short maturity: Usually 270 days or less (to avoid SEC registration).
  • Discount pricing: Sold at discount, redeemed at face value.
  • Low cost: Interest rates typically lower than bank loans.

DTC Processing

Commercial paper is processed under DTC's Money Market Instruments (MMI) program with special requirements:

  • MMI forms: Specialized form letters instead of standard LORs.
  • Dedicated help line: (866) 724-4402 for MMI-specific questions.
  • Separate rules: Processed under dedicated DTC Rules rather than Operational Arrangements.
  • Banker's acceptances: Also processed under MMI program.
Registration exemption: Commercial paper with maturity ≤ 270 days is exempt from SEC registration under Rule 3(a)(2) if it meets certain requirements. This exemption is why commercial paper programs can operate efficiently.
Source: SEC Rule 3(a)(2); DTC MMI Guidelines. sec.gov | dtcc.com (MMI)

Banker's Acceptance

Short-Term Credit Instrument
Banker's Acceptance

A time draft drawn on and accepted by a bank, representing the bank's obligation to pay the stated amount at maturity. Used primarily in international trade to finance transactions.

How It Works

  • Import transaction: Buyer's bank accepts a time draft from the exporter.
  • Bank guarantee: The bank's acceptance makes it a bank obligation, not just a corporate obligation.
  • Discounting: The exporter can sell the acceptance in the secondary market at a discount.
  • Maturity: Typically 30 to 180 days.

DTC Processing

Like commercial paper, banker's acceptances are processed under DTC's Money Market Instruments (MMI) program:

  • MMI forms: Specialized documentation requirements.
  • Bank involvement: The accepting bank must be a DTC Participant or have arrangements with one.
  • Trade finance focus: Common in letters of credit and trade transactions.
Credit quality: Banker's acceptances are considered high-quality short-term instruments because they carry the bank's credit rather than just the corporate credit. This makes them attractive to money market funds.
Source: DTC MMI Guidelines; Uniform Commercial Code Article 3. dtcc.com (MMI)

FAST Agent

DTC Program Participant
FAST Agent

A transfer agent that has executed the necessary agreements with DTC to participate in the FAST (Fast Automated Securities Transfer) program, allowing electronic immobilization and transfer of securities without physical certificate movement.

FAST Program Requirements

To act as a FAST Agent, the transfer agent must have on file with DTC:

  • Operational Arrangements Agent Letter: Basic agreement to process DTC-eligible securities.
  • Balance Certificate Agreement: Agreement regarding certificate balances and reconciliation.
  • Operational Criteria for FAST Transfer Agent Processing: Technical and operational requirements.

How FAST Works

  • Immobilization: Physical certificates are held at the FAST Agent or DTC.
  • Book-entry transfer: Securities move electronically between DTC and FAST Agent.
  • No physical movement: Eliminates risk and delay of certificate shipping.
  • BEO eligibility: Book-Entry-Only securities typically use FAST Agents for certificate immobilization.
Coordination: The Transfer Agent listed on the eligibility questionnaire must be a FAST Agent (or have executed the necessary agreements) for the issue to be eligible for FAST processing. If not, DTC may require physical certificate delivery.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.B; DTC FAST Program Guidelines. dtcc.com

Serial Maturities

Bond Structure
Serial Maturities

A bond structure where principal is repaid in multiple installments over time, with different maturities for each installment. Each serial maturity typically has its own CUSIP and may have different interest rates.

Serial vs. Term Bonds

  • Serial bonds: Multiple maturity dates (e.g., 2026, 2027, 2028...2035). Principal amortizes over time.
  • Term bonds: Single maturity date. Entire principal repaid at once (bullet maturity).

DTC CUSIP Requirements

Each serial maturity receives its own CUSIP number and row in the CUSIP Information table:

  • Type code "S": Used for serial maturities in the CUSIP table.
  • Individual interest rates: Each maturity may have its own interest rate.
  • Separate principal amounts: Each maturity specifies the principal repayment amount.
  • Example: A 2026–2035 serial issue requires 10 CUSIPs and 10 table rows.
Municipal bonds: Serial structures are common for municipal bonds to match revenue streams with debt service. Corporate bonds typically use term structures, but serial bonds are used for project financing where cash flows match amortization schedules.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.2; CUSIP Services Manual. cusip.com

Term Bond

Bond Structure
Term Bond

A bond with a single maturity date when the entire principal amount is repaid. Unlike serial bonds, which amortize over time, term bonds are bullet maturities with one principal repayment date.

Characteristics

  • Single maturity: Entire principal due at one specified date.
  • Type code "T": Used in DTC's CUSIP table for term bonds.
  • One CUSIP: Term bonds typically require only one CUSIP (unless multiple tranches).
  • Corporate bonds: Most corporate bonds are term structures.

Advantages and Disadvantages

  • Issuer advantage: No principal payments until final maturity — full use of proceeds.
  • Investor advantage: Known repayment date and full principal at maturity.
  • Refinancing risk: Large balloon payment at maturity creates refinancing risk.
  • Serial comparison: Serial bonds reduce refinancing risk but require ongoing principal payments.
DTC efficiency: Term bonds are simpler for DTC processing — one CUSIP, one row in the CUSIP table, one set of certificates. Serial bonds multiply administrative workload because each maturity is treated as a separate security.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.2; Municipal Securities Rulemaking Board. msrb.org

Beneficial Owner

Ownership Concept
Beneficial Owner

The person or entity that enjoys the benefits of ownership (economic interest, voting rights, dividend entitlement) even though the security may be registered in the name of another (such as Cede & Co. or a broker).

Indirect Holding System

Under the modern securities system (post-1994 UCC Article 8 revisions):

  • Registered owner: Cede & Co. appears on issuer's books as owner of record.
  • Intermediaries: DTC Participants (brokers, banks) hold positions with DTC.
  • Beneficial owners: Investors hold economic interests through their intermediaries.
  • Chain of title: Issuer → Cede & Co. → DTC → Participants → Beneficial owners.

Rights of Beneficial Owners

  • Economic benefits: Entitled to dividends, interest, and principal payments.
  • Voting rights: Can vote through their intermediary (proxy voting chain).
  • Corporate actions: Can participate in tender offers, exchanges, and rights offerings.
  • Sale proceeds: Can sell their position through their broker.
Legal protection: The 1994 revisions to NY UCC Article 8 specifically validated the rights of beneficial owners through intermediaries. Notice to Cede & Co. constitutes notice to all beneficial owners under §8-503.
Source: NY UCC Article 8 (1994 Revised); SEC Release No. 34-38067. cornell.edu

Nominee

Legal Concept
Nominee

A person or entity that holds legal title to securities on behalf of another, who is the beneficial owner. The nominee has no beneficial interest and holds the securities only as a matter of convenience or legal structure.

Cede & Co. as Nominee

DTC's nominee, Cede & Co., is the classic example:

  • Legal title: Cede & Co. is registered owner on issuer's books.
  • No beneficial interest: Cede & Co. cannot benefit from ownership.
  • Administrative convenience: Eliminates need to register millions of individual owners.
  • Indirect holding system: Made possible by 1994 UCC Article 8 revisions.

Other Nominee Arrangements

  • Street name registration: Brokers hold securities in "street name" (nominee) for customers.
  • Trust nominees: Trust companies may hold assets as nominees for trusts.
  • Custody nominees: Custodians may hold securities as nominees for asset managers.

Foreign Issuers

For non-U.S. issuers, DTC may require a foreign legal opinion confirming that:

  • DTC's rights as nominee are enforceable under local law.
  • The indirect holding system is recognized.
  • Beneficial owners' rights through intermediaries are protected.
Legal distinction: The nominee holds legal title but not equitable title. Beneficial owners hold equitable title (economic interest). This separation is fundamental to modern securities holding systems.
Source: NY UCC Article 8 (1994 Revised); Uniform Commercial Code. cornell.edu
