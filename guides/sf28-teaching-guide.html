Affidavit of Individual Surety (SF 28)

Prescribed by GSA-FAR (48 CFR) 53.228(e) · OMB Control No. 9000-0001 · Expires 1/31/2027

⏱ Critical Requirements at a Glance

  • Must be a natural person: No corporation, partnership, or unincorporated association can serve as an individual surety.
  • U.S. citizenship required: For contracts awarded in the United States. For outlying areas/foreign countries, permanent residency suffices.
  • Original signatures only: Reproduced or electronic signatures are not acceptable. The affidavit must be notarized.
  • Net worth ≥ penal amount: The surety must demonstrate net worth not less than the penal amount of the bond.
  • No financial interest in principal: The surety cannot have any financial interest in assets connected with the principal on the bond.
  • Documentation of pledged assets must be attached: Including CUSIP numbers and par amounts for U.S. government securities and other Treasury-eligible obligations per 31 CFR part 225.

Red Flags: Individual Surety Compliance Risks

Reproduced Signatures = Rejection

All signatures on the affidavit must be originals. Affidavits bearing reproduced, stamped, or electronic signatures are not acceptable. DocuSign, Adobe Sign, or photocopied signatures will result in immediate rejection by the contracting officer.

Corporate or Partnership as Surety

No corporation, partnership, or unincorporated association is acceptable as an individual surety. The surety must be a natural person. Stockholders of corporate principals are only acceptable if their qualifications are independent of their stockholdings.

Financial Interest in Principal

An individual surety cannot include any financial interest in assets connected with the principal on the bond. Stockholders of corporate principals are only acceptable if their qualifications are independent of their stockholdings.

Net Worth Below Penal Amount

The individual surety must show a net worth not less than the penal amount of the bond. Pledging assets worth less than the bond amount, or failing to account for existing liens, renders the affidavit non-compliant.

Unattached Asset Documentation

The form explicitly states: "Documentation Of The Pledged Asset Must Be Attached." Missing certified evidence of title, recorded liens, tax assessments, or appraisals will delay or prevent acceptance.

Undisclosed Prior Pledges (3-Year Lookback)

Section 9 requires disclosure of all bonds for which the subject assets have been pledged within three years. Failing to disclose a prior pledge can constitute a false statement under 18 U.S.C. § 1001 — a federal felony.

Missing Notarization

The affidavit must be notarized with an original notary seal. A notary commission must be current (check expiration date in field 12e). Self-notarization or notarization by an interested party is void.

Citizenship Requirement for U.S. Contracts

For contracts awarded in the United States, the surety must be a U.S. citizen. Permanent residency only suffices when the contracting officer is located in an outlying area or foreign country.

Scenario-Based Learning

Work through realistic surety situations to see how SF 28 applies in practice. Click a tab to switch scenarios.

Note: Following a February 2021 FAR rule change, real estate is no longer acceptable collateral for individual sureties. Current rules require Treasury-eligible securities (31 CFR part 225). The real estate scenarios below reflect historical practice.

Real Estate $500K Bond Single Surety

Scenario A: John Doe — Performance Bond on Construction Contract

The Situation: John Doe owns a $1.2 million home free and clear in Fairfax County, Virginia. He is asked to serve as individual surety on a $500,000 performance bond for a friend's construction company.

SF 28 Walkthrough:

  • Field 1 (Name): John Michael Doe — must match the name on the property deed exactly.
  • Field 2A (Home Address): 1234 Maple Drive, Fairfax, VA 22030 — the address of the pledged property.
  • Field 3 (Occupation): Retired Engineer, 35 years — demonstrates stability and income history.
  • Field 4A (Employer): Self-employed consultant — since retired, he states "Retired; former employer: ABC Engineering, Inc."
  • Field 7 (Assets): Real estate: Single-family residence at 1234 Maple Drive, Fairfax, VA. Legal description: Lot 14, Block 3, Fairfax County Plat Book 445, Page 22. Market value: $1,200,000 (per attached certified appraisal dated within 6 months). Current tax assessment: $1,100,000.
  • Field 8 (Encumbrances): "None. Property is owned free and clear. No mortgages, liens, judgments, or tax delinquencies." Attach title insurance policy and current tax receipt.
  • Field 9 (Prior Pledges): "None. This property has never been pledged as surety on any prior bond."
  • Field 10 (Signature): Original ink signature required. John signs in blue or black ink.
  • Field 12 (Notary): A Virginia notary public administers the oath, signs, affixes seal, and notes commission expiration date.
Key Takeaway: Real estate is the most common pledged asset for individual sureties. The legal description must be precise, the appraisal must be current (within 6 months), and the title must be clean. Net worth ($1.2M) exceeds penal amount ($500K) by a comfortable margin.
Securities $250K Bond Broker-Held

Scenario B: Jane Smith — Treasury Portfolio as Surety

The Situation: Jane Smith holds $400,000 in U.S. Treasury securities through her brokerage account at Fidelity. She is asked to serve as surety on a $250,000 payment bond for a software development contract.

SF 28 Walkthrough:

  • Field 1–6: Standard personal information. Jane is a U.S. citizen, software architect at TechCorp for 12 years.
  • Field 5A (Surety Broker): Fidelity Investments, 100 Summer Street, Boston, MA 02110.
  • Field 6A (Financial Institution): Fidelity Investments — same as broker since she holds securities directly.
  • Field 6C (RTN): 021000018 (Fidelity's routing number for wire transfers).
  • Field 7 (Assets):
    • CUSIP: 912828XM6 | Description: U.S. Treasury Note 2.875% due 05/15/2028 | Par: $150,000 | Market Value: $148,500
    • CUSIP: 912828XN4 | Description: U.S. Treasury Note 3.125% due 11/15/2029 | Par: $100,000 | Market Value: $102,000
    • CUSIP: 912828XO2 | Description: U.S. Treasury Bill due 08/15/2025 | Par: $150,000 | Market Value: $149,250
    Total pledged value: $399,750 (exceeds $250K penal amount).
  • Field 8 (Encumbrances): "None. Securities are held in a non-margin, fully paid cash account. No loans, liens, or rehypothecation agreements." Attach Fidelity account statement showing "Cash" account type.
  • Field 9 (Prior Pledges): "None. These Treasury securities have never been pledged as surety on any prior bond within the past three years."
  • Attached Documentation: Fidelity account statement (most recent month), CUSIP verification printout, broker attestation letter confirming sole ownership and no encumbrances.
Key Takeaway: Securities are excellent surety assets because they are liquid and easily valued. U.S. Treasuries are preferred because they carry minimal credit risk. Always verify the account is non-margin (no loans against the securities) and obtain a broker attestation letter.
Co-Surety $1M Bond Two Sureties

Scenario C: Brothers Serving as Co-Sureties

The Situation: Two brothers, Robert and William Chen, each own a $750,000 condominium in Manhattan. Their father's construction company needs a $1,000,000 performance bond. Each brother agrees to pledge his condo as co-surety.

SF 28 Walkthrough:

  • Two separate SF 28 forms are required — one for each co-surety. Each brother completes his own affidavit.
  • Oath Paragraph: Both brothers check the co-surety box. They bind themselves "jointly and severally" — meaning the government can sue either or both for the full $1M.
  • Robert Chen's Field 7: Condo at 456 Park Avenue, Unit 12B, New York, NY 10022. Legal description: Unit 12B, Condominium Plan XYZ, New York County. Market value: $750,000. Tax assessment: $720,000.
  • William Chen's Field 7: Condo at 789 Lexington Avenue, Unit 8C, New York, NY 10022. Legal description: Unit 8C, Condominium Plan ABC, New York County. Market value: $750,000. Tax assessment: $710,000.
  • Field 8 (Encumbrances): Both: "Mortgage with Chase Bank, N.A. in original amount of $300,000, current balance approximately $180,000. Mortgage is current; no delinquencies." Attach mortgage statements showing current balance.
  • Net Worth Calculation: Each condo has $750K value minus $180K mortgage = $570K equity. Combined equity: $1,140,000. This exceeds the $1M penal amount.
  • Field 9 (Prior Pledges): Both: "None within the past three years."
  • Notarization: Each brother must have his own affidavit notarized separately. They cannot share a single notarization.
Key Takeaway: Co-sureties must each file a separate SF 28. The "jointly and severally" language means each surety is individually liable for the full bond amount. Net worth calculations must account for ALL encumbrances, not just the pledged property. A mortgage reduces available equity.
Foreign Contract Permanent Resident Guam

Scenario D: Maria Santos — Contract in Guam

The Situation: Maria Santos is a permanent resident of Guam (a U.S. territory) but not a U.S. citizen. She holds a Green Card. A contracting officer in Guam awards a $300,000 construction contract to a local builder, and Maria is asked to serve as individual surety.

SF 28 Walkthrough:

  • Citizenship Exception: Because the contracting officer is located in an outlying area (Guam), Maria does not need to be a U.S. citizen. Her permanent residency in Guam satisfies the requirement per FAR 28.203 and SF 28 Instructions, Item 3.
  • Field 1 (Name): Maria Elena Santos.
  • Field 2A (Address): 123 Beach Road, Tamuning, GU 96913.
  • Field 3 (Occupation): Small business owner, 15 years — operates a retail store in Guam.
  • Field 7 (Assets): Commercial property at 456 Marine Drive, Tamuning, GU. Legal description per Guam Recorder of Deeds. Market value: $500,000 (certified appraisal). Tax assessment: $480,000.
  • Field 8 (Encumbrances): "First mortgage with Bank of Guam in amount of $200,000, current balance $150,000. Current on all payments." Attach mortgage statement and payment history.
  • Net Worth: $500,000 value - $150,000 mortgage = $350,000 equity. This exceeds the $300,000 penal amount.
  • Field 9 (Prior Pledges): "This property was pledged as surety on a $100,000 bid bond for Guam Public Works Contract #2022-044, executed March 15, 2022. That contract closed successfully on June 30, 2022, and the bond was released."
  • Notarization: Must be notarized by a Guam notary public or other authorized official.
Key Takeaway: The citizenship requirement is relaxed for outlying areas and foreign countries — permanent residency suffices. However, all other requirements (original signatures, notarization, net worth verification, documentation) remain strict. Prior pledges within the 3-year lookback must be fully disclosed even if the bond was successfully released.
Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

❌ Using a Scanned or Electronic Signature

SF 28 Instructions, Item 4 explicitly states: "All signatures of the affidavit submitted must be originals. Affidavits bearing reproduced signatures are not acceptable." DocuSign, Adobe Sign, stamped signatures, and photocopies are all rejected. The surety must physically sign the form in the presence of a notary (or have the notary witness the signature).

❌ Failing to Disclose a Mortgage on Pledged Real Estate

A $600,000 house with a $400,000 mortgage has only $200,000 in equity. If the bond penal amount is $250,000, the surety is under-capitalized. The contracting officer will reject the affidavit. Always calculate net equity (market value minus all liens and encumbrances) and ensure it exceeds the penal amount.

❌ Omitting the 3-Year Prior Pledge History

Section 9 requires disclosure of ALL bonds for which the subject assets were pledged within three years prior. This includes bid bonds, performance bonds, payment bonds, and bid guarantees. Even if the prior bond was released or the contract completed, it must be disclosed. Failure to disclose is a false statement under 18 U.S.C. § 1001.

❌ Using Margin Account Securities

Securities held in a margin account may be subject to the broker's security interest and can be liquidated without the surety's consent. DOD and GSA contracting officers typically reject margin-account securities. Use only fully paid, cash-account securities. Obtain a broker letter confirming the account type.

❌ Expired or Invalid Notary Commission

The notary's commission must be current on the date the oath is administered (Field 12a). If the commission expires the next day, the notarization may still be valid, but if it expired before the oath date, the affidavit is void. Always verify the notary's commission expiration date in Field 12e.

❌ Pledging Assets with a Financial Interest in the Principal

SF 28 Instructions, Item 2: "An individual surety will not include any financial interest in assets connected with the principal on the bond." A stockholder cannot pledge shares of the principal corporation as surety. An employee cannot pledge a company car. The pledged assets must be wholly independent of the principal's business.

Study Guide & Section-by-Section Checklist

Before You Start (Eligibility Verification)

  • Confirm the surety is a natural person — not a corporation, partnership, or LLC
  • Verify U.S. citizenship (or permanent residency for outlying areas/foreign contracts)
  • Confirm surety is of full age and legally competent (18+ and mentally capable)
  • Ensure surety has no financial interest in assets connected with the principal
  • Calculate net worth (total assets minus all liens/encumbrances) ≥ penal amount
  • Identify a qualified notary public with current commission

Personal Information Checklist (Fields 1–6)

  • Field 1: Full legal name matches ID and property/deed records exactly
  • Field 2A: Home address is current and matches tax records
  • Field 3: Occupation and duration demonstrate financial stability
  • Field 4A: Employer name and address are current; if self-employed, state clearly
  • Field 5A: Surety broker information (if used) is complete with contact details
  • Field 6: Financial institution details include RTN and contact person

Asset & Documentation Checklist (Fields 7–9)

  • Field 7: All CUSIP numbers are valid and securities are in a non-margin account
  • Real estate: Legal description, street address, market value, and tax assessment attached
  • Certified appraisal for real estate (dated within 6 months)
  • Field 8: All liens, judgments, mortgages, and tax delinquencies fully disclosed
  • Field 9: Complete 3-year history of all prior bond pledges disclosed
  • All supporting documentation physically attached to the affidavit

Signature & Notary Checklist (Fields 10–12)

  • Field 10: Original ink signature only — no reproductions, scans, or e-signatures
  • Field 11: Bond and contract number clearly identified
  • Field 12a: Date of oath administration is complete (month, day, year)
  • Field 12b: City and state of notarization match notary's jurisdiction
  • Field 12c: Notary name and title are typed or printed legibly
  • Field 12d: Notary's original signature and official seal affixed
  • Field 12e: Notary commission expiration date is current and valid

Standard Form 28 (SF 28)

Federal Form
Standard Form 28 — Affidavit of Individual Surety

The official GSA-prescribed form used by individual sureties to provide sworn financial information and asset pledges in connection with government contract bonds. Current revision: October 2023.

SF 28 is required under FAR 28.203 whenever an individual (as opposed to a corporate surety company) serves as surety on a bid bond, performance bond, or payment bond for a federal government contract. The form serves as both a financial disclosure and a legal affidavit.

The October 2023 revision updated the form to include additional fields for email addresses, surety broker information, and financial institution contact details — reflecting modern communication practices.

Source: GSA Standard Forms; FAR 48 CFR 53.228(e); OMB Control No. 9000-0001. gsa.gov

FAR 53.228(e) — Bond Forms

Federal Regulation
48 CFR 53.228(e)

The Federal Acquisition Regulation provision that prescribes Standard Form 28 as the required affidavit for individual sureties on government contract bonds.

FAR 53.228(e) states that SF 28 "shall be used" for individual sureties. This is not optional — when an individual surety is used on a federal contract, the contracting officer must receive a completed SF 28.

The regulation works in conjunction with FAR 28.203, which establishes the requirements for individual sureties, and FAR 28.106-1, which lists all required bond forms.

Source: Federal Acquisition Regulation, 48 CFR 53.228(e); FAR 28.203. acquisition.gov

State of Execution

Jurisdictional Field
State

The U.S. state (or territory) in which the affidavit is executed and notarized. This determines the applicable notary laws and the jurisdiction for any legal proceedings.

The state entered here should match:

  • The state where the notary public is commissioned
  • The state where the oath is administered
  • The state of the surety's residence (typically, though not strictly required)

For contracts in outlying areas (Guam, Puerto Rico, Virgin Islands, etc.), enter the territory name. For foreign contracts, enter the foreign country.

Source: SF 28 Instructions, Item 3; FAR 28.203. gsa.gov (SF 28)

County of Execution

Jurisdictional Field
County

The county (or parish, borough, or equivalent subdivision) within the state where the affidavit is executed and notarized.

The county should match the notary's jurisdiction. In Louisiana, use "Parish." In Alaska, use "Borough." In independent cities (e.g., Baltimore, St. Louis), the city name may substitute for the county name.

This field is important because real estate records, lien searches, and notary commissions are typically organized by county. The contracting officer may verify the notary's commission in this county.

Source: SF 28 Form; standard notary practices. gsa.gov (SF 28)

Surety — Individual Surety

Legal Role
Individual Surety

A natural person who pledges personal assets to guarantee the performance of a government contract, as distinguished from a corporate surety (an insurance company licensed to issue bonds).

Individual sureties are an alternative to corporate surety companies (like Liberty Mutual, Travelers, or CNA). While corporate sureties are the norm for large contracts, individual sureties may be used for smaller contracts or when a corporate surety is unavailable.

Key restrictions on individual sureties:

  • Must be a natural person — no corporations, LLCs, or partnerships
  • Must be a U.S. citizen (or permanent resident for outlying areas/foreign contracts)
  • Must be of full age and legally competent
  • Net worth must be ≥ penal amount of the bond
  • Cannot have a financial interest in the principal's assets
Practical note: Many contracting officers prefer corporate sureties because they are regulated, rated, and have established claims procedures. Individual sureties require more due diligence and documentation.
Source: FAR 28.203; SF 28 Instructions, Items 1–3. acquisition.gov

Bond — Types of Government Contract Bonds

Financial Guarantee
Bond

A legally binding guarantee that the principal (contractor) will fulfill its obligations under a government contract. If the principal defaults, the surety must complete the contract or pay damages up to the penal amount.

Types of Bonds Requiring SF 28

  • Bid Bond (SF 24): Guarantees that the bidder will enter into the contract if awarded. Typically 5–20% of bid price.
  • Performance Bond (SF 25): Guarantees the contractor will complete the contract according to terms. Typically 100% of contract price.
  • Payment Bond (SF 25A): Guarantees payment to subcontractors and suppliers. Typically 100% of contract price.
  • Bid Guarantee: A narrower form of bid security — may be a bid bond, certified check, or irrevocable letter of credit.

Penal Amount

The "penal amount" is the maximum liability of the surety. For performance bonds, it is typically the full contract price. The individual surety's net worth must equal or exceed this amount.

Source: FAR 28.101–28.106; Miller Act (40 U.S.C. § 3131 et seq.). acquisition.gov

Citizen of the United States

Eligibility Requirement
U.S. Citizenship

A requirement that the individual surety be a citizen of the United States for contracts awarded within the United States. This ensures the surety is subject to U.S. jurisdiction and can be sued in U.S. courts.

SF 28 Instructions, Item 3 establishes the citizenship requirement:

  • Contracts in the United States: Surety must be a U.S. citizen.
  • Contracts in outlying areas (Puerto Rico, Virgin Islands, Guam, etc.): Surety must be a permanent resident of that area.
  • Contracts in foreign countries: Surety must be a permanent resident of that country.

Proof of citizenship may include: U.S. passport, birth certificate, naturalization certificate, or certificate of citizenship. The contracting officer may request documentation.

Important: A Green Card (Lawful Permanent Resident status) does NOT satisfy the citizenship requirement for contracts in the 50 states. It only suffices for outlying areas and foreign contracts.
Source: SF 28 Instructions, Item 3; FAR 28.203. gsa.gov (SF 28)

Full Age and Legally Competent

Capacity Requirement
Full Age and Legally Competent

The surety must be at least 18 years old (the age of majority in most U.S. jurisdictions) and mentally capable of understanding and entering into a binding legal contract.

"Full age" means the surety has reached the age of majority — typically 18, though some states set it at 19 or 21. Minors cannot serve as individual sureties because contracts with minors are generally voidable.

"Legally competent" means the surety has the mental capacity to understand the nature and consequences of the surety obligation. A person under legal guardianship, or someone adjudicated incompetent, cannot serve as surety.

The notary public administering the oath implicitly verifies capacity by witnessing the surety's acknowledgment and signature.

Source: Contract law principles; SF 28 Oath Paragraph. gsa.gov (SF 28)

Co-Sureties

Multiple Sureties
Co-Sureties

Two or more individual sureties who jointly guarantee a single bond. Each co-surety must complete a separate SF 28, and their combined net worth must equal or exceed the penal amount.

When co-sureties act together:

  • Each surety files a separate SF 28 — one form per surety
  • The oath paragraph is modified to reference "co-sureties"
  • Each surety binds themselves "jointly and severally" — the government can sue any one surety for the full amount
  • The combined pledged assets must cover the full penal amount

Example: A $1,000,000 bond with two co-sureties. Surety A pledges assets worth $600,000; Surety B pledges assets worth $500,000. Combined: $1,100,000 (sufficient). The government can sue Surety A alone for $1,000,000 if Surety B becomes insolvent.

Best practice: When using co-sureties, ensure each has independently sufficient assets. Do not rely on one surety to "cover" for another — the government can pursue either for the full amount.
Source: SF 28 Oath Paragraph; FAR 28.203. gsa.gov (SF 28)

Jointly and Severally

Legal Liability
Jointly and Severally

A legal term meaning that multiple obligors (sureties) are each individually liable for the entire debt, and the creditor (the government) can collect the full amount from any one of them. The creditor need not sue all obligors together.

"Jointly" means the sureties are liable together. "Severally" means each is liable individually. In practice:

  • The government can sue all co-sureties together in one lawsuit
  • OR the government can sue any single surety for the full penal amount
  • If one surety pays the full amount, that surety can seek contribution from the other sureties
  • The government is not required to exhaust remedies against the principal before pursuing the surety

SF 28 clarifies that the "jointly and severally" language applies "only for the purpose of allowing a joint action or actions against any or all of us." For all other purposes, each surety binds itself jointly and severally with the Principal.

Source: SF 28 Oath Paragraph; contract law principles. gsa.gov (SF 28)

Severally

Legal Liability
Severally

Individually; separately. When sureties bind themselves "severally," each is responsible for their own share of the obligation, as distinguished from being responsible for the entire obligation.

In the SF 28 oath, the term "severally" is used in contrast to "jointly and severally." The distinction:

  • "Jointly and severally" — for procedural purposes (allowing joint lawsuits), the sureties are treated as jointly liable
  • "Severally" — for substantive liability, each surety is only responsible for their own share, unless the bond terms state otherwise

This nuanced language protects co-sureties from being automatically liable for each other's shares, while still allowing the government to sue them together for efficiency.

Source: SF 28 Oath Paragraph; contract law principles. gsa.gov (SF 28)

Principal

Contract Party
Principal

The contractor or bidder who is the primary obligor on the bond — the party whose performance or payment is being guaranteed by the surety.

In the surety-bond relationship, there are three parties:

  • Principal: The contractor who obtains the bond and is obligated to perform the contract
  • Surety: The individual (or corporate entity) who guarantees the principal's performance
  • Obligee: The government agency that is protected by the bond (the beneficiary)

SF 28 Instructions, Item 2 prohibits the individual surety from having any "financial interest in assets connected with the principal on the bond." This prevents self-dealing and ensures the surety's assets are truly independent collateral.

Source: SF 28 Instructions, Item 2; FAR 28.203. gsa.gov (SF 28)

False, Fictitious, or Fraudulent Statement

Federal Crime
False Statement

A material misrepresentation of fact made knowingly and willfully on a document submitted to a federal agency. Under 18 U.S.C. § 1001, such statements are punishable by up to 5 years in prison and fines.

The SF 28 oath explicitly warns the surety that false statements may subject them to prosecution under:

  • 18 U.S.C. § 1001: False Statements — prohibits knowingly and willfully making false statements in matters within federal jurisdiction. Penalty: up to 5 years imprisonment and/or fines.
  • 18 U.S.C. § 494: Contractors' Bonds, Bids, and Public Records — specifically addresses false statements on bonds and bids for government contracts. Penalty: up to 5 years imprisonment and/or fines.

Common false statements on SF 28 include:

  • Overstating asset values or net worth
  • Failing to disclose existing liens or mortgages
  • Concealing prior bond pledges within the 3-year lookback
  • Claiming U.S. citizenship when not a citizen
  • Pledging assets that are not actually owned
Warning: The false statement statute does not require the government to prove intent to defraud — only that the statement was made knowingly and willfully. Negligent errors (e.g., forgetting a small lien) may still trigger liability if they are material to the government's decision to accept the surety.
Source: 18 U.S.C. § 1001; 18 U.S.C. § 494; SF 28 Oath Paragraph. cornell.edu (18 U.S.C. § 1001)

Title 18, United States Code

Federal Criminal Code
Title 18 U.S.C.

The portion of the United States Code that contains federal criminal statutes. Sections 1001 and 494 are the specific provisions cited in SF 28 that criminalize false statements on surety affidavits and government bonds.

18 U.S.C. § 1001 — False Statements

Prohibits knowingly and willfully making any materially false, fictitious, or fraudulent statement or representation in any matter within the jurisdiction of the executive, legislative, or judicial branch of the U.S. government.

Elements:

  • The defendant made a statement
  • The statement was false
  • The statement was material (capable of influencing the government's decision)
  • The defendant acted knowingly and willfully
  • The statement concerned a matter within federal jurisdiction

18 U.S.C. § 494 — Contractors' Bonds, Bids, and Public Records

Specifically criminalizes false statements on bonds, bids, and public records related to government contracts. This statute is tailored to the procurement context and carries the same penalties as § 1001.

Source: 18 U.S.C. § 1001; 18 U.S.C. § 494. cornell.edu (Title 18)

CUSIP — Committee on Uniform Securities Identification Procedures

Securities Identifier
CUSIP Number

A unique 9-character alphanumeric code used to identify securities (stocks, bonds, notes) in the U.S. and Canada. Administered by CUSIP Global Services (CGS), a division of S&P Global.

On SF 28, the surety must list the CUSIP number for each security being pledged. This allows the contracting officer to:

  • Verify the security exists and is valid
  • Check current market value through financial data providers
  • Confirm the security type (U.S. Treasury, corporate bond, municipal bond, etc.)
  • Ensure the security is not already pledged or encumbered

Format: 9 characters (e.g., 912828XM6 for a U.S. Treasury note). The first 6 characters identify the issuer; characters 7–8 identify the specific issue; the 9th is a check digit.

Where to find CUSIP numbers:

  • Brokerage account statements
  • Trade confirmations
  • SEC EDGAR filings (for corporate bonds)
  • TreasuryDirect.gov (for U.S. Treasuries)
  • CUSIP lookup services (e.g., Fidelity, Bloomberg)
Important: If pledging U.S. Treasury securities held in TreasuryDirect, the CUSIP may not be immediately visible. Contact TreasuryDirect customer service or check the security detail page for the CUSIP.
Source: CUSIP Global Services; SF 28 Field 7. cusip.com

Liens

Property Interest
Lien

A legal claim or encumbrance against property (real or personal) that secures payment of a debt or performance of an obligation. A lien reduces the net equity available to secure the bond.

Common types of liens that must be disclosed on SF 28, Field 8:

  • Mortgages: The most common lien on real estate. The outstanding balance reduces the property's net equity.
  • Home equity lines of credit (HELOCs): Even if unused, the available credit line represents a potential lien.
  • Tax liens: Federal, state, or local tax liens for unpaid income, property, or other taxes.
  • Mechanic's liens: Claims by contractors or suppliers for unpaid work on the property.
  • Judgment liens: Court-ordered liens resulting from lawsuits.
  • Child support liens: Liens imposed for unpaid child support obligations.

To verify liens, the surety should obtain:

  • A title search or title commitment from a title company
  • A UCC search (for personal property liens)
  • Current tax records showing no delinquencies
Critical: Even a small, forgotten lien can render the surety's net worth insufficient. A $600,000 house with a $200,000 mortgage and a $50,000 tax lien has only $350,000 in equity — which may not cover a $400,000 bond.
Source: SF 28 Field 8; property law principles. gsa.gov (SF 28)

Judgments

Court Order
Judgment

A formal decision by a court resolving a dispute and determining the rights and obligations of the parties. A money judgment can become a lien on the judgment debtor's property.

Judgments that must be disclosed on SF 28 include:

  • Money judgments: Court orders requiring payment of a specific sum. These may be recorded as liens on real property.
  • Unsatisfied judgments: Even if not yet recorded as liens, they represent potential claims against the surety's assets.
  • Pending litigation: While not yet a judgment, pending lawsuits that could result in a judgment against the surety should be disclosed as a matter of good faith.

A judgment lien typically attaches to all real property owned by the judgment debtor in the county where the judgment is recorded. The lien remains until the judgment is paid or expires (usually 10–20 years, depending on state law).

To check for judgments:

  • Search county court records in all counties where the surety owns property
  • Search federal court records (PACER)
  • Review credit reports (judgments appear on credit reports for 7 years)
Source: SF 28 Field 8; civil procedure law. gsa.gov (SF 28)

Encumbrances

Property Restriction
Encumbrance

Any claim, lien, charge, or liability attached to real or personal property that reduces its value or burdens it, but does not necessarily prevent transfer of title. A broader term than "lien."

Encumbrances include but are not limited to:

  • Liens: Mortgages, tax liens, judgment liens, mechanic's liens
  • Easements: Rights of others to use the property (e.g., utility easements, access easements)
  • Restrictions: Covenants, conditions, and restrictions (CC&Rs) in subdivisions
  • Encroachments: Structures that extend onto neighboring property
  • Zoning violations: Non-conforming uses that may require remediation
  • Pending condemnation: Government proceedings to take the property
  • Real estate taxes due and payable: Unpaid property taxes are a form of encumbrance

SF 28 Field 8 requires disclosure of "all liens, judgments, or any other encumbrances." The phrase "any other encumbrances" is intentionally broad — when in doubt, disclose.

Best practice: Obtain a preliminary title report from a title company before completing SF 28. This will reveal all recorded encumbrances and provide a clean basis for Field 8 disclosures.
Source: SF 28 Field 8; property law principles. gsa.gov (SF 28)

Bid Guarantees

Security Instrument
Bid Guarantee

A form of security that ensures the bidder will accept the contract if awarded. Common forms include bid bonds (SF 24), certified checks, and irrevocable letters of credit.

SF 28 Field 9 requires disclosure of all bonds, including bid guarantees, for which the subject assets have been pledged within the past three years. This includes:

  • Bid bonds (SF 24)
  • Performance bonds (SF 25)
  • Payment bonds (SF 25A)
  • Annual bid bonds (SF 34)
  • Any other surety obligation to a government agency

Even if the prior bond was released or the contract was completed successfully, it must still be disclosed. The 3-year lookback is absolute.

Why this matters: If the same assets were pledged on a prior bond that is still active, the surety may be over-pledged — meaning the assets cannot cover both obligations simultaneously.

Source: SF 28 Field 9; FAR 28.101. gsa.gov (SF 28)

Stockholders

Corporate Ownership
Stockholders

Owners of shares in a corporation. When a corporate principal is bidding on a government contract, stockholders may not serve as individual sureties unless their personal qualifications are entirely independent of their corporate stock ownership.

The independence requirement: A stockholder's personal assets (home, savings, investments) cannot be used to guarantee a contract for their own corporation. This creates a conflict of interest — the surety would essentially be guaranteeing themselves, which defeats the purpose of having a third-party guarantor.

Acceptable scenario: A stockholder may serve as surety for a different company (unrelated to their stock ownership) if they meet all other SF 28 requirements independently.

Source: FAR 28.101-2(b); SF 28 Instructions. acquisition.gov (FAR 28.101-2)

Penal Amount

Bond Liability
Penal Amount

The maximum dollar amount for which a surety can be held liable under a bond. This is the bond's face value — the amount the government can collect from the surety if the principal defaults.

Net worth requirement: FAR 28.203-1(b) requires that an individual surety's net worth be "not less than the penal amount" of the bond. This means if the bond is $500,000, the surety must show a net worth of at least $500,000 after accounting for all debts and encumbrances.

Calculation example: If you have $1.2 million in assets and $400,000 in liabilities (mortgage, loans, credit cards), your net worth is $800,000. This qualifies you for bonds up to $800,000 in penal amount.

Why this matters: The government must be confident that if it calls on the bond, the surety has sufficient personal resources to pay the full amount. The penal amount is the ceiling of that exposure.

Source: FAR 28.203-1(b); SF 28 Instructions. acquisition.gov (FAR 28.203)

Contracting Officer

Government Official
Contracting Officer

The federal government official responsible for administering government contracts, including evaluating bond acceptability and surety qualifications.

Role in bond acceptance: The contracting officer reviews SF 28 affidavits to ensure they meet FAR requirements, verify that pledged assets are sufficient and properly documented, and determine whether the surety is acceptable for the specific contract.

Authority: Contracting officers have broad discretion to reject inadequate surety arrangements. They may require additional documentation, reject affidavits with discrepancies, or demand corporate surety bonds in place of individual surety arrangements.

Geographic jurisdiction: The contracting officer's location determines certain requirements, such as whether permanent resident (non-citizen) sureties are acceptable. Outlying areas have special citizenship rules.

Source: FAR 1.601; FAR 28.203. acquisition.gov (FAR 1.601)

Outlying Area

Geographic Exception
Outlying Area

A geographic location outside the 50 states and the District of Columbia where the standard U.S. citizenship requirement for individual sureties may be relaxed.

Citizenship exception: Under FAR 28.203, when a contract is to be performed in an outlying area, a permanent resident alien (lawful permanent resident/green card holder) may serve as an individual surety, even though they are not a U.S. citizen.

What qualifies as outlying area: This includes Guam, Puerto Rico, U.S. Virgin Islands, American Samoa, Northern Mariana Islands, and certain U.S. territories and possessions. It does not include foreign countries.

Contracting officer location: The citizenship exception applies when the contracting officer is located in an outlying area, not just where the contract will be performed. The rationale is that it may be difficult to find U.S. citizens willing to serve as sureties in these locations.

Source: FAR 28.203; SF 28 Instructions. acquisition.gov (FAR 28.203)

Performance Bond

Contract Bond Type
Performance Bond

A bond that guarantees the principal (contractor) will complete the contract according to its terms. If the principal defaults or fails to perform, the surety must either complete the contract or pay damages up to the penal amount.

Common in construction: Performance bonds are most frequently required on construction contracts, but they may also be used for service contracts, supply contracts, and other types of federal agreements.

Relationship to payment bonds: On many federal construction contracts (under the Miller Act), a performance bond must be accompanied by a payment bond, which guarantees the principal will pay subcontractors, laborers, and material suppliers.

Penal amount: Performance bonds are typically set at 100% of the contract price. For a $500,000 contract, the performance bond would be $500,000.

Source: FAR 28.102-1; Miller Act (40 U.S.C. §§ 3131-3134). acquisition.gov (FAR 28.102-1)

Construction Company

Contractor Type
Construction Company

A business entity engaged in building, construction, renovation, or repair projects. Construction companies are the most common type of principal on federal performance bonds.

Why construction needs bonds: Construction contracts involve significant upfront costs, long project timelines, and complex dependencies. If a contractor fails to complete a project, the financial impact on the government can be substantial. Performance bonds protect the government from this risk.

Surety challenges for construction: Construction companies often have thin margins and volatile cash flows, making them poor candidates for self-bonding. Individual sureties are often needed when the company lacks established bonding capacity with corporate sureties.

Common bond requirements: Construction contracts typically require both performance bonds (guaranteeing completion) and payment bonds (guaranteeing payment to subcontractors and suppliers).

Source: FAR 28.102-1; Miller Act. acquisition.gov (FAR 28.102-1)

Legal Description

Property Documentation
Legal Description

The formal, technically precise description of real property as recorded in official land records (deeds, plat books, tax maps). This description, not just the street address, is what legally identifies the pledged asset.

Format example: "Lot 14, Block 3, Fairfax County Plat Book 445, Page 22" or "Beginning at a point on the north side of Oak Street, thence running north 150 feet to the north line of said tract, thence east 200 feet..." (metes and bounds).

Why precision matters: Vague descriptions like "the house at 1234 Maple Street" are insufficient for legal purposes. The legal description must exactly match what's recorded in the county land records to prove ownership and enable the government to perfect its lien interest if needed.

Where to find it: On your property deed, current survey, title commitment, or property tax records. The same legal description appears on all these documents.

Source: Local county recorder office; property title documents.

Title Insurance

Insurance Product
Title Insurance Policy

An insurance policy that protects against loss from defects in title to real property, such as undisclosed liens, forged documents, or errors in the public record.

Required for pledged assets: SF 28 requires "Documentation of the pledged asset must be attached" (Section 7). For real estate, a current title insurance policy is the preferred evidence of clear, marketable ownership.

What it shows: The title commitment or policy reveals the current owner, any existing mortgages or liens, easements, restrictions, and other encumbrances that could affect the government's ability to claim the asset if needed.

Owner's policy vs. lender's policy: The surety should provide an owner's policy (protecting the surety's interest) rather than a lender's policy (which only protects a mortgage lender). The government requires evidence that the surety has clear, insurable title.

Source: SF 28 Field 7 instructions; American Land Title Association.

Appraisal

Valuation Document
Certified Appraisal

A professional estimate of the fair market value of an asset, performed by a qualified appraiser and accompanied by their certification statement. For real estate, this establishes the property's current market value for asset qualification purposes.

Recency requirement: SF 28 instructions specify that appraisals should be "current" — typically within 6 months of the affidavit date. Real estate markets change, and stale valuations may not reflect current conditions.

Qualified appraiser: The appraiser should be state-licensed or certified, familiar with the local market where the property is located, and independent of the transaction (no conflict of interest).

Contents required: The appraisal must include the property address, legal description, date of inspection, comparables used in valuation, appraiser's qualifications, and certification signature.

Source: SF 28 Field 7 instructions; Uniform Standards of Professional Appraisal Practice (USPAP).

Notary Commission

Official Authorization
Notary Commission

The official authorization granted by a state government to an individual to perform notarizations — witnessing signatures, administering oaths, and certifying the authenticity of documents.

Expiration date: SF 28 Field 12e requires the notary to record their commission expiration date. Notary commissions typically last 4-10 years depending on the state. An expired commission invalidates the notarization.

Current commission required: The notarization must be performed while the commission is current. A notarization performed after a commission expired cannot be "ratified" or validated retroactively.

Verification: Contracting officers may verify notary commissions through state notary databases, especially for high-value bonds or if fraud is suspected.

Source: State notary statutes; SF 28 Field 12 instructions. nationalnotary.org (Notary Commission)

Notary Seal

Notarization Element
Official Seal

The official stamp or embossing device used by a notary to authenticate their notarization. The seal contains the notary's name, commission number, state, and commission expiration date.

Physical vs. electronic seals: SF 28 requires original ink signatures and original seals. Traditional rubber stamps with ink are acceptable. Embossing seals are also common. Electronic notarization (e-notarization) is generally not acceptable for SF 28.

Placement on form: The seal should be affixed in the designated seal box on SF 28 (Field 12). If the form has no seal box, the seal should be placed near the notary's signature without obscuring any text.

Importance: The seal proves the notarization was performed by a duly commissioned official. A notarization without a seal may be rejected as invalid by the contracting officer.

Source: SF 28 Field 12 instructions; Model Notary Act.

Natural Person

Legal Entity Type
Natural Person

A living human being, as opposed to a legal entity such as a corporation, partnership, or LLC. In the context of SF 28, only natural persons can serve as individual sureties.

Why this matters: FAR 28.101-2 explicitly states that corporations, partnerships, and unincorporated associations are not acceptable as individual sureties. This requirement prevents companies from serving as their own guarantors, which would defeat the purpose of having a third-party surety.

Verification: The surety must provide personal information (name, home address, SSN/Tax ID) that demonstrates they are an individual. Business addresses or corporate information indicate the entity is not a natural person.

Related restriction: Even if a natural person is used, they cannot have a financial interest in the principal (the contractor they are guaranteeing). Stockholders of corporate principals must have qualifications entirely independent of their stock ownership.

Source: FAR 28.101-2(b); SF 28 Instructions. acquisition.gov (FAR 28.101-2)

Permanent Resident

Citizenship Status
Permanent Resident

A non-citizen who has been granted the right to live and work permanently in the United States, typically evidenced by a Green Card (Form I-551). Also known as a lawful permanent resident (LPR).

Citizenship requirement: For federal contracts awarded in the 50 states and D.C., individual sureties must be U.S. citizens. However, FAR 28.203 provides an exception for outlying areas and foreign countries.

Outlying area exception: When a contract is to be performed in an outlying area (Guam, Puerto Rico, U.S. Virgin Islands, American Samoa, Northern Mariana Islands) or a foreign country, a permanent resident of that area may serve as an individual surety, even if they are not a U.S. citizen.

Contracting officer location: The exception applies based on the contracting officer's location, not just where the contract will be performed. If the contracting officer is in an outlying area, permanent residency may suffice.

Documentation: Permanent residents should be prepared to provide their Green Card or other permanent residency documentation if requested by the contracting officer.

Source: FAR 28.203; SF 28 Instructions. acquisition.gov (FAR 28.203)

Net Worth

Financial Measure
Net Worth

Total assets minus total liabilities (debts and encumbrances). For individual sureties, net worth must equal or exceed the penal amount of the bond.

FAR requirement: FAR 28.203-1(b) requires that an individual surety's net worth be "not less than the penal amount" of the bond. This is a strict requirement — the contracting officer will reject affidavits that show net worth below the bond amount.

Calculation: Net Worth = (Real Estate + Securities + Cash + Other Assets) − (Mortgages + Liens + Loans + Credit Cards + Other Debts). All liabilities must be disclosed, even if they seem small.

Example: If you have $1.2 million in assets (home, investments, savings) and $400,000 in liabilities (mortgage, student loans, credit cards), your net worth is $800,000. This qualifies you for bonds up to $800,000 in penal amount.

Hidden liabilities: Tax liens, judgments, and undisclosed debts can suddenly reduce net worth below requirements. Always disclose all encumbrances in Field 8 of SF 28.

Source: FAR 28.203-1(b); SF 28 Instructions. acquisition.gov (FAR 28.203)

Payment Bond

Contract Bond Type
Payment Bond

A bond that guarantees the principal (contractor) will pay subcontractors, laborers, and material suppliers. If the principal fails to pay, the surety must pay these parties up to the penal amount.

Miller Act requirement: On federal construction contracts exceeding $150,000, the Miller Act (40 U.S.C. §§ 3131-3134) requires both a performance bond and a payment bond. The payment bond protects the subcontractors and suppliers who work on the project.

Penal amount: Payment bonds are typically set at 100% of the contract price, matching the performance bond amount. For a $500,000 contract, both bonds would be $500,000.

Relationship to performance bonds: Performance bonds guarantee the work gets done; payment bonds guarantee the workers and suppliers get paid. Both are commonly required on construction projects.

Individual surety eligibility: Individual sureties can guarantee payment bonds just as they can guarantee performance bonds, provided they meet all SF 28 requirements.

Source: FAR 28.102-1; Miller Act (40 U.S.C. §§ 3131-3134). acquisition.gov (FAR 28.102-1)

Market Value

Asset Valuation
Market Value

The estimated price an asset would fetch in an open transaction between a willing buyer and a willing seller. For SF 28 purposes, market value determines the asset's contribution toward the surety's required net worth.

Real estate market value: For real estate pledged as security, market value should be supported by a certified appraisal performed within the last 6 months. Tax assessments are not sufficient alone, as they may be outdated.

Securities market value: For stocks, bonds, and Treasury securities, market value is based on current trading prices. Field 7 of SF 28 requires both par value and current market value.

Why it matters: Net worth calculations use market value, not purchase price or tax assessment. A home purchased for $500,000 may now be worth $800,000 (good) or $400,000 (insufficient for larger bonds).

Contracting officer review: Contracting officers may question market values that seem inconsistent with market conditions. Be prepared to support your valuations with recent appraisals or broker statements.

Source: FAR 28.203-1(b); SF 28 Field 7 instructions. acquisition.gov (FAR 28.203)

Tax Assessment

Government Valuation
Tax Assessment

The value assigned to real property by local government for property tax purposes. Tax assessments are typically updated periodically (often annually) and may differ from actual market value.

Required documentation: SF 28 requires attaching current tax assessments for real estate pledged as security. This provides government-verified property information that contracting officers can rely on.

Limitations: Tax assessments may lag behind market changes. In rapidly appreciating markets, tax assessment may be lower than current market value. In declining markets, it may be higher. Use appraisals for current market value.

What to attach: The most recent property tax bill or assessment notice from the county or municipal tax assessor's office. This should show the assessment value, property description, and tax amount due.

Verification of clean title: Current tax assessments also help verify that property taxes are paid up to date. Unpaid property taxes represent a lien against the property that must be disclosed in Field 8.

Source: SF 28 Field 7 instructions; FAR 28.203-1(b). acquisition.gov (FAR 28.203)

Miller Act

Federal Law
Miller Act

A federal law (40 U.S.C. §§ 3131-3134) requiring performance and payment bonds for federal construction contracts exceeding $150,000. Enacted in 1935, it protects subcontractors and laborers on federal projects.

Bond requirements: The Miller Act requires performance bonds (guaranteeing contract completion) and payment bonds (guaranteeing payment to subcontractors, laborers, and material suppliers) for construction contracts above the threshold.

Penal amounts: Performance bonds must be for 100% of the contract price. Payment bonds must be for the amount of the performance bond, with a minimum determined by a formula based on contract size.

Relationship to SF 28: When an individual (rather than a corporate surety company) provides the Miller Act-required bonds, they must complete SF 28 to demonstrate they meet the financial and citizenship requirements.

Waiver for small contracts: Contracts of $150,000 or less may be performed without Miller Act bonds at the contracting officer's discretion, though other bond requirements may still apply.

Source: 40 U.S.C. §§ 3131-3134; FAR 28.102-1. Cornell Law (40 U.S.C. § 3131)

Evidence of Title

Ownership Documentation
Evidence of Title

Official documentation proving legal ownership of real property. For SF 28 purposes, evidence of title demonstrates that the surety actually owns the property they are pledging as security.

Required documents: Current property deed, title abstract, or title insurance policy showing the surety as the legal owner. These documents should be certified copies from the county recorder's office or title company.

Why it matters: The government must verify that the surety has clear ownership rights to the pledged asset. Evidence of title confirms the property can be legally used as collateral for the bond.

Title insurance: A title insurance policy is particularly valuable evidence because it includes research into the property's ownership history and identifies any existing liens or encumbrances.

Recording: Real estate ownership is recorded at the county level. The evidence of title should reference the official recording information (book, page, document number) for verification.

Joint ownership: If property is jointly owned (e.g., married couple), all owners typically must consent to the pledge, and this should be reflected in the documentation.

Source: SF 28 Field 7 instructions; FAR 28.203-1(b). acquisition.gov (FAR 28.203)

Recorded Lien

Encumbrance Type
Recorded Lien

A legal claim or encumbrance on property that has been officially filed with government authorities (typically the county recorder). Recorded liens publicly notify that the property owner owes money to a creditor.

Common examples: Mortgages, home equity loans, tax liens, mechanics' liens, judgment liens. These must all be disclosed in Field 8 of SF 28 as encumbrances on pledged assets.

Why disclosure is critical: Recorded liens reduce the surety's net worth and the government's ability to claim the asset. Failing to disclose a recorded lien constitutes a false statement under 18 U.S.C. § 1001.

How to find recorded liens: Title searches, property records searches at the county recorder's office, and credit reports will reveal most recorded liens. These should be reviewed before completing SF 28.

Impact on qualification: A property with substantial recorded liens may not provide sufficient unencumbered value to qualify as security. The equity (market value minus all liens) must still meet net worth requirements.

Title insurance: Title insurance policies typically identify recorded liens and are excellent documentation for both the surety's review and the contracting officer's verification.

Source: SF 28 Field 8 instructions; FAR 28.203-1(b). acquisition.gov (FAR 28.203)

Routing Transit Number (RTN)

Banking Identifier
Routing Transit Number

A 9-digit code that identifies financial institutions in the United States. RTNs are used for processing electronic transfers, wire transfers, and direct deposits between banks.

Purpose in SF 28: Field 6C requires the RTN of the financial institution submitting the pledge of securities on behalf of the individual surety. This enables the government to verify the securities pledge and transfer ownership if needed.

Where to find it: RTNs are printed on checks (bottom left, first 9 digits), available on the bank's website, or can be obtained by contacting the financial institution directly.

Verification: The RTN must correspond to the financial institution listed in Field 6A. Mismatched information may delay processing or raise questions about the validity of the securities pledge.

Security note: RTNs are public information and can be freely shared. They are not sensitive account numbers like account numbers or passwords.

International context: RTNs are U.S.-specific. For international financial institutions, equivalent codes (SWIFT/BIC codes) would be used, though SF 28 is primarily designed for domestic sureties.

Source: SF 28 Field 6C instructions; Federal Reserve Bank Services. Federal Reserve Routing Directory

Financial Institution

Banking Entity
Financial Institution

A bank, credit union, brokerage firm, or other entity that holds securities and can pledge them on behalf of an individual surety to support a government bond.

Role in SF 28: Field 6A-6F require detailed information about the financial institution that is submitting the pledge of securities. This includes name, address, contact person, and routing transit number.

Securities pledging: When securities (stocks, bonds, Treasury bills) are used as surety assets, they are typically held by a financial institution in a brokerage or custodial account. The institution pledges these securities to the government on the surety's behalf.

Documentation requirements: The financial institution must provide written confirmation of the pledge, including CUSIP numbers, par amounts, and current market values. This documentation is attached to the SF 28 affidavit.

Government relationship: The financial institution essentially becomes a third-party custodian of the pledged assets, providing assurance that the securities exist and are properly held for the government's potential claim.

Choice of institution: Sureties should use established financial institutions with experience in government securities pledging. The contracting officer may verify the institution's credentials.

Source: SF 28 Field 6 instructions; FAR 28.203-1(b). acquisition.gov (FAR 28.203)

Surety Broker

Intermediary Role
Surety Broker

An intermediary who assists in arranging surety bonds, including individual surety arrangements. Surety brokers help connect principals with suitable sureties and handle administrative aspects of bond procurement.

Role in SF 28: Fields 5A-5D request information about the individual surety broker used, if applicable. This includes name, address, telephone numbers, and email address.

When brokers are used: Contractors who need individual sureties often work with brokers to find qualified individuals willing to serve as sureties. Brokers may also help complete paperwork and ensure compliance with requirements.

Distinction from corporate surety: A surety broker arranges bonds but does not issue them. Corporate surety companies (insurance companies) actually issue and back the bonds. Individual sureties provide their personal assets directly.

Value to the process: Brokers can help ensure that SF 28 affidavits are completed correctly, that all required documentation is attached, and that the surety understands their obligations under the bond.

Optional information: If no surety broker is involved (e.g., a direct arrangement between principal and surety), these fields can be left blank or marked "N/A".

Source: SF 28 Field 5 instructions; surety industry practice. National Association of Surety Bond Producers
