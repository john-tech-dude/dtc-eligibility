The Depository Trust Company (DTC) — Eligibility Questionnaire

DTC is a subsidiary of The Depository Trust & Clearing Corporation

⏱ Critical Deadlines at a Glance

  • 10 business days before closing: Submit completed questionnaire + offering statement/prospectus to DTC's Underwriting Dept. via UW SOURCE or UWC.
  • 7 business days before closing: Provide complete CUSIP INFORMATION (numbers, interest rates, final maturities) in writing.
  • 10 business days after closing: Transfer Agent must submit Agent Confirmation via email to DTC's Underwriting Department.
  • 1 business day before closing: Deliver signed Letter of Representations (if BEO) and physical securities by 12:00 noon ET.
  • Corporate issues: CUSIP info due upon pricing (not a fixed pre-closing deadline).
Note: Per the June 2026 OA, these timeframes are "standards and not deadlines" — DTC may extend any timeframe in its sole discretion. However, do not rely on extensions; plan for the standard timelines.
To make a new issue of securities DTC eligible, the completed questionnaire and a copy of the offering statement/prospectus (preliminary or final) must be submitted to DTC's Underwriting Dept. at least 10 business days prior to the issue's closing date. (With respect to corporate issues, upon pricing of the issue.)

If complete CUSIP INFORMATION (CUSIP numbers, interest rates, and final maturities) is not included with the questionnaire, please provide to DTC in writing at least seven business days prior to the closing date.

Agent Confirmation: The Transfer Agent must submit an Agent Confirmation via email to DTC's Underwriting Department within ten (10) business days after the Closing Date. This confirms the agent has executed an Operational Arrangements Agent Letter and is prepared to perform its duties.

Electronic submission: DTC now accepts submissions through the UW SOURCE or Underwriting Central (UWC) platforms. Paper submissions are still accepted but electronic is preferred.

Red Flags: DTC's Discretionary Powers

Absolute Discretion on Counsel

DTC reserves absolute discretion to approve or reject the counsel providing a legal opinion. Even a well-known firm can be rejected if DTC determines it lacks sufficient securities expertise or has a conflict of interest.

Deadline Extensions Are Discretionary

The OA states all timeframes are "standards and not deadlines." DTC may extend deadlines, but never count on it. Missing the 10-day submission window can delay or derail a closing.

Indemnity Letters for Restricted Issuers

Issuers with ownership restrictions (REITs, NOL preservation provisions, maritime issuers) must provide an indemnity letter protecting DTC and Participants. DTC may require this even if not explicitly listed in the standard questionnaire.

OFAC Sanctions Compliance

All issuers, agents, and underwriters must agree not to conduct any transaction through DTC that violates OFAC sanctions. This is a hard stop — DTC will deny eligibility if sanctions concerns exist.

Certificate Sizing >$500M

If any single maturity exceeds $500 million, the issuer must provide multiple certificates (one per $500M tranche). A single oversized certificate will be rejected.

Foreign Issuer Legal Opinions

For non-U.S. issuers, DTC may require a foreign legal opinion confirming that DTC's rights as nominee are enforceable under local law. This can add significant time and cost.

DTC Can Discontinue Services Anytime

DTC reserves the right to discontinue providing depository services for any security at any time by giving reasonable notice. This is not merely a theoretical risk — DTC has exercised this right for securities with persistent operational failures, compliance violations, or issuer insolvency.

Non-U.S. Currency Requires Special Rider

Securities denominated in or paying interest in non-USD currencies require a distinct LOR rider separate from the UK/Ireland rider. This is often overlooked for euro-denominated bonds from non-UK issuers. The rider addresses FX conversion mechanics and payment timing.

Scenario-Based Learning

Work through four realistic offerings to see how the questionnaire applies in practice. Click a tab to switch scenarios.

Municipal Serial Maturities BEO

Scenario A: State of Franklin General Obligation Bonds, Series 2025A

The Offering: $250 million GO bond with serial maturities from 2026–2035, issued as Book-Entry-Only. Lead underwriter is a DTC Participant. Transfer Agent is U.S. Bank.

Questionnaire Walkthrough:

  • Issue Type: Check "Municipal." Do NOT check "Limited Underwriting" because principal exceeds $1 million.
  • Article 8 Security: Yes — municipal bonds are clearly securities under NY UCC Article 8.
  • UK Entity: No — issuer is a U.S. state.
  • Registration: Municipal securities are exempt from SEC registration under Rule 3(a)(2). Check "Exempt under Rule 3(a)(2)."
  • CUSIP Table: You need 10 rows (one per serial maturity). Each gets its own CUSIP, interest rate, and maturity date. Type code = "S" for Serial.
  • BEO: Yes — no certificates for investors. A Letter of Representations is required. Check if the State of Franklin already has a Blanket LOR on file with DTC. If yes, no new LOR needed.
  • Put/Tender: No — standard GO bonds without optional redemption features.
  • Agent Confirmation: U.S. Bank (Transfer Agent) must submit confirmation via email within 10 business days after closing.
Key Takeaway: Serial maturities multiply your CUSIP workload. Each maturity is a separate security in DTC's system. Verify the issuer's BLOR status before closing to avoid last-minute LOR execution.
Corporate Rule 144A Non-Investment Grade

Scenario B: Acme Corp. 8.500% Senior Notes due 2030 (Rule 144A)

The Offering: $500 million high-yield notes sold only to QIBs under Rule 144A. Distinct CUSIP from any registered notes. Lead underwriter is a DTC Participant.

Questionnaire Walkthrough:

  • Issue Type: Check "Corporate."
  • Registration: Check "Eligible for resale under Rule 144A of the '33 Act."
  • CUSIP: Must be distinct from any registered Acme Corp. notes. The CUSIP table gets one row (term bond). Type code = "T" for Term.
  • LOR Requirements: Because this is Rule 144A, a rider to the LOR is required. The rider confirms that the securities are legally restricted and that Cede & Co. will be the registered owner of the restricted securities.
  • PORTAL: Historically required for non-IG 144A issues. Per current OA, only the distinct CUSIP and LOR rider are required. Private trading venues have replaced PORTAL.
  • Electronic Submission: Submit through UW SOURCE upon pricing. CUSIP info is due upon pricing (not 7 days pre-close).
  • Certificate Sizing: $500 million exactly — one certificate for the full amount is acceptable (threshold is exceeding $500M). If it were $500,000,001, you'd need two certificates.
Key Takeaway: Rule 144A issues trigger the most LOR complexity. Always confirm the CUSIP is distinct from registered securities of the same class. The LOR rider is easy to overlook but mandatory.
Corporate UK Entity SEC Registered

Scenario C: Thames Industries plc — SEC-Registered Notes

The Offering: UK-incorporated issuer doing a registered offering of $300 million notes under Form F-3. Lead underwriter is a DTC Participant. Transfer Agent is Computershare.

Questionnaire Walkthrough:

  • UK Entity: Check "Yes." This triggers the UK/Ireland LOR rider, which addresses conflicts of law and insolvency considerations.
  • Registration: Check "Registered with the SEC."
  • State of Incorporation: "United Kingdom" — this may trigger a request for a UK legal opinion confirming DTC's rights.
  • LOR: Standard Issuer LOR plus the UK/Ireland rider. Because this is registered (not 144A/Reg S), no additional restriction rider is needed.
  • Signatures: Because the issuer is non-U.S., original manual signatures are required on the LOR. Electronic signatures are NOT permitted for non-U.S. entities per the OA.
  • Agent Confirmation: Computershare must confirm within 10 business days after closing. Ensure they have an executed Operational Arrangements Agent Letter on file with DTC.
  • OFAC: The underwriter must confirm no OFAC sanctions apply to Thames Industries or its principals.
Key Takeaway: Foreign issuers add layers. The UK rider, potential foreign legal opinion, and original signature requirement can each add days to the timeline. Start the LOR process early.
Corporate UK Entity Rule 144A + Reg S BEO Put Feature

Scenario D: British Telecom plc — Dual 144A/Reg S Notes with Put Option

The Offering: £400 million notes issued by a UK-incorporated entity, eligible for resale under both Rule 144A to QIBs and Regulation S to non-U.S. persons. Book-Entry-Only with a 3-year put option. Lead underwriter is a DTC Participant. Transfer Agent is Euroclear Bank.

Questionnaire Walkthrough:

  • UK Entity: Check "Yes." This triggers the UK/Ireland LOR rider addressing conflicts of law, insolvency, and enforceability under UK law.
  • Article 8 Security: Yes — notes are clearly securities under NY UCC Article 8.
  • Registration: Check BOTH "Eligible for resale under Rule 144A of the '33 Act" AND "Eligible for resale under Regulation S of the '33 Act." This dual eligibility is increasingly common for global issuers.
  • BEO: Yes — Book-Entry-Only with no certificates available to investors. Requires LOR execution.
  • Put/Tender: Yes — the notes have a 3-year put option requiring a Tender Letter of Representations (Tender LOR) in addition to the standard Issuer LOR.
  • LOR Requirements: This is the most complex scenario requiring THREE LOR components: (1) Standard Issuer LOR, (2) UK/Ireland rider, and (3) Tender LOR. Because of the 144A eligibility, ensure the LOR rider addresses restricted securities and Cede & Co. ownership.
  • Signatures: Original manual signatures required on ALL LOR components because the issuer is non-U.S. Electronic signatures are not permitted.
  • CUSIP: Must be distinct from any registered British Telecom notes. The dual 144A/Reg S structure requires separation from any U.S. registered securities of the same class.
  • Currency: £ (GBP) denomination — may trigger a non-U.S. currency rider in addition to the UK rider. Verify with DTC if both riders are required for GBP-denominated UK issuer securities.
  • Agent Confirmation: Euroclear Bank must confirm within 10 business days after closing. Ensure they have an executed Operational Arrangements Agent Letter with DTC and understand BEO mechanics.
  • OFAC: Underwriter must confirm no OFAC sanctions apply to British Telecom or its principals.
Key Takeaway: This scenario represents maximum complexity — UK entity + dual 144A/Reg S + BEO + put feature + foreign currency. The combination of UK rider, potential currency rider, Tender LOR, and 144A restrictions means the LOR package becomes quite involved. Start the LOR process at least 3-4 weeks before closing, and verify with DTC whether both UK and currency riders are required for GBP-denominated issues.
Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

❌ Missing the Agent Confirmation

The Transfer Agent must submit an Agent Confirmation via email to DTC's Underwriting Department within 10 business days after the Closing Date. This confirms the agent has executed an Operational Arrangements Agent Letter with DTC and is prepared to perform its duties. This requirement was updated in the June 2026 OA (changed from pre-closing to post-closing timing).

❌ Wrong Certificate Size for Large Maturities

If a single maturity exceeds $500 million, you must provide multiple certificates (one per $500M tranche plus remainder). A single $750M certificate will be rejected. This applies to both BEO and certificated issues.

❌ Assuming a BLOR Covers a New Issuer Name

If the issuer's legal name on the offering document differs from the name on the existing Blanket LOR (e.g., due to a merger or reorganization), DTC will require a new LOR or legal opinion. Always verify the exact name match.

❌ Using the Same CUSIP for 144A and Registered Securities

DTC requires distinct CUSIPs for restricted and unrestricted securities of the same class. Using the same CUSIP will cause eligibility rejection and may create settlement failures.

❌ Forgetting the Indemnity Letter for REITs / NOL Issuers

Issuers with ownership restrictions (e.g., REITs preserving their tax status, or companies with NOL carryforwards) must provide an indemnity letter. DTC will not process eligibility without it, and it's not explicitly requested on the standard questionnaire.

❌ Electronic Signature for Non-U.S. Entities

While the June 2026 OA permits electronic signatures in many cases, non-U.S. entities and legal opinions still require original manual signatures. Using DocuSign for a UK issuer's LOR will result in rejection.

Other Eligibility Paths

The standard new-issue questionnaire is not the only path to DTC eligibility. These alternative paths apply to specific situations.

Older Issue Eligibility

For already-issued and outstanding securities (not new underwritten offerings), DTC uses a distinct process:

  • Submit an Older Eligibility Questionnaire (different form)
  • Present physical certificates for the outstanding securities
  • Provide an Agent Attestation form
  • Submit through UW SOURCE as an "Eligibility Only" request
This path is common for secondary market listings, private placements becoming tradable, or legacy securities seeking DTC access.

Custody-Only Services

Some securities qualify only for limited custody service, not full nominee registration or book-entry transfer:

  • Securities with transfer restrictions that prevent free book-entry movement
  • Non-fungible bulk securities (e.g., physical commodities, unique certificates)
  • Issues where DTC holds certificates but does not register Cede & Co. as nominee
Custody-only securities cannot be settled through DTC's book-entry system. They are held physically and transferred manually.

Money Market Instruments (MMI)

MMI are processed under dedicated DTC Rules with special form letters and a separate help line:

  • Commercial paper, banker's acceptances, and short-term notes
  • Dedicated customer help line: (866) 724-4402
  • Specialized MMI form letters instead of standard LORs
  • Processing under DTC Rules rather than Operational Arrangements
MMI eligibility is faster but has different documentation requirements. Always use the MMI-specific forms.

Segregation Account 100 (Seg 100)

For issuers with ownership restrictions (shipping, FCC, gaming licenses), DTC offers the Seg 100 compliance tool:

  • Allows Participants to track and restrict ownership for regulatory compliance
  • DTC does not monitor compliance — it provides the tool only
  • Requires an indemnity letter from the issuer
  • Commonly used by REITs, maritime issuers, and broadcast licensees
Seg 100 is not an eligibility path per se, but a post-eligibility compliance mechanism. The standard questionnaire still applies.

Retail Certificates of Deposit (E-CD)

Retail CDs use a dedicated Electronic Master Certificate BLOR:

  • Specialized form distinct from standard corporate or municipal LORs
  • Covers all CDs issued under a master certificate program
  • Requires bank issuer to maintain the master certificate at DTC
  • Individual CDs are issued in book-entry form without separate certificates
Bank issuers should contact DTC's Corporate Eligibility team (212-855-3782) for the E-CD BLOR template.

Unit Securities — Separation Rules

Unit securities (debt + equity/warrant combined) have special separation rules:

  • Voluntary separation: At closing, the underwriter may elect to separate the unit into its components, each receiving its own CUSIP
  • Mandatory separation: After closing, DTC may require separation if the components trade independently
  • Each component must independently qualify for DTC eligibility
  • The unit itself receives a CUSIP, plus each component receives its own
Coordinate with DTC Underwriting before closing if your offering includes units. The separation decision affects CUSIP assignment and settlement.

The Depository Trust Company (DTC)

Definition
The Depository Trust Company

The world's largest securities depository, holding over $87 trillion in assets. Founded in 1973 and headquartered in New York City, DTC is a subsidiary of The Depository Trust & Clearing Corporation (DTCC).

DTC provides custody and book-entry transfer services for eligible securities. When securities are "DTC-eligible," they can be held, transferred, and settled electronically through DTC's systems rather than via physical certificate delivery.

DTC's nominee, Cede & Co., is the registered owner of record for all securities held in DTC's depository. Beneficial owners hold their interests through DTC Participants (broker-dealers, banks, and other financial institutions).

DTC was created to solve the "paperwork crisis" of the late 1960s and early 1970s, when the surge in trading volume overwhelmed the manual, certificate-based settlement system.

Source: Securities Exchange Act of 1934, §17A(a); DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I. dtcc.com

The Depository Trust & Clearing Corporation (DTCC)

Parent Organization
DTCC

The parent company of DTC and other critical financial infrastructure organizations. A systemically important financial market utility overseen by the Federal Reserve, SEC, and CFTC.

DTCC's subsidiaries include:

  • DTC — Depository Trust Company (securities custody & book-entry)
  • NSCC — National Securities Clearing Corporation (equity trade clearing)
  • FICC — Fixed Income Clearing Corporation (bond clearing)
Source: 17 CFR §240; SEC Oversight. dtcc.com

What does "DTC Eligible" mean?

Eligibility Standard
DTC Eligible

A security that DTC has accepted for its book-entry delivery, settlement, and depository services, enabling electronic trading and settlement.

DTC eligibility is the gateway to the U.S. clearing and settlement infrastructure. Without it, trades would require manual handling of physical certificates, dramatically reducing liquidity and increasing counterparty risk.

Generally, issues eligible for DTC services are those issued in a transaction that: (i) has been registered with the SEC under the Securities Act of 1933; (ii) was exempt from registration under a Securities Act exemption that does not involve transfer or ownership restrictions; or (iii) permits resale pursuant to Rule 144A or Regulation S.

To be DTC-eligible, issues must generally: Be registered with the SEC or qualify for an exemption without transfer restrictions · Not have ownership restrictions conflicting with DTC holding as nominee · Have proper documentation (Letter of Representations) · Have valid CUSIP numbers · Meet special requirements for the security type
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.1. dtcc.com

UW SOURCE / Underwriting Central (UWC)

Electronic Submission Platform
UW SOURCE / UWC

DTC's online platforms for submitting eligibility requests, offering documents, and supporting materials. UW SOURCE (Securities Origination, Underwriting and Reliable Corporate Action Environment) and Underwriting Central (UWC) have replaced paper-based submission as the preferred method.

What Changes with Electronic Submission

  • Faster processing: Electronic submissions are routed directly into DTC's underwriting workflow, reducing manual data entry delays.
  • Document management: Offering statements, prospectuses, and legal opinions can be uploaded as PDFs rather than mailed or faxed.
  • Status tracking: Submitters can track the status of their eligibility request in real time.
  • Not mandatory: Paper submissions are still accepted, but electronic is strongly preferred and may process faster.
Access: UW SOURCE and UWC are available to DTC Participants and authorized users. Contact your DTC relationship manager or DTC's Client Services for access credentials.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.A.1 — Submission Requirements (June 2026). dtcc.com

Agent Confirmation

Updated Requirement (June 2026 OA)
Agent Confirmation

A written confirmation from the Transfer Agent (or other designated agent) submitted via email to DTC's Underwriting Department within ten (10) business days after the Closing Date, confirming the agent has executed an Operational Arrangements Agent Letter with DTC and is prepared to perform its duties.

Why This Matters

The Agent Confirmation requirement was updated in the June 2026 OA to provide post-closing timing (changed from 3 business days before closing). This ensures Transfer Agents are properly onboarded and contractually bound to DTC while providing flexibility for the closing process.

What the Transfer Agent Must Confirm

  • It has executed an Operational Arrangements Agent Letter with DTC.
  • It understands its responsibilities for the specific issue (income collection, transfers, corporate actions).
  • It will comply with DTC's instructions and timelines.
Action item: Confirm with your Transfer Agent that they have an active OA Agent Letter with DTC before the closing date. If they don't, the agent must execute one, which can take several days. The Agent Confirmation itself is due within 10 business days after closing.

Closing Date

Transaction Milestone
Closing Date

The date on which the issuer delivers the securities to the underwriter and receives the proceeds of the offering. All DTC eligibility documentation must be completed before this date.

The 10-business-day submission deadline is measured backward from this date. For corporate issues, the CUSIP information trigger is "upon pricing" rather than a fixed pre-closing deadline, reflecting that corporate pricing often occurs closer to closing than in municipal offerings.

Article 8 of the NY UCC — "Security" Definition

Legal Framework
Article 8 — Investment Securities

The New York Uniform Commercial Code article governing investment securities. Defines what constitutes a "security" and provides the legal basis for the indirect holding system used by DTC.

Article 8 defines a "security" as an obligation of an issuer or a share, participation, or other interest in an issuer or in property or an enterprise of an issuer that:

  • Is represented by a security certificate in bearer or registered form, or transferable upon books maintained by or on behalf of the issuer;
  • Is one of a class or series of shares, participations, interests, or obligations; and
  • Is, or is of a type, dealt in or traded on securities exchanges or securities markets, or is a medium for investment.

This definition is critical because DTC's book-entry system operates under Article 8. If an instrument is not a "security" under Article 8, it cannot be held in DTC's depository system. New York is the governing jurisdiction because DTC is a New York corporation and the UCC Article 8 framework validates the indirect holding system (where Cede & Co. is the registered owner).

Practical guidance: Answer "Yes" for virtually all debt and equity instruments. Answer "No" only if the instrument does not meet the UCC Article 8 definition (rare).
Source: NY UCC Article 8 (1994 Revised Article 8); DTC Operational Arrangements. cornell.edu (UCC Article 8)

New York Uniform Commercial Code (NY UCC)

State Law
New York Uniform Commercial Code

New York's codification of the Uniform Commercial Code, the body of law governing commercial transactions. Article 8 (Investment Securities) provides the legal foundation for DTC's book-entry system.

Article 8 of the NY UCC was substantially revised in 1994 to accommodate the indirect holding system, where securities are held through intermediaries rather than directly by beneficial owners. Under this system, DTC's nominee (Cede & Co.) is the registered owner on the issuer's books, while beneficial owners hold their interests through DTC Participants. This is legally validated by NY UCC Section 8-503 and related provisions.

Source: NY UCC Article 8 (1994 Revised); DTC Operational Arrangements. cornell.edu (UCC Article 8)

Rule 144A

Regulation S — Offshore Offerings

SEC Safe Harbor
Regulation S

A safe harbor under the Securities Act of 1933 for offers and sales of securities outside the United States. Designed to facilitate access to international capital markets without full SEC registration.

Distribution Compliance Period

Regulation S prohibits offers and sales to U.S. persons during the distribution compliance period. For debt securities, this is typically 40 days (90 days for certain categories). During this period, beneficial interests may only be held through Euroclear and Clearstream as DTC participants.

Restriction
U.S. Person Prohibition

During the compliance period, securities cannot be purchased or held by U.S. persons, except in limited circumstances. This includes direct purchases and indirect holdings through U.S. intermediaries.

Key Requirements for DTC Eligibility

  • Must have a distinct CUSIP or CINS number different from any registered securities of the same class.
  • Prior to expiration of the Distribution Compliance Period (40 days for debt, 1 year for equity), beneficial interests may only be held through Euroclear and Clearstream as DTC participants.
  • A rider to the Letter of Representations is required to address the restricted nature of Regulation S securities.
  • Dual 144A/Reg S: Many offerings combine Rule 144A and Regulation S eligibility. In these cases, the 144A LOR rider (addressing restricted securities and Cede & Co. ownership) typically covers both exemptions.
  • Global offerings: Regulation S is commonly used in global offerings where issuers want to reach both U.S. institutional investors (via 144A) and international investors (via Reg S).
Source: SEC Regulation S (17 CFR §230.901 et seq.); DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.1.b. sec.gov

Section 3(c)(7) of the Investment Company Act

Exemption
Section 3(c)(7)

An exemption from registration as an "investment company" for issuers whose outstanding securities are owned exclusively by "qualified purchasers" — individuals with $5M+ in investments, entities with $25M+.

Unlike Section 3(c)(1) (which caps beneficial owners at 100), Section 3(c)(7) has no ownership limit but restricts ownership to qualified purchasers. Popular for private equity funds, hedge funds, and other pooled investment vehicles targeting ultra-high-net-worth investors.

OFAC Compliance — Sanctions Screening

Treasury Department
OFAC (Office of Foreign Assets Control)

U.S. Treasury agency that administers and enforces economic and trade sanctions based on U.S. foreign policy and national security goals. OFAC maintains the Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) list and other sanctions programs.

OFAC Requirements for DTC Eligibility

Underwriters must confirm that neither the issuer nor its principals (officers, directors, significant shareholders) appear on OFAC sanctions lists. This is a hard stop — DTC will deny eligibility if sanctions concerns exist.

Compliance Steps
OFAC Screening Process

Before submitting the eligibility questionnaire, underwriters should: (1) Screen the issuer's legal name against the SDN list, (2) Screen officers, directors, and 10%+ shareholders, (3) Check for matches against all OFAC sanctions programs (not just SDN list), (4) Document the screening process and results.

Red Flags

  • High-risk jurisdictions: Issuers from countries under comprehensive U.S. sanctions (e.g., Iran, North Korea, Syria, Russia)
  • Name variations: Issuers with names similar to sanctioned entities require additional due diligence
  • Complex ownership: Shell companies or opaque ownership structures may hide sanctioned individuals
  • Political exposure: Foreign government officials or their family members may be subject to sanctions

Letter of Representations (LOR)

Legal Agreement

A legal agreement between the issuer, its agent, and DTC that sets forth the operational terms under which DTC will provide depository and book-entry services for the securities.

Non-U.S. Currency LOR Rider

Specialized LOR Addendum
Non-U.S. Currency Rider

A required addendum to the Letter of Representations for securities denominated in or having payments in a currency other than U.S. dollars. Distinct from the UK/Ireland rider.

When Required

  • Securities with principal or interest payable in euros, pounds, yen, or any non-USD currency
  • Dual-currency securities (e.g., principal in USD, interest in EUR)
  • Securities with currency conversion features or contingent currency provisions

What the Rider Covers

  • Currency conversion mechanics and timing
  • Exchange rate risk allocation between issuer and DTC
  • Payment instructions in non-USD currencies through DTC's systems
  • Coordination with Euroclear/Clearstream for cross-border settlement

Practical Impact

The non-USD currency rider adds operational complexity because DTC's income collection and payment systems are primarily USD-based. The issuer must establish:

  • A foreign currency account with DTC's designated bank
  • Clear instructions for currency conversion on payment dates
  • Backup USD payment provisions in case of currency unavailability
Key distinction: The UK/Ireland rider addresses legal jurisdiction and insolvency law. The non-USD currency rider addresses payment mechanics and FX risk. Both may be required for a UK issuer issuing euro-denominated bonds.

Approved Correspondent Relationship

Eligibility Path
Correspondent Relationship

An arrangement where a non-Participant underwriter or financial advisor partners with a DTC Participant to submit eligibility requests and handle settlement. The Participant acts as the formal DTC counterparty.

Cede & Co. — DTC's Nominee

Nominee Name
Cede & Co.

The registered nominee of The Depository Trust Company. Cede & Co. appears as the registered owner of record on the issuer's books for all securities held in DTC's depository.

Why Cede & Co. Exists

The indirect holding system — where intermediaries hold securities on behalf of beneficial owners — requires a single registered owner on the issuer's transfer records. Rather than registering millions of individual beneficial owners (which would be administratively impossible), DTC uses its nominee, Cede & Co., as the sole registered owner.

This structure was legally validated by the 1994 revisions to Article 8 of the NY UCC, which explicitly recognized the indirect holding system and the rights of beneficial owners through their intermediaries.

How the Chain Works

  1. Issuer's books: Show "Cede & Co." as the registered owner of 100% of the issue.
  2. DTC's records: Show which DTC Participants hold what positions.
  3. Participant's records: Show which beneficial owners (customers) hold what positions.
  4. Beneficial owner: Holds the economic interest but is not on the issuer's register.

Legal Rights

  • Cede & Co. holds legal title but has no beneficial interest. It cannot vote, receive payments for its own benefit, or exercise put/tender rights except as directed by DTC Participants.
  • DTC Participants (broker-dealers, banks) are the "direct participants" in DTC's system. They hold securities in their DTC account and allocate positions to their customers.
  • Beneficial owners (investors) hold their interests through their broker or bank. They receive proxy materials, income payments, and corporate action notices through their intermediary.

Practical Implications

  • Dividends & interest: The Paying Agent remits to DTC in the name of Cede & Co. DTC then allocates to Participants, who allocate to beneficial owners.
  • Proxy voting: Issuers send proxy materials to Cede & Co. DTC forwards to Participants, who forward to beneficial owners. Votes flow back up the chain.
  • Corporate actions: Tender offers, exchanges, and redemptions are processed through DTC's systems (ATOP, ASOP, etc.) with Cede & Co. as the formal counterparty.
  • Legal proceedings: In class actions or bankruptcies, notice to Cede & Co. constitutes notice to all beneficial holders under NY UCC §8-503.
Key point for issuers: When you sign a Letter of Representations, you agree to recognize Cede & Co. as the sole registered owner and to make all payments and send all notices through DTC. You do not deal directly with beneficial owners for book-entry securities.
Source: NY UCC Article 8 (1994 Revised), §8-503; DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.B.1.d; SEC Release No. 34-38067. cornell.edu (UCC Article 8) | dtcc.com

Issuer Name / Issue Description

Form Field
Issuer Name / Issue Description

The legal name of the entity issuing the securities, followed by a brief description of the specific issue (series, tranche, or program).

What to Enter

  • Issuer Name: The exact legal name of the issuing entity as it appears in the organizational documents and offering statement. For municipal issuers, this is the governmental entity (e.g., "State of California" or "New York City Municipal Water Finance Authority"). For corporate issuers, use the full corporate name (e.g., "Apple Inc." not "Apple").
  • Issue Description: A concise identifier for this specific issuance. Examples:
    • "Series 2025A General Obligation Bonds"
    • "5.250% Notes due 2035"
    • "Floating Rate Notes, Series F"
    • "Trust Certificates, Series 2025-1" (for ABS/CMO)

Why Accuracy Matters

DTC uses this field to create the security master record. The name must match the offering documents exactly because:

  • It appears on DTC's Participant reports and position listings.
  • It feeds into market data vendors (Bloomberg, Refinitiv) and rating agency systems.
  • It determines whether an existing Blanket LOR (BLOR) covers this issuer — if the name differs from the BLOR, a new LOR or legal opinion may be required.
  • It is used for tax reporting (1099, 1042-S) and regulatory filings.
Best practice: Copy the issuer name directly from the offering document's cover page. Do not abbreviate or use trade names. For SPV/trust issuers, include the full trust name (e.g., "ABC Auto Receivables Trust 2025-1").

Share Quantity

Form Field
Share Quantity

The total number of shares, units, or certificates being issued. For debt securities, this field may be left blank or marked "N/A" since debt is typically measured by principal amount.

When to Use This Field

  • Equity securities: Enter the total number of shares (common or preferred) being offered. Example: "10,000,000" for 10 million shares.
  • Warrants / rights: Enter the number of warrants or rights units.
  • Unit investment trusts (UITs): Enter the number of trust units.
  • Exchange-traded funds (ETFs): Enter the number of creation units or shares.
  • Debt securities (bonds, notes): Typically leave blank or enter "N/A" — the "Principal / Offering Amount" field captures the par value. However, if the debt is issued in certificated form with a specific number of certificates, you may enter that number here.

Relationship to Other Fields

  • Share Quantity × Offering Price per Share = Gross Proceeds (for equity).
  • For unit securities (debt + equity combined), enter the number of units. The debt and equity components should each have their own CUSIP and row in the CUSIP Information table.
  • For ADR/GDR programs, enter the number of depositary receipts, not the underlying shares.
Tip: If your offering includes both debt and equity (e.g., convertible bonds), fill in "Share Quantity" only for the equity component. The convertible feature is described in the Issue Description and captured in the CUSIP table.

Closing Date — Form Field

Form Field
Closing Date

The scheduled date on which the issuer will deliver the securities to the underwriter and receive the offering proceeds. This is the anchor date from which all DTC deadlines are calculated.

How to Determine the Closing Date

  • Firm commitment underwriting: Closing is typically 3–5 business days after pricing, allowing time for document finalization and settlement.
  • Best efforts / continuous offerings: Closing may be a rolling date or the final date of the offering period.
  • Municipal bond closings: Often scheduled to align with project funding needs and may involve multiple closings ("draw-down" structures).
  • Rule 144A / Reg S: Closing may occur simultaneously with or shortly after the initial sale to QIBs or offshore purchasers.

What Happens on Closing Date

  1. Issuer delivers executed securities (or master certificate for BEO) to DTC or the designated DTC Participant.
  2. Underwriter wires offering proceeds to the issuer.
  3. DTC credits the securities to the Participant's account specified on the questionnaire.
  4. Securities become available for book-entry trading and settlement among DTC Participants.
Critical: If the closing date changes (a "delayed closing"), notify DTC's Underwriting Department immediately. DTC may need to adjust its processing schedule, and the Letter of Representations may need to be re-dated.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.2 — Standard Time Frames; NABL "Demystifying DTC" (2023). dtcc.com

Lead Underwriter — Form Field

Form Field
Lead Underwriter

The investment bank or financial institution managing the offering and acting as the primary liaison with DTC for eligibility processing. This entity is typically responsible for submitting the questionnaire.

Who Qualifies as Lead Underwriter

  • Manager / Bookrunner: The firm that structures the offering, sets pricing, and allocates securities to investors.
  • Sole underwriter: In smaller offerings, a single firm may act as both lead and sole underwriter.
  • Syndicate lead: In larger offerings, multiple underwriters form a syndicate; the "lead" or "left lead" is the managing underwriter with primary responsibility.
  • Placement agent: In private placements (e.g., Rule 144A), the placement agent performs a similar role but does not purchase securities for resale in the same way a traditional underwriter does.
  • Initial purchaser: In 144A/Reg S offerings, the initial purchaser buys from the issuer and resells to QIBs or offshore investors.

DTC Requirements

  • The Lead Underwriter (or its clearing DTC Participant) must be a DTC Participant or have an approved correspondent relationship with one. Only Participants can request eligibility.
  • The Lead Underwriter is responsible for the accuracy of all information on the questionnaire.
  • The Lead Underwriter must ensure the Letter of Representations is delivered to DTC no later than one business day before closing.
  • For municipal offerings, the MSRB requires underwriters to comply with DTC's time frames.
If the Lead Underwriter is NOT a DTC Participant: You must identify a Clearing DTC Participant (below) who will handle the book-entry settlement. The clearing participant becomes the direct counterparty with DTC, even though the lead underwriter manages the offering.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.A.1 — Submission Requirements; SEC Securities Act of 1933, §2(a)(11). dtcc.com

Lead Underwriter Contact — Form Field

Form Field
Lead Underwriter Contact

The specific individual at the Lead Underwriter who DTC should contact with questions about the eligibility request, document submissions, or closing coordination.

Who to Name

  • Operations / settlement desk: The person who handles DTC eligibility submissions and closing logistics. This is often a Vice President or Director in the underwriter's Operations, Settlement, or Corporate Trust group.
  • Capital markets / syndicate desk: In some firms, the syndicate manager coordinates with DTC.
  • Legal / compliance: For complex structures (ABS, CMO, 144A/Reg S), the underwriter's counsel may be the primary contact.

What DTC Uses This For

  • Eligibility review: DTC may call or email to clarify information on the questionnaire or request additional documents.
  • Closing coordination: On the closing date, DTC confirms receipt of securities and credits the Participant's account. Any issues (missing docs, certificate problems) are escalated to this contact.
  • Post-closing: If discrepancies are found (e.g., incorrect CUSIP, wrong principal amount), DTC reaches out to resolve.

Information to Provide

  • Full name and title
  • Direct phone number (not a general desk line)
  • Email address
  • Backup contact (optional but recommended for time-sensitive closings)
Best practice: Provide a direct line, not a general operations desk. DTC Underwriting works on tight deadlines and needs immediate access to someone with authority to answer questions and make decisions.
Source: DTC Eligibility Questionnaire; DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.A.1. dtcc.com

Issuer's Counsel — Form Field

Form Field
Issuer's Counsel

The law firm representing the issuer in the offering. This firm is responsible for drafting the offering documents, ensuring regulatory compliance, and providing legal opinions that may be required by DTC.

Role in DTC Eligibility

  • Opinion of counsel: DTC may require an opinion from Issuer's Counsel confirming that the securities are properly registered or exempt, that they qualify for DTC eligibility, and that the issuer has authority to issue the securities. This opinion must be from outside counsel (not in-house) licensed to practice in the relevant jurisdiction.
  • Letter of Representations: Issuer's Counsel often reviews and advises on the LOR before execution, ensuring the issuer understands its obligations to DTC.
  • Foreign legal opinions: For issuers organized outside the U.S., Issuer's Counsel may need to engage local counsel to provide a foreign legal opinion confirming DTC's rights under foreign law.
  • Indemnity letters: For securities with ownership restrictions (e.g., REITs, maritime issues), Issuer's Counsel drafts the indemnity letter protecting DTC and its Participants.

DTC's Requirements for Counsel

  • Must be engaged in independent private practice — in-house counsel opinions are generally not accepted.
  • Must be licensed and in good standing in the relevant jurisdiction.
  • Must be an experienced securities practitioner familiar with DTC eligibility requirements.
  • Must not have a beneficial ownership interest in the securities and must not be an officer, director, or employee of the issuer.
  • DTC reserves absolute discretion to approve or reject the counsel providing the opinion.

Information to Provide

  • Law firm name
  • Specific attorney name and title
  • Direct phone number
  • Email address
Pro tip: If DTC requests a legal opinion, do not wait until the last minute. Drafting, reviewing, and delivering a securities opinion can take several days. Build this into your 10-business-day timeline.

CUSIP Number — Column Explained

Column Field
CUSIP Number

The unique 9-character identifier for this specific security issue. Every distinct tranche, maturity, or series of an offering receives its own CUSIP.

Why This Matters for DTC

  • DTC uses CUSIP numbers as the primary key for all book-entry records. Without a valid CUSIP, DTC cannot create the security in its system.
  • Each row in this table represents a distinct CUSIP — so a single bond issue with 5 different maturities requires 5 rows.
  • For Rule 144A and Regulation S securities, the restricted CUSIP must differ from any unrestricted CUSIP of the same class.
  • CUSIPs are assigned by CUSIP Global Services (CGS), a division of S&P Global. Issuers or their agents must request assignment before submitting this questionnaire.
Pro tip: Verify your CUSIP with CGS before submitting. An invalid or duplicate CUSIP will delay eligibility approval. The check digit (9th character) should be mathematically valid.

Format

9 alphanumeric characters: XXXXXX XX X (6 issuer + 2 issue + 1 check digit). Example: 037833100 (Apple Inc. common stock).

Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.1.b; CUSIP Global Services. dtcc.com

Interest Rate — Column Explained

Column Field
Interest Rate

The annual coupon rate (stated interest rate) payable on the security, expressed as a percentage of par value.

What to Enter

  • Fixed-rate bonds: Enter the stated coupon (e.g., "5.25%" or "5.250%").
  • Variable-rate demand obligations (VRDOs): Enter the initial rate or "Variable" and provide the remarketing agent details in the Contact Information section.
  • Zero-coupon / capital appreciation bonds: Enter "0.000%" or "N/A — Zero Coupon." These instruments accrete in value rather than paying periodic interest.
  • Floating-rate notes: Enter the spread over the reference index (e.g., "SOFR + 125 bps") plus any rate cap or floor.
  • Step-up / step-down bonds: List all applicable rates with their effective dates in the description field.

DTC Processing

DTC uses this rate to calculate income payments distributed through its income collection system. The Paying Agent must remit payments based on this rate. If the rate changes (e.g., for VRDOs or step-ups), the issuer or agent must notify DTC promptly to update its records.

Common mistake: Do not confuse the interest rate with the yield to maturity or the initial offering yield. Enter only the stated coupon rate from the indenture or offering document.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section III.C — Payment Instructions; DTC Underwriting FAQ. dtcclearning.com

Final Maturity / Expiration Date — Column Explained

Column Field
Final Maturity / Expiration Date

The date on which the principal amount of the security becomes due and payable in full. For options or warrants, this is the expiration date.

What to Enter

  • Term bonds: Single maturity date (e.g., "06/15/2035").
  • Serial bonds: Multiple maturity dates within a single issue — each maturity gets its own CUSIP row.
  • Callable bonds: Enter the final maturity date, not the call date. Use the "Type of Issue" column to note if callable.
  • Puttable bonds: Enter the final maturity. The put dates are captured separately in the questionnaire's put/tender feature question.
  • Perpetual securities: Enter "Perpetual" or "No Maturity."
  • Warrants / rights: Enter the expiration date.

DTC Processing

DTC tracks maturity dates to trigger redemption notifications to Participants. On the maturity date, DTC instructs the Paying Agent to remit principal plus any final interest payment. DTC then distributes proceeds to Participants' accounts. If a maturity is extended or amended, the issuer must provide DTC with an indemnity letter authorizing the change.

Important: If the maturity date is amended after eligibility (e.g., via consent solicitation or restructuring), the issuer must notify DTC in writing and may need to provide an indemnity letter. DTC cannot unilaterally change maturity dates on its records.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section V.A — Redemptions; Section I.B.4.b — Revisions of Eligible Securities. dtcc.com

Principal / Offering Amount — Column Explained

Column Field
Principal / Offering Amount

The par value or face amount of this specific CUSIP being offered. This is the amount on which interest is calculated and the amount due at maturity.

What to Enter

  • Fixed principal: The total par amount for this CUSIP (e.g., "$50,000,000").
  • Accreted value bonds (CABs): Enter the initial offering amount (par at maturity). DTC tracks accretion separately.
  • Unit securities: Enter the aggregate principal amount of the debt component. The equity component is listed separately if it has a different CUSIP.
  • Reopened issues: Enter only the new principal amount being added, not the total outstanding.

Relationship to Total Offering

The sum of all "Principal / Offering Amount" values across all CUSIP rows should equal the Issue Principal / Offering Amount stated in the Issue Information section above. DTC uses this figure to verify that the total deposited certificates match the offering size.

Certificate Sizing

Per DTC's Operational Arrangements, if the aggregate principal amount of any single maturity exceeds $500 million, the issuer must provide multiple certificates: one for each $500 million tranche plus one for the remainder. Each certificate is registered in the name of Cede & Co.

Formatting: Use full dollar amounts with commas (e.g., "$100,000,000"). Do not abbreviate (e.g., avoid "$100MM"). DTC's systems parse these values for payment calculations.

Type of Issue — Column Explained

Column Field
Type of Issue

A single-letter code indicating the structural feature of the security. This helps DTC's systems apply the correct processing rules.

Valid Codes

  • C = Capital Appreciation — The security is issued at a discount and accretes to par at maturity. No periodic interest payments; the return is the difference between purchase price and redemption value. Common in municipal zero-coupon bonds.
  • P = Put — The security contains a put feature allowing holders to demand repayment before maturity on specified dates. Requires a Tender Letter of Representations if certificated.
  • S = Serial — The issue contains multiple maturity dates (serial maturities), with principal amortizing over time. Each serial maturity typically receives its own CUSIP and row in this table.
  • T = Term — The entire issue matures on a single date. The simplest structure; principal is repaid in full at maturity.

Combinations

Some securities may combine features (e.g., a serial bond with a put option). In such cases, list all applicable codes or describe the structure in the Issuer Name / Issue Description field. When in doubt, contact DTC's Underwriting Department before submission.

DTC Processing Impact

The type code determines which DTC processing modules are activated:

  • C (Capital Appreciation): DTC suppresses income payment processing until maturity.
  • P (Put): DTC enables tender/put processing workflows and requires coordination with the Tender Agent.
  • S (Serial): DTC sets up multiple maturity events and sinking fund schedules.
  • T (Term): Standard single-maturity processing.
Tip: If your issue does not fit neatly into one category (e.g., a VRDO with a mandatory tender), describe the structure in detail in your offering documents and contact DTC Underwriting for guidance on the correct code.
Source: DTC Eligibility Questionnaire; DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.C.2 — Unit Securities; Section VII — VRDOs. dtcc.com

Initial Offering Price (To the Public) — Column Explained

Column Field
Initial Offering Price (To the Public)

The price at which the security is first sold to investors, expressed as a percentage of par value (e.g., "99.50%" or "100.00%"). Also called the "public offering price" or "reoffering price."

What to Enter

  • Par pricing: "100.00%" or "$100.00" — the security is sold at face value.
  • Discount pricing: "99.25%" or "$99.25" — the security is sold below par; investors receive the difference as additional yield.
  • Premium pricing: "101.50%" or "$101.50" — the security is sold above par; typical for high-coupon issues in a low-rate environment.
  • Capital appreciation bonds: Enter the initial accreted value (e.g., "$45.00" for a bond that accretes to $100.00 at maturity).
  • Unit securities: Enter the combined offering price of the unit (debt + equity/warrant).

DTC Processing

DTC uses the initial offering price to establish a baseline market value for the security in its systems. This value is posted to Participants' accounts and serves as the starting point for:

  • Position valuation: Participants use this to report holdings values to beneficial owners.
  • Margin calculations: Clearing firms use DTC's posted values for margin and collateral purposes.
  • Corporate action processing: Exchange ratios and proration calculations may reference the offering price.

If no offering price is provided, DTC will post a price of $0.00 until the underwriter provides an initial indicative bid price.

Underwriter Obligation

Per DTC's Operational Arrangements, underwriters should provide DTC with an initial indicative bid price in U.S. dollars prior to the Closing Date. This is separate from the offering price but serves a similar function for secondary market tracking.

Formatting: Use percentage of par (preferred) or dollar price per $100 par. Be consistent across all CUSIPs in the same offering. Include two decimal places (e.g., "99.50" not "99.5").
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.A.5 — Market Values; DTC Fee Schedule. dtcc.com
