Overview — Securities as Collateral & UCC Article 8

When a borrower pledges securities as collateral for a loan, the lender needs a legally enforceable right to those assets. In the U.S., that right is governed by Uniform Commercial Code Article 8 — the section of the UCC that deals with investment securities. Article 8 establishes a framework for perfection of security interests in securities, built around the concept of "control."

Unlike tangible collateral (e.g., a car or equipment), where a lender perfects by filing a UCC-1 financing statement, securities are intangible assets held in book-entry form through intermediaries like DTC and its nominee Cede & Co. The key question for a secured lender is: how do you prove your right to an asset that sits in a DTC-linked account, when the only registered owner on the issuer's books is Cede & Co.? The answer lies in Article 8's "control" concept and the tri-party Account Control Agreement (ACA).

Borrower / Pledgor
Owner of the Securities
The party who owns the securities and grants a security interest to the lender. The borrower retains beneficial ownership but grants the lender a secured claim against the assets. In a margin loan, the borrower is typically an investor or a fund.
Lender / Secured Party
Provider of Credit
The bank, broker-dealer, or other financial institution that extends credit against the pledged securities. The lender's rights are perfected through control (an ACA) or, in limited cases, by filing a UCC-1 financing statement.
Securities Intermediary
Custodian / DTC Participant
The financial institution (bank, broker-dealer) that holds the securities in its DTC participant account on behalf of the borrower. The intermediary is the critical third party in an Account Control Agreement.
Cede & Co.
Registered Owner (Issuer Books)
DTC's nominee partnership. On the issuer's books, Cede & Co. is the sole registered owner. The chain of ownership runs from Cede → DTC → participant → borrower → secured party (through the ACA).

Perfection of Security Interests — Control vs. Filing

Under UCC Article 8, a security interest in securities can be perfected in two ways: by control (the default and preferred method) or, in limited circumstances, by filing a UCC-1 financing statement. The distinction is critical because the two methods have vastly different priority rules and practical implications.

Perfection by Control
UCC Article 8 — § 8-106
The secured party obtains "control" over the securities account. This is achieved through an Account Control Agreement (ACA) signed by the borrower, the secured party, and the securities intermediary. Control is the gold standard for securities collateral: it provides automatic priority over most other claimants and requires no filing.
Perfection by Filing
UCC Article 9 — § 9-312(a)
The secured party files a UCC-1 financing statement in the borrower's jurisdiction. Filing is a permissive method of perfection available for any investment property — certificated securities, uncertificated securities, security entitlements, and securities accounts alike. It is legally valid on its own, but under § 9-328 it is always subordinate to a secured party that later perfects by control, so it is rarely relied on as the sole method for book-entry collateral.
Priority
UCC Article 9 — § 9-328(a)
A secured party with control has priority over a secured party that merely filed a UCC-1 — regardless of which party acted first in time. This "control priority" rule is the central organizing principle for investment property under Article 9, and the key reason why lenders insist on ACAs for book-entry collateral rather than relying on a filing.
Automatic Perfection
UCC § 9-309(10)–(11)
A narrow category of security interests is automatically perfected without filing or control — specifically, a security interest that a broker, securities intermediary, or commodity intermediary creates in its own favor against a customer's account (for example, to secure a margin loan). This does not apply to a third-party lender's interest; for most commercial lending, an ACA is the standard and safest route.
Method How It Works Priority Practical Use
Control (ACA) Tri-party agreement among borrower, lender, and intermediary. The intermediary agrees to follow the lender's instructions without further consent from the borrower. Highest priority — beats filing parties and most other claimants. Standard for book-entry securities held at a broker-dealer or bank custody account.
Filing (UCC-1) Financing statement filed with the Secretary of State in the borrower's jurisdiction. Available under § 9-312(a) for any investment property, not just certificated securities. Always subordinate to a later-perfected control party under § 9-328(a), regardless of filing date. Used for certificated securities held outside DTC, non-DTC-eligible securities, or as a protective "backup" filing alongside an ACA.
Automatic Perfection A broker, securities intermediary, or commodity intermediary's own security interest against a customer's securities account (e.g., to secure a margin loan) under § 9-309(10)–(11). Perfected automatically, but still ranks below a conflicting control party under § 9-328. Broker-dealer margin lending only; not available to a third-party institutional lender.
Diagram — The § 9-328(a) Priority Waterfall

Priority ladder for conflicting security interests in the same investment property under UCC § 9-328(a). Rank one: a secured party with control, such as through an Account Control Agreement, always outranks every other claimant, regardless of timing. Rank two: a secured party perfected only by filing a UCC-1 is subordinate to any control party, but still outranks general unsecured creditors and a bankruptcy trustee. Rank three: an unperfected security interest offers no priority protection and is typically defeated by a bankruptcy trustee and by later perfected secured parties.

A secured party with control always wins under § 9-328(a) — timing never matters against it. This is why an ACA, not a UCC-1 filing, is the standard for book-entry securities collateral.

Why Control Matters: Control is the most important concept in Article 8 secured transactions. A lender with control does not need to file a UCC-1, does not need to worry about the borrower filing bankruptcy (the security interest is effective against the bankruptcy trustee), and has priority over virtually all other claimants. Control is achieved through an Account Control Agreement — a tri-party contract that gives the lender direct rights against the securities intermediary.
Filing Trap: Relying solely on a UCC-1 filing for book-entry securities is risky. Filing does not give the secured party "control" as defined in UCC § 8-106. Under the priority rule in § 9-328(a), a secured party with control always outranks one who merely filed — so if another lender later obtains an ACA with the same intermediary, that later lender will have priority, even though the first lender filed first. For book-entry securities, always use an ACA.

Account Control Agreements vs. UCC-1 — When to Use Which

The choice between an Account Control Agreement (ACA) and a UCC-1 financing statement depends on the nature of the collateral and the practical realities of the lending relationship.

Account Control Agreements (ACAs)

An ACA is a tri-party agreement among (i) the borrower/pledgor, (ii) the lender/secured party, and (iii) the securities intermediary (e.g., the broker-dealer or bank custody department where the securities are held). The ACA gives the lender the right to:

  • Direct the intermediary to transfer the securities to the lender (or to a third party) upon the occurrence of a default, without further consent from the borrower.
  • Receive all distributions (dividends, interest) from the securities directly, or to direct the intermediary to sweep such payments.
  • Exercise voting rights — though this is typically not a focus of ACAs, the lender may require the intermediary to follow its instructions on corporate actions.
  • Control the account in a manner that perfects the security interest under UCC § 8-106, giving the lender priority over other claimants.

The ACA is the only way to achieve "control" over a securities account held through a DTC participant. Without an ACA, the lender is relying on a UCC-1 filing, which as discussed above is subordinate to any later control party.

Key ACA Clause — "Control" Language: To achieve control under UCC § 8-106, the ACA must contain language that the intermediary agrees to comply with the lender's entitlement orders concerning the securities without further consent from the borrower. The exact language is critical — the intermediary must affirmatively agree to follow the lender's instructions, not merely to "notify" the lender of instructions.

UCC-1 Financing Statements

A UCC-1 financing statement is the traditional method of perfecting a security interest in tangible personal property, and under § 9-312(a) it is also a legally permissible way to perfect a security interest in any investment property — certificated, uncertificated, or held through an intermediary. In practice, though, filing is subordinate to control (§ 9-328(a)), so it is mainly used in a narrower set of situations:

  • Certificated securities — where the borrower holds physical certificates that are not deposited with DTC. Here a lender will usually prefer to take possession of the certificates (which achieves control) but may file a UCC-1 as an alternative or supplement.
  • Non-DTC securities — private company securities that are not DTC-eligible and are held in certificate form.
  • Uncertificated securities with no intermediary — where the issuer itself (not a broker or bank) maintains the ownership records directly. An ACA isn't possible because there is no intermediary to sign one, so filing (or registering the lender as owner, which achieves control) is the practical route.
  • Backup perfection — some lenders file a UCC-1 in addition to obtaining an ACA, as an extra layer of protection in case the ACA is later challenged or the intermediary fails to honor it.
Best Practice: For DTC-eligible securities held in a custody account, always obtain an ACA. Consider filing a UCC-1 as a backup (sometimes called a "protective filing") in case the intermediary fails to honor the ACA or in case of intermediary insolvency. The cost of a UCC-1 filing is negligible compared to the risk of losing priority.

Rehypothecation & Margin Lending

Rehypothecation is the practice whereby a prime broker or lender re-uses collateral posted by a client to finance its own activities or to back its own borrowings. In the context of margin lending, this is a feature of the broker-dealer model — and a significant source of systemic risk.

When an investor borrows on margin, the securities in the margin account are pledged as collateral to the broker-dealer. The broker-dealer (as the secured party) may then re-pledge those securities to another institution (e.g., to a bank for a repo loan) or use them to cover its own short positions. This re-hypothecation is permitted under the broker's customer agreement, subject to the quantitative limits and customer-asset segregation rules of SEC Rule 15c3-3 and the margin framework of Regulation T.

Primary Pledge
Customer → Broker
The customer grants a security interest in the securities in their margin account to the broker-dealer. This is the first-level pledge that secures the customer's margin loan.
Rehypothecation
Broker → Third Party
The broker-dealer re-pledges the customer's securities to a bank, clearing house, or other counterparty to secure its own borrowings or to finance its proprietary trading. The customer's securities are "re-used."
Haircut / Margin
Collateral Discount
The discount applied to the value of the collateral. Under Regulation T, the initial margin requirement is 50% (i.e., a 50% haircut). For institutional lending, haircuts may vary from 2% (for Treasuries) to 50%+ for illiquid equities.
Risk of Rehypothecation
Counterparty & Systemic Risk
If the broker-dealer fails, the re-hypothecated securities may be tied up with the broker's other creditors, making it difficult or impossible for the customer to recover their collateral. This was a central feature of the Lehman Brothers bankruptcy.
Lehman Brothers Lesson: In the 2008 financial crisis, Lehman Brothers had re-hypothecated client assets multiple times. When Lehman failed, hedge funds and other clients found their collateral tied up in bankruptcy proceedings — some recovered only a fraction of their assets. The episode led to regulatory reforms, including enhanced disclosure requirements under Rule 15c3-3 (the "Customer Protection Rule") and limits on rehypothecation for certain types of accounts.
Regulation T & Margin: Federal Reserve Regulation T limits the amount of credit a broker-dealer can extend to a customer for the purchase of securities. The initial margin requirement is 50% — meaning the customer must put up at least 50% of the purchase price. Maintenance margin requirements (typically 25–30%) are set by the broker-dealer and the exchanges. If the account's equity falls below the maintenance requirement, the broker issues a margin call.

Perfection
The process by which a secured party establishes the priority of its security interest against other creditors. For securities, perfection is achieved by control (via an ACA) or, in limited cases, by filing a UCC-1 financing statement.
UCC § 9-308; § 8-106
Account Control Agreement (ACA)
A tri-party agreement among the borrower, lender, and securities intermediary that gives the lender "control" over the securities account under UCC § 8-106. The ACA perfects the lender's security interest and provides priority over other claimants.
UCC § 8-106; § 9-106
Rehypothecation
The practice of a prime broker or lender re-using collateral posted by a client to finance its own activities or to back its own borrowings. Permitted under Regulation T but subject to limits and disclosure requirements.
SEC Rule 15c3-3; Regulation T
Entitlement Holder
The person (or entity) identified on the records of a securities intermediary as having a security entitlement against the intermediary. In the collateral context, the borrower is the entitlement holder with respect to the securities account.
UCC § 8-102(a)(7)
Securities Intermediary
A person (typically a bank, broker-dealer, or clearing corporation) that maintains securities accounts for others. The intermediary is a DTC participant and holds positions in book-entry form. The ACA is signed by the intermediary.
UCC § 8-102(a)(14)
Control (UCC § 8-106)
The legal concept under UCC Article 8 that gives a secured party priority over other claimants. Control over a securities account is achieved through an ACA, by the secured party being the entitlement holder, or by the secured party taking possession of a certificated security.
UCC § 8-106

UCC Article 8 — Investment Securities

Uniform Commercial Code Article
Article 8

The section of the Uniform Commercial Code that governs investment securities — including the creation, transfer, and perfection of security interests in securities. Article 8 establishes the framework for "security entitlements" held through intermediaries and introduces the concept of "control" as the primary method of perfection for book-entry securities.

Key Provisions

  • § 8-102: Definitions — including "security entitlement," "entitlement holder," and "securities intermediary."
  • § 8-106: Control — defines when a person has control of a securities account.
  • § 8-501: Security entitlements — the rights of an entitlement holder against a securities intermediary.
  • § 8-504: Duties of securities intermediaries — including the duty to maintain records and follow entitlement holder instructions.
Source: Uniform Commercial Code, Article 8 (Investment Securities), as adopted in all U.S. states.

Perfection of Security Interests

Legal Process
Perfection

The process by which a secured party establishes the priority of its security interest against other creditors (including bankruptcy trustees and other secured parties). Perfection is what makes a security interest enforceable against third parties, not just between the borrower and lender.

Methods of Perfection for Securities

  • Control (UCC § 8-106): The secured party obtains "control" over the securities account through an Account Control Agreement, by being the entitlement holder, or by taking possession of a certificated security. This is the primary method for book-entry securities.
  • Filing (UCC Article 9): The secured party files a UCC-1 financing statement in the borrower's jurisdiction. This is legally available for any investment property under § 9-312(a), but it is always subordinate to control under § 9-328(a).
  • Automatic Perfection: A narrow set of security interests is perfected automatically upon attachment — specifically, a broker's, securities intermediary's, or commodity intermediary's own security interest against a customer's account (e.g., a margin loan) under § 9-309(10)–(11).
See also: Control, UCC-1, Account Control Agreement
Source: UCC § 9-308 (Perfection); § 8-106 (Control); § 9-328 (Priority).

Control — UCC § 8-106

Legal Concept
Control

The legal concept under UCC Article 8 that gives a secured party priority over other claimants. Control is the gold standard for perfection of a security interest in a securities account. Under § 8-106, a secured party has control if the securities intermediary agrees to follow the secured party's instructions without further consent from the entitlement holder (the borrower).

Three Ways to Achieve Control

  • Account Control Agreement (ACA): The intermediary agrees to follow the secured party's instructions concerning the securities without further consent.
  • Secured Party as Entitlement Holder: The secured party itself is the entitlement holder on the intermediary's books.
  • Possession of Certificated Security: The secured party takes possession of the physical certificate (for certificated securities).
Source: UCC § 8-106; Official Comment 1 to § 8-106.

Account Control Agreement (ACA)

Tri-Party Contract
Account Control Agreement

A tri-party agreement among (i) the borrower/pledgor, (ii) the lender/secured party, and (iii) the securities intermediary (e.g., the broker-dealer or bank where the securities are held). The ACA gives the lender "control" over the securities account under UCC § 8-106, perfecting the security interest and providing priority over other claimants.

Key ACA Provisions

  • Control language: The intermediary agrees to comply with the lender's entitlement orders without further consent from the borrower.
  • Distribution direction: The intermediary agrees to direct dividends, interest, and other distributions to the lender (or to the lender's designee) upon default.
  • Notice and cure: The borrower is typically given notice of default and a short cure period before the lender can exercise control.
  • Indemnity: The borrower often indemnifies the intermediary for following the lender's instructions.
Source: UCC § 8-106; UCC § 9-106; ISDA/Cleary Gottlieb ACA templates.

UCC-1 Financing Statement

Filing
UCC-1 Financing Statement

A public notice filed with the Secretary of State (or other designated office) that gives notice of a secured party's security interest in a debtor's collateral. Under § 9-312(a), filing is a legally permissible way to perfect a security interest in any investment property — but it is always subordinate to a party that perfects by control (§ 9-328(a)).

When Filing Is Practically Used for Securities

  • Certificated securities: When the borrower holds physical certificates that are not deposited with DTC.
  • Non-DTC securities: For private company securities that are not eligible for the DTC system.
  • Backup perfection: Some lenders file a UCC-1 in addition to obtaining an ACA, as an extra layer of protection.
Warning: Filing a UCC-1 does not give the secured party "control" as defined in UCC § 8-106. Under § 9-328(a), a later lender who obtains an ACA will have priority over an earlier UCC-1 filer. Always use an ACA for book-entry securities.
Source: UCC § 9-312(a) (Perfection by Filing); UCC § 9-328(a) (Priority of Security Interests in Investment Property).

Rehypothecation

Financial Practice
Rehypothecation

The practice whereby a prime broker or lender re-uses collateral posted by a client to finance its own activities or to back its own borrowings. The re-pledged collateral is used as security for the broker's own obligations — for example, in a repo transaction or to cover a short position. Rehypothecation is permitted under SEC Regulation T and the customer agreement, subject to limits.

Key Considerations

  • Regulation T: Limits the amount of credit a broker-dealer can extend and sets initial margin requirements.
  • Rule 15c3-3: The Customer Protection Rule requires broker-dealers to maintain custody of customer securities and limits rehypothecation of fully paid-for securities.
  • Counterparty risk: If the broker-dealer fails, re-hypothecated securities may be tied up with the broker's other creditors (as seen in the Lehman Brothers bankruptcy).
  • Disclosure: Broker-dealers are required to disclose their rehypothecation practices in customer agreements.
Source: SEC Rule 15c3-3; Federal Reserve Regulation T; Lehman Brothers bankruptcy proceedings.

Securities Intermediary

Financial Institution
Securities Intermediary

A person (typically a bank, broker-dealer, or clearing corporation) that maintains securities accounts for others and holds securities in book-entry form through the DTC system. The intermediary is a DTC participant and is the party that signs the Account Control Agreement (ACA) to give the lender "control" over the securities account.

Duties of a Securities Intermediary

  • Maintain records: Keep accurate records of each entitlement holder's positions.
  • Follow instructions: Comply with entitlement holder instructions (and, under an ACA, with the secured party's instructions upon default).
  • Pass through distributions: Collect dividends, interest, and other distributions and credit them to the appropriate accounts.
  • Corporate actions: Process tender offers, mergers, and other corporate actions as directed by the entitlement holder or the secured party.
Source: UCC § 8-102(a)(14); UCC § 8-504 (Duties of Securities Intermediary).

Borrower / Pledgor

Collateral Owner
Borrower / Pledgor

The party that owns the securities and grants a security interest in them to the lender. The borrower retains beneficial ownership of the securities but grants the lender a secured claim against the assets. In a margin loan, the borrower is typically an investor or a fund.

Rights and Obligations

  • Right to income: The borrower typically continues to receive dividends and interest from the securities unless the ACA directs otherwise upon default.
  • Voting rights: The borrower usually retains the right to vote the securities, unless the ACA provides otherwise.
  • Obligation to maintain collateral: The borrower must maintain sufficient collateral value to meet margin requirements (maintenance margin).
  • Default consequences: Upon default, the lender may direct the intermediary to transfer the securities to the lender or to a third party.
Source: UCC § 9-102(a)(28) (Debtor); Regulation T; customary margin agreements.

Lender / Secured Party

Creditor
Lender / Secured Party

The bank, broker-dealer, or other financial institution that extends credit against the pledged securities. The lender's rights are perfected through control (an ACA) or, in limited cases, by filing a UCC-1 financing statement. The lender has a secured claim against the securities that is enforceable upon default.

Lender's Rights and Remedies

  • Control: Through the ACA, the lender can direct the intermediary to transfer the securities upon default.
  • Priority: A lender with control has priority over other claimants, including other secured parties and judgment creditors.
  • Collateral valuation: The lender monitors the value of the collateral and may issue margin calls if the value declines.
  • Foreclosure: Upon default, the lender may foreclose on the collateral by taking possession of the securities or by selling them in a commercially reasonable manner.
Source: UCC § 9-102(a)(72) (Secured Party); UCC § 9-610 (Disposition of Collateral).

Depository Trust Company (DTC)

Central Securities Depository
DTC

A limited-purpose trust company and the primary central securities depository for U.S. equity and debt securities. DTC holds the majority of U.S. securities in book-entry form, eliminating the need for physical certificates. DTC is a subsidiary of DTCC and a regulated clearing agency under SEC oversight. All DTC participants hold their securities positions in DTC accounts.

Why DTC Matters for Collateral

  • Immobilization: Physical certificates are immobilized at DTC; positions are maintained in book-entry form.
  • Intermediary chain: DTC → DTC participant → borrower → secured party (through ACA).
  • Settlement: DTC provides clearing and settlement services, with T+1 settlement for most transactions.
  • Collateral eligibility: DTC-eligible securities are the primary collateral for margin lending, repo, and secured lending.
See also: Cede & Co., Securities Intermediary
Source: DTC Rules; DTCC website.

Cede & Co.

Nominee Partnership
Cede & Co.

A New York general partnership formed by DTC to serve as the registered owner of securities on the books of issuers. Cede & Co. holds legal title as nominee for DTC participants and their customers. It does not have beneficial ownership — it is purely a nominee. The beneficial ownership chain runs through DTC to the participant, to the borrower, and then to the lender (through the ACA) for collateral purposes.

Source: DTC Rules; New York Partnership Law; SEC Concept Release on the U.S. Proxy System, Release No. 34-62495 (2010).

Book-Entry Securities

Securities Form
Book-Entry

Securities that are held in electronic form rather than as physical certificates. Ownership is recorded on the books of the issuer and its transfer agent, with the vast majority of U.S. securities held in book-entry form through the DTC system. Book-entry securities are the primary collateral for margin lending and secured transactions.

Key Characteristics

  • Immobilization: Physical certificates are immobilized at DTC; no physical certificates are issued to individual owners.
  • Intermediary chain: Issuer → Cede & Co. → DTC → DTC participant → Entitlement Holder.
  • Transfer: Securities are transferred by book-entry — that is, by debiting and crediting accounts at DTC — rather than by physical delivery.
  • Collateral: Book-entry securities are pledged by means of a security entitlement — a legal interest in the securities held by the intermediary.
Source: UCC § 8-102(a)(16) (Security Entitlement); DTC Rules.
