Overview — Corporate Structures & Securities

Securities are issued by a wide range of entities, each with distinct legal characteristics. Limited Liability Companies (LLCs), C-Corporations, S-Corporations, and partnerships are the most common forms of for-profit entities that issue securities. Understanding how securities are titled, traded, and held through the DTC system is essential for corporate issuers, investors, and legal professionals.

This guide covers the securities mechanics for each entity type, from initial issuance and DTC eligibility to trading, restricted securities rules, and corporate actions. It also explores governance structures — how shareholders (or members) exercise voting rights — and the legal and operational frameworks that apply to each entity type.

C-Corporation
Traditional Corporation
A separate legal entity that issues common and preferred stock. C-corps are subject to corporate income tax (double taxation) but offer unlimited growth potential and the ability to have an unlimited number of shareholders.
S-Corporation
Pass-Through Entity
A corporation that elects pass-through taxation under Subchapter S of the IRC. Limited to 100 shareholders (individuals, estates, certain trusts), with one class of stock. Cannot be owned by non-U.S. residents.
LLC
Limited Liability Company
A flexible structure offering pass-through taxation and limited liability. LLCs issue membership interests (not stock). Can be member-managed or manager-managed. Popular for private businesses and real estate.
Partnership
Pass-Through Entity
A business structure where two or more partners share ownership. General partnerships (GP) have unlimited liability; limited partnerships (LP) have general and limited partners; limited liability partnerships (LLP) offer liability protection.
Concept Map — Entity Types & Securities

Decision tree of entity types: For-Profit Entities branch into Corporations (C-Corp, S-Corp), LLCs, and Partnerships (GP, LP, LLP). Each has different securities characteristics: C-Corp issues common/preferred stock, unlimited shareholders, double taxation; S-Corp issues one class of stock, 100 shareholder limit, pass-through; LLC issues membership interests, flexible, pass-through; Partnerships issue partnership interests, pass-through.

Entity types and their securities: C-Corps (stock, double taxation), S-Corps (stock, pass-through), LLCs (membership interests, pass-through), and Partnerships (partnership interests, pass-through).

Limited Liability Companies (LLCs)

A Limited Liability Company (LLC) is a flexible business structure that combines the limited liability of a corporation with the pass-through taxation of a partnership. LLCs are governed by state law (typically the LLC Act of the state of formation) and are popular for small businesses, real estate investment, and as holding vehicles for family assets.

LLCs issue membership interests rather than "stock" or "shares." These interests represent the economic and governance rights of the members. LLCs can be member-managed (where all members participate in management) or manager-managed (where designated managers run the business).

Securities Ownership in LLCs

For DTC purposes, LLC membership interests can be held in book-entry form if the LLC meets DTC eligibility requirements. However, LLCs face additional challenges:

  • Transfer Restrictions: Many LLC operating agreements restrict the transfer of membership interests, which can limit trading and DTC eligibility.
  • Tax Status: LLCs are typically pass-through entities, which may limit the pool of eligible investors (especially tax-exempt entities and foreign investors).
  • Securities Law: LLC interests are securities under federal and state law, subject to registration or exemption requirements.
Concept Map — LLC Structure & Securities

LLC structure: Members (owners) hold membership interests. They execute an Operating Agreement governing rights and management. The LLC can be member-managed or manager-managed. The LLC holds assets and issues securities. Securities can be held at DTC if eligible. Cede and Co. is the registered owner on the transfer agent's books.

LLC structure: Members own interests governed by an operating agreement; management can be by members or designated managers; securities are held through DTC.

Corporations — C-Corp vs S-Corp

Corporations are the most common form of entity for public companies. A C-Corporation is a separate legal entity subject to corporate income tax (the "double taxation" problem). An S-Corporation is a corporation that elects pass-through taxation under Subchapter S of the Internal Revenue Code, avoiding double taxation.

Concept Map — Corporate Structure & Securities

Corporate structure: Shareholders own stock and elect a Board of Directors. The Board oversees Officers (CEO, CFO, etc.) who manage the corporation. The corporation issues common and preferred stock. Preferred stock often has special rights (dividend preference, liquidation preference). Securities are held at DTC with Cede and Co. as registered owner.

Corporate structure: Shareholders elect the board; the board oversees officers who run the corporation. The corporation issues common and preferred stock held in DTC.

Entity Choice Tip: For a business planning to raise capital from venture capital or go public, a C-Corporation is the preferred structure. For a closely held business with a limited number of individual owners, an S-Corporation or LLC may be more tax-efficient.

Partnerships

A partnership is a business entity where two or more partners share ownership and management. The three main types are:

  • General Partnership (GP): All partners have unlimited liability and participate in management.
  • Limited Partnership (LP): Has one or more general partners (unlimited liability, management) and limited partners (liability limited to their investment, no management).
  • Limited Liability Partnership (LLP): All partners have limited liability; often used by professional services firms (law, accounting).

Partnerships issue partnership interests to their partners. These interests represent economic and governance rights. Publicly traded partnerships — known as Master Limited Partnerships (MLPs) — are a special category of publicly traded partnerships that are often DTC-eligible.

Passive Activity Loss Limitations: Partnership interests are subject to complex tax rules, including passive activity loss limitations and at-risk rules. Investors in partnerships should consult with tax advisors before investing.

Securities Issuance & Registration

The process of issuing securities involves the creation of the security, registration (or exemption from registration) under federal and state securities laws, and deposit with DTC (if eligible). The Securities Act of 1933 governs the offer and sale of securities in the United States.

Concept Map — Securities Issuance Lifecycle

Securities issuance lifecycle: Corporation (issuer) decides to issue securities. It prepares a registration statement (Form S-1, S-3, etc.) and files with SEC. SEC reviews and declares the registration effective. Securities are priced and sold in an offering (IPO, secondary). Securities are deposited with DTC via the transfer agent (FAST). Securities become DTC-eligible and are held in book-entry form.

Issuance lifecycle: From board approval to SEC registration to offering to DTC deposit and book-entry trading.

Process Map — Issuing Securities to DTC

Seven-step process: Issuer approves issuance; drafts and files registration statement with SEC; SEC declares effective; securities are priced and sold; transfer agent prepares DTC deposit; DTC credits participant accounts (underwriters); securities are distributed to investors.

Step summary (text)
1 · Approve — The issuer's board (and shareholders, if required) approves the issuance.
2 · File — The issuer drafts and files a registration statement with the SEC (Form S-1, S-3, etc.).
3 · Declare Effective — The SEC reviews and declares the registration effective.
4 · Price & Sell — The securities are priced and sold in an offering (IPO, secondary, etc.).
5 · Deposit — The transfer agent deposits the securities with DTC (via FAST or physical deposit).
6 · Trade — Securities become DTC-eligible and trade in book-entry form; Cede & Co. is registered owner.

DTC Eligibility for Corporate Securities

Not all securities are automatically eligible for DTC services. DTC eligibility is a critical operational requirement for publicly traded securities because it enables efficient clearing and settlement through the DTC system.

To become DTC-eligible, an issuer must:

  • Engage a transfer agent that participates in DTC's FAST program.
  • Execute a Letter of Representations agreeing to DTC's procedures.
  • Ensure the securities are freely tradable (or eligible under Rule 144A or Regulation S).
  • Satisfy DTC's operational requirements regarding corporate actions, dividends, and record-keeping.

For C-Corporations, DTC eligibility is typically straightforward for publicly traded securities. For S-Corps and LLCs, transfer restrictions and shareholder limitations often make DTC eligibility impractical or unnecessary.

Issuer Tip: DTC eligibility is critical for marketability. Most institutional investors require securities to be DTC-eligible; otherwise, they cannot be held in street name and trades must be settled manually — which is expensive and time-consuming.

Restricted Securities — Rule 144, 144A & Regulation S

Restricted securities are securities acquired in private transactions (not public offerings) that are subject to resale restrictions. The primary rules governing resale are Rule 144 (for affiliates and non-affiliates), Rule 144A (for qualified institutional buyers), and Regulation S (for offshore transactions).

Concept Map — Restricted Securities Framework

Restricted securities framework: Restricted securities are acquired in private transactions. Resale pathways: Rule 144 (public resale with holding periods, volume limits, manner of sale); Rule 144A (resale to QIBs in private markets); Regulation S (offshore resale for foreign investors). Public securities are freely tradable through DTC.

Restricted securities framework: Rule 144 (public resale), Rule 144A (QIB private market), and Regulation S (offshore resale).

Rule 144 — Public Resale

Rule 144 provides a safe harbor for the resale of restricted securities and securities held by affiliates. Key requirements include:

  • Holding Period: Typically 6 months (or 1 year for non-reporting companies).
  • Current Public Information: The issuer must be current in its SEC filings.
  • Volume Limitations: For affiliates, the amount sold cannot exceed certain volume limits.
  • Manner of Sale: Affiliates must sell in a manner consistent with the rule (e.g., broker transactions).
  • Filing Requirement: Affiliates must file Form 144 with the SEC.

Rule 144A — Private Resale to QIBs

Rule 144A allows the resale of restricted securities to Qualified Institutional Buyers (QIBs) without the holding periods and volume limitations of Rule 144. Rule 144A securities are typically held in DTC with a restricted CUSIP and can only be transferred to other QIBs.

Regulation S — Offshore Transactions

Regulation S provides a safe harbor for offers and sales of securities made outside the United States. It allows issuers and distributors to sell securities to foreign investors without SEC registration, provided the sale is made in an offshore transaction and no "directed selling efforts" are made in the U.S.

Restricted Securities Risk: Investors who acquire restricted securities must be aware of resale restrictions. Failure to comply with Rule 144, Rule 144A, or Regulation S can result in violations of the Securities Act of 1933, potentially leading to rescission rights and penalties.

Mergers, Reorganizations & Transfers

Corporate reorganizations — including mergers, acquisitions, spin-offs, and stock-for-stock exchanges — involve the transfer and exchange of securities. DTC plays a critical role in processing these corporate actions efficiently.

Process Map — Merger & Reorganization Processing (DTC)

Six-step merger processing: Issuer announces the merger or reorganization. DTC is notified and sets up the corporate action in its systems. Participants are notified of the election deadlines and procedures. Shareholders elect their choice (cash, stock, or combination) through brokers. DTC processes the elections and exchanges securities. New securities are credited to participants' accounts; legacy securities are retired.

Step summary (text)
1 · Announce — The issuer announces the merger, reorganization, or corporate action.
2 · Notify DTC — The issuer (via transfer agent) notifies DTC and sets up the corporate action.
3 · Election — Shareholders elect their choice (cash, stock, or combination) through their brokers.
4 · Process — DTC processes elections and exchanges securities per the transaction terms.
5 · Credit — New securities are credited to participants' accounts; legacy securities are retired.
Corporate Action Tip: DTC's ATOP (Automated Tender Offer Program) and other automated services streamline the processing of mergers, tender offers, and reorganizations. Issuers and transfer agents should ensure timely and accurate notification to DTC to avoid processing delays.

Governance & Voting Rights

For securities held in street name through DTC, voting rights are exercised through the beneficial owner proxy voting system. The issuer sends proxy materials to Cede & Co.; DTC forwards them to participants; participants forward to beneficial owners; and votes flow back through the same chain.

Concept Map — Governance & Voting Stack

Governance stack: Issuer sends proxy materials to Cede and Co. (registered owner). Cede/DTC forwards to participants. Participants forward to beneficial owners. Beneficial owners vote. Votes are aggregated and returned through participants to DTC to the issuer. The stack mirrors the ownership stack but operates in reverse for voting rights.

Governance stack: Proxy materials flow from the issuer down to the shareholder; votes flow back up through the same chain.

Corporate Transparency Act & Beneficial Ownership Information

The Corporate Transparency Act (CTA) directed the Treasury Department’s Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) to collect beneficial ownership information (BOI) for certain legal entities. That regime is easy to confuse with DTC “beneficial ownership” — they use similar words for different systems. This section separates the two and states the current federal BOI reporting posture for teaching purposes.

Current status (as of March 2025; still in force mid-2026): FinCEN’s interim final rule (announced March 21, 2025; published March 26, 2025) exempts all entities created in the United States — the former “domestic reporting companies” — and their beneficial owners from the requirement to report BOI to FinCEN. Remaining reporting obligations focus primarily on certain foreign-formed entities that have registered to do business in a U.S. state or Tribal jurisdiction. Always verify the latest FinCEN guidance before relying on this summary; a final rule or further amendments could change the scope.

Two meanings of “beneficial ownership”

In this teaching suite, beneficial owner usually means the economic owner of a security held in street name through DTC / Cede & Co. — the investor who receives dividends, proxy rights, and sale proceeds even though the registered owner on the issuer’s books is the nominee. CTA / FinCEN BOI uses “beneficial owner” for natural persons who own or control a legal entity (for example, an LLC or corporation). Confusing the two leads students to assume every small LLC must file a FinCEN report simply because it issues membership interests or holds DTC-eligible securities. That is not how the current federal BOI rule works.

DTC beneficial ownership
Securities holding chain
Who enjoys the economic rights in a book-entry security. Legal title sits with Cede & Co.; beneficial ownership is recorded on participant and customer books.
CTA / FinCEN BOI
Entity control reporting
Who owns or controls a reporting company as a legal entity. Separate from securities settlement infrastructure and not required for U.S.-created entities under the March 2025 interim final rule.

What the CTA was designed to do (2021–2024)

Congress enacted the CTA as part of the National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2021. Implementing regulations took effect for many companies on January 1, 2024, with phased deadlines for existing entities. In its original regulatory design, millions of domestic LLCs, corporations, and similar entities were expected to file BOI reports with FinCEN identifying natural-person beneficial owners (and, in some cases, company applicants), subject to a long list of statutory exemptions (for example, large operating companies, certain highly regulated entities, and tax-exempt organizations).

That 2024 design is important historical context — and it is what many older secondary sources still describe. It is not the operative federal rule for domestic companies after FinCEN’s March 2025 interim final rule.

What applies now (March 2025 interim final rule)

  • U.S.-created entities: Entities formed under U.S. state or Tribal law (former “domestic reporting companies”) and their beneficial owners are exempt from BOI reporting to FinCEN under the interim final rule.
  • Foreign reporting companies: FinCEN revised the regulatory definition of “reporting company” to mean entities formed under foreign law that have registered to do business in any U.S. state or Tribal jurisdiction by filing with a secretary of state (or similar office), and that do not qualify for an exemption.
  • U.S. persons as beneficial owners: Under the interim final rule framework, reporting companies are not required to report U.S. persons as beneficial owners, and U.S. persons are not required to provide BOI for entities for which they are beneficial owners.
  • Enforcement posture: FinCEN has stated it will not enforce BOI penalties against U.S. citizens or domestic reporting companies (or their beneficial owners) consistent with the interim final rule and related Treasury announcements.

Deadlines for remaining foreign reporting companies were adjusted when the interim final rule took effect (for example, entities registered to do business in the United States before March 26, 2025, had an April 25, 2025 filing deadline under FinCEN’s public guidance). Those deadlines matter only for entities that still meet the narrowed “reporting company” definition.

Practical takeaways for corporate structures students

  • Do not treat 2024 “file or face daily penalties” guidance as current for ordinary U.S. LLCs and corporations. That framing is outdated under the interim final rule.
  • Entity choice (LLC vs C-Corp vs S-Corp) still drives tax, governance, and securities issuance analysis in this guide — CTA BOI status is a separate compliance overlay that currently has limited impact on domestic entities.
  • Cross-border formations remain the main residual BOI risk area: foreign-formed entities that register to do business in the U.S. may still have filing obligations; counsel should check FinCEN’s current definition, exemptions, and deadlines.
  • Watch for a final rule. FinCEN indicated intent to finalize after notice-and-comment; publication timing and ultimate scope can change. Date-stamp any advice and re-check fincen.gov/boi.
  • State law is separate. Some states maintain their own beneficial-ownership or transparency regimes. Federal CTA exemption does not automatically erase state filing or disclosure rules.
Educational disclaimer: This section summarizes publicly announced FinCEN positions for teaching. It is not legal advice, not a determination of any entity’s filing status, and not a substitute for reviewing the interim final rule text, FinCEN FAQs, or qualified counsel. Rules and enforcement priorities can change.

For-Profit Entity

Business Organization
For-Profit Entity

A legal organization formed to generate profit for its owners. Common types include corporations (C-Corp, S-Corp), LLCs, and partnerships. Each has distinct tax, liability, and securities characteristics.

Key characteristics: For-profit entities are the primary issuers of securities in the capital markets. They are distinguished from non-profit entities by their ability to distribute profits to owners and by the existence of ownership interests that can be bought, sold, and traded.

Tax treatment: For-profit entities may be taxed as C-Corps (double taxation), S-Corps or LLCs (pass-through taxation), or partnerships (pass-through). The choice of entity has significant tax implications for both the entity and its owners.

Source: IRS; state business entity statutes.

Limited Liability Company (LLC)

Business Structure
LLC

A flexible business structure that provides limited liability to its owners (members) and pass-through taxation. LLCs are governed by state law and an operating agreement. They issue membership interests rather than stock.

Key features: LLCs combine the limited liability of a corporation with the pass-through taxation of a partnership. They are governed by an operating agreement that sets forth management, profit distribution, and transfer rights. LLCs can be member-managed or manager-managed.

Securities aspects: LLC membership interests are securities under federal and state law. For DTC eligibility, LLCs must have a transfer agent that participates in FAST, and their operating agreements must permit transfers of interests. S-Corps and LLCs are generally not DTC-eligible unless they meet strict requirements.

Use cases: Real estate investment vehicles, family holding companies, small to medium businesses, professional services firms (as PLLCs), and private equity fund structures.

Source: IRS; state LLC statutes; Uniform LLC Act.

C-Corporation

Corporation Type
C-Corporation

A corporation taxed under Subchapter C of the Internal Revenue Code. C-Corps are subject to corporate income tax (double taxation) and can have an unlimited number of shareholders, multiple classes of stock, and non-U.S. residents.

Key features: C-Corps are the most common structure for public companies. They have a board of directors elected by shareholders, who appoint officers to manage day-to-day operations. C-Corps issue common and preferred stock. They are subject to corporate income tax at the entity level, and shareholders pay tax on dividends received (double taxation).

DTC eligibility: Publicly traded C-Corps are typically DTC-eligible through their transfer agents. They execute a Letter of Representations with DTC and participate in the FAST program for electronic book-entry transfers.

Use cases: Public companies, high-growth startups seeking venture capital, companies planning to go public, and multinational corporations.

Source: IRC § 11; state corporate statutes; Model Business Corporation Act.

S-Corporation

Corporation Type
S-Corporation

A corporation that elects pass-through taxation under Subchapter S of the Internal Revenue Code. S-Corps are limited to 100 shareholders (individuals, estates, certain trusts) and cannot have non-U.S. residents. They can have only one class of stock.

Key features: S-Corps are pass-through entities, meaning income and losses flow through to shareholders' personal tax returns. They avoid the double taxation of C-Corps but face strict eligibility requirements. S-Corps are limited to 100 shareholders, all of whom must be U.S. citizens or residents. They can only issue one class of stock.

DTC eligibility: S-Corps are typically not DTC-eligible because their transfer restrictions and shareholder limitations make them unsuitable for public trading. Most S-Corps are closely held and do not seek DTC eligibility.

Use cases: Small to medium businesses, family-owned enterprises, and professional services firms that want to avoid double taxation while maintaining corporate limited liability.

Source: IRC §§ 1361-1379; Treas. Reg. § 1.1361-1.

Partnership

Business Structure
Partnership

A business entity where two or more partners share ownership and management. Types include General Partnerships (GP), Limited Partnerships (LP), and Limited Liability Partnerships (LLP). Partnerships are pass-through entities.

Key features: Partnerships are pass-through entities — income and losses flow through to partners' personal tax returns. GPs have unlimited liability for all partners; LPs have general partners (unlimited liability, management) and limited partners (liability limited to their investment, no management); LLPs offer limited liability to all partners.

Securities aspects: Partnership interests are securities under federal and state law. Publicly traded partnerships (MLPs) are DTC-eligible. Private partnership interests are typically not DTC-eligible due to transfer restrictions and illiquidity.

Use cases: GP: small businesses; LP: real estate and private equity; LLP: law firms, accounting firms; MLP: energy, infrastructure, and natural resources.

Source: IRC §§ 701-777; Uniform Partnership Act; Uniform Limited Partnership Act.

Corporation

Business Structure
Corporation

A separate legal entity owned by shareholders. Corporations have a board of directors that oversees officers who manage the business. They issue common and preferred stock and are governed by state corporate law.

Key features: Corporations are the dominant form of business organization for large enterprises. They offer limited liability to shareholders, perpetual existence, and centralized management through a board of directors and officers. Corporations can raise capital by issuing stock and debt securities.

DTC eligibility: Publicly traded corporations are the primary users of DTC services. They typically have transfer agents that participate in FAST and execute Letters of Representations with DTC. Corporate securities are held in book-entry form at DTC.

Use cases: Large enterprises, public companies, multinational corporations, and entities seeking to raise capital through public markets.

Source: Model Business Corporation Act; state corporate statutes.

Shareholder

Owner
Shareholder

A person or entity that owns shares of stock in a corporation. Shareholders have voting rights, receive dividends, and have a residual claim on the corporation's assets after debts are paid.

Rights and responsibilities: Shareholders have the right to vote on major corporate matters (e.g., electing directors, approving mergers), receive dividends when declared, and inspect corporate records. They are not personally liable for the corporation's debts.

Types of shareholders: Institutional shareholders (mutual funds, pension funds, hedge funds), individual retail investors, and insiders (officers, directors, and employees). Institutional shareholders often hold significant voting power and influence corporate governance.

Source: Model Business Corporation Act; state corporate statutes.

Board of Directors

Governance
Board of Directors

A group of individuals elected by shareholders to oversee the management of a corporation. The board sets corporate strategy, appoints officers, and ensures the corporation operates in the best interests of shareholders.

Key responsibilities: The board has a fiduciary duty to act in the best interests of shareholders. Responsibilities include setting corporate strategy, appointing and overseeing the CEO, approving major transactions (e.g., mergers, acquisitions), and ensuring compliance with laws and regulations.

Governance structure: Boards have committees (audit, compensation, nominating) to focus on specific areas. Directors are elected annually or staggered terms. Independent directors are required for public companies under listing standards.

Source: Model Business Corporation Act; NYSE and NASDAQ listing standards.

Officer

Management
Officer

An individual appointed by the board of directors to manage the day-to-day operations of a corporation. Key officers include CEO, CFO, COO, and Secretary. Officers are fiduciaries of the corporation and its shareholders.

Key roles: CEO: chief executive responsible for overall operations; CFO: chief financial officer responsible for financial management and reporting; COO: chief operating officer responsible for daily operations; Secretary: maintains corporate records and minutes of meetings.

Fiduciary duties: Officers owe duties of care and loyalty to the corporation and its shareholders. They must act in good faith, with the care of a prudent person, and in the best interests of the corporation.

Source: Model Business Corporation Act.

Membership Interest

LLC Security
Membership Interest

The ownership interest in a limited liability company (LLC). It represents the member's economic and governance rights. Unlike stock, membership interests are governed by the LLC's operating agreement.

Economic rights: Members have the right to receive distributions of profits, allocations of income and losses, and a share of the LLC's assets upon dissolution. These rights are defined in the operating agreement.

Governance rights: Members may have voting rights on major decisions (e.g., amending the operating agreement, admitting new members, dissolving the LLC). The extent of voting rights depends on whether the LLC is member-managed or manager-managed.

Transferability: Membership interests are typically subject to transfer restrictions in the operating agreement. Some agreements require consent of other members or the manager before a transfer can occur. DTC eligibility for LLC interests depends on the operating agreement permitting transfers and the LLC having a transfer agent that participates in FAST.

Source: Uniform LLC Act; state LLC statutes.

Operating Agreement

LLC Governance
Operating Agreement

The governing document of a limited liability company (LLC). It sets forth the rights and obligations of the members, management structure, profit distribution, and transfer restrictions on membership interests.

Key provisions: The operating agreement covers member capital contributions, profit and loss allocations, distributions, management structure (member-managed or manager-managed), voting rights, transfer restrictions, and dissolution procedures. It is the primary source of governance for LLCs.

Legal significance: The operating agreement is a contract among the members and the LLC. It governs the internal affairs of the LLC and can override default provisions of state LLC statutes. Courts enforce operating agreements as contracts.

Importance for DTC eligibility: For an LLC's membership interests to be DTC-eligible, the operating agreement must permit transfers (or have a mechanism for approval) and the LLC must have a transfer agent that participates in FAST.

Source: Uniform LLC Act; state LLC statutes.

Member-Managed LLC

LLC Management
Member-Managed

An LLC structure where all members participate in the management of the business. This is the default for many LLCs and is common in smaller businesses where all members are actively involved.

Key features: In a member-managed LLC, each member has the authority to bind the LLC in the ordinary course of business. Decisions are made by majority vote (or as specified in the operating agreement). This structure is simpler and more direct than manager-management.

Use cases: Small businesses, family-owned LLCs, real estate investment LLCs with a small number of active members, and any LLC where all members want to participate in management.

Source: Uniform LLC Act.

Manager-Managed LLC

LLC Management
Manager-Managed

An LLC structure where designated managers (who may or may not be members) run the business. This is common in larger LLCs or where some members are passive investors.

Key features: In a manager-managed LLC, the managers (who can be members or non-members) have exclusive authority to manage the LLC's affairs. Members who are not managers do not participate in day-to-day management and have limited authority to bind the LLC. This structure allows passive investors to invest without taking on management responsibilities.

Use cases: Larger LLCs with multiple investors, real estate syndications, private equity and venture capital fund structures, and businesses where some members are passive investors who want limited liability without management duties.

Source: Uniform LLC Act.

Member

LLC Owner
Member

An owner of a limited liability company (LLC). Members hold membership interests and have rights to profits, losses, and distributions as set forth in the operating agreement.

Rights and responsibilities: Members have economic rights (distributions, profit allocations) and governance rights (voting on major decisions). The extent of governance rights depends on whether the LLC is member-managed or manager-managed. Members are not personally liable for the LLC's debts beyond their capital contributions.

Types of members: Individual investors, institutions (banks, pension funds, investment funds), family offices, and other LLCs or corporations. Membership can be held indirectly through holding companies or trusts.

Source: Uniform LLC Act; state LLC statutes.

Partnership Interest

Partnership Security
Partnership Interest

The ownership interest in a partnership. It represents the partner's economic rights (profits, losses, distributions) and governance rights (voting, management). Partnership interests are governed by the partnership agreement.

Economic rights: Partners have the right to receive allocations of income and losses, distributions of cash and property, and a share of partnership assets upon dissolution. These rights are defined in the partnership agreement.

Governance rights: Partners may have voting rights on major decisions (e.g., admitting new partners, dissolving the partnership, amending the partnership agreement). The extent of voting rights depends on the type of partnership (GP, LP, LLP) and the partnership agreement.

Transferability: Partnership interests are typically subject to transfer restrictions in the partnership agreement. Some agreements require consent of other partners before a transfer can occur. Publicly traded partnership interests (MLPs) are DTC-eligible.

Source: Uniform Partnership Act; Uniform Limited Partnership Act.

Master Limited Partnership (MLP)

Public Partnership
MLP

A publicly traded partnership that offers the tax benefits of a partnership with the liquidity of publicly traded securities. MLPs are commonly used in the energy and infrastructure sectors.

Key features: MLPs are publicly traded partnerships that are listed on national exchanges. They are pass-through entities, meaning they do not pay corporate income tax. Instead, income flows through to unit holders (partners) who pay tax at their individual rates. MLPs are typically used in energy, infrastructure, and natural resources.

DTC eligibility: MLP units are typically DTC-eligible through their transfer agents. They execute Letters of Representations with DTC and participate in the FAST program for electronic book-entry transfers.

Use cases: Energy pipeline companies, storage and transportation infrastructure, renewable energy projects, and other capital-intensive businesses with stable cash flows.

Source: IRC § 7704; SEC rules.

General Partnership (GP)

Partnership Type
General Partnership

A partnership where all partners have unlimited liability for the debts of the partnership and participate in management. This is the most basic partnership structure.

Key features: In a GP, all partners share equally in management and profits (unless agreed otherwise). Each partner has unlimited personal liability for the debts of the partnership. GPs are pass-through entities — income and losses flow through to partners' personal tax returns.

Use cases: Small businesses, professional services firms (in some states), and entities that do not require liability protection. GPs are increasingly rare as LLCs offer similar features with limited liability.

Source: Uniform Partnership Act.

Limited Partnership (LP)

Partnership Type
Limited Partnership

A partnership with one or more general partners (unlimited liability, management) and one or more limited partners (liability limited to their investment, no management). LPs are commonly used in real estate and private equity.

Key features: The general partner manages the partnership and has unlimited liability. Limited partners contribute capital but do not participate in management and have liability limited to their investment. LPs are pass-through entities.

Use cases: Real estate investment funds, private equity and venture capital funds, hedge funds, and family limited partnerships. LPs are popular for tax and liability reasons in investment fund structures.

Source: Uniform Limited Partnership Act.

Limited Liability Partnership (LLP)

Partnership Type
Limited Liability Partnership

A partnership where all partners have limited liability for the debts of the partnership. LLPs are commonly used by professional services firms like law firms and accounting firms.

Key features: In an LLP, partners are not personally liable for the partnership's debts or for the negligence of other partners. Each partner has limited liability for their own actions and the actions of those they supervise. LLPs are pass-through entities.

Use cases: Professional services firms — law firms, accounting firms, consulting firms, and architectural firms. LLPs are popular because they provide liability protection while maintaining a partnership structure for tax purposes.

Source: Uniform Partnership Act; state LLP statutes.

Restricted Securities

Securities Law
Restricted Securities

Securities acquired in private transactions (not public offerings) that are subject to resale restrictions under the Securities Act of 1933. Restricted securities are typically held for a period before they can be publicly resold.

Key features: Restricted securities are issued in private placements under Regulation D, Section 4(a)(2), or other exemptions from registration. They are subject to resale restrictions under the Securities Act of 1933. The restrictions are designed to protect investors and ensure that securities sold privately remain in private hands unless proper registration or exemption applies.

Resale pathways: Restricted securities can be resold under Rule 144 (public resale with holding periods), Rule 144A (resale to QIBs), or Regulation S (offshore resale). Each pathway has specific conditions that must be met.

Holding periods: Rule 144 requires a holding period of 6 months for reporting companies and 1 year for non-reporting companies. The holding period is measured from the date the securities were acquired.

Source: Securities Act of 1933; Rule 144; Rule 144A; Regulation S.

Rule 144

SEC Rule
Rule 144

An SEC rule that provides a safe harbor for the public resale of restricted securities. Key requirements include a holding period (typically 6 months), current public information, volume limitations (for affiliates), and proper manner of sale.

Holding period: 6 months for reporting companies; 1 year for non-reporting companies. The holding period is measured from the date the securities were fully paid for and acquired.

Current public information: The issuer must be current in its SEC filings (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K).

Volume limitations: For affiliates, the amount sold during any 3-month period cannot exceed the greater of 1% of the outstanding shares or the average weekly trading volume over the preceding 4 weeks.

Manner of sale: Affiliates must sell in a manner consistent with the rule (e.g., broker transactions, riskless principal transactions).

Filing requirement: Affiliates must file Form 144 with the SEC.

Source: 17 CFR § 230.144.

Rule 144A

SEC Rule
Rule 144A

An SEC rule that allows the resale of restricted securities to Qualified Institutional Buyers (QIBs) without public registration. Rule 144A provides a private market for institutional trading of restricted securities.

Key features: Rule 144A transactions are exempt from the registration requirements of the Securities Act of 1933. They provide a mechanism for institutions to buy and sell restricted securities privately. Rule 144A securities are typically held in DTC with a restricted CUSIP and can only be transferred to other QIBs.

QIB definition: A QIB is an institution with $100 million or more in securities holdings. QIBs include insurance companies, investment companies, banks, and pension funds.

DTC holding: Rule 144A securities are often held at DTC in book-entry form with a restricted CUSIP. They can be transferred between QIBs through DTC's systems, providing liquidity in the private market.

Source: 17 CFR § 230.144A.

Regulation S

SEC Rule
Regulation S

An SEC rule that provides a safe harbor for offers and sales of securities made outside the United States. It allows issuers and distributors to sell securities to foreign investors without SEC registration, provided certain conditions are met.

Key features: Regulation S transactions must be made in an offshore transaction (outside the U.S.) and with no "directed selling efforts" in the U.S. The rule provides a safe harbor for issuers, distributors, and their affiliates.

Conditions: The sale must be made in an offshore transaction; no directed selling efforts in the U.S.; the buyer must be a non-U.S. person; and the offering must be conducted in compliance with foreign laws.

Restrictions: Regulation S securities are subject to restrictions on resale into the U.S. They typically have a restricted CUSIP and can be held at DTC with transfer restrictions.

Source: 17 CFR § 230.901-905.

Qualified Institutional Buyer (QIB)

Definition
QIB

An institution that meets the definition under Rule 144A, typically with $100 million or more in securities holdings. QIBs are the only entities eligible to purchase securities in Rule 144A transactions.

Eligibility: An entity must have $100 million or more in securities holdings (investments in securities that are not affiliated with the entity). QIBs include insurance companies, investment companies registered under the Investment Company Act, banks, pension funds, and certain investment advisers.

Importance: QIBs are the primary participants in the private placement market. They have the resources and sophistication to evaluate private offerings without the protections of public registration. The QIB designation is central to the Rule 144A private market.

Source: Rule 144A(a)(1).

Corporate Action

Event
Corporate Action

An event that affects the securities of a corporation, such as a merger, acquisition, spin-off, dividend, stock split, or rights offering. Corporate actions require processing by the issuer, transfer agent, and DTC.

Types of corporate actions: Mandatory (e.g., stock splits, mergers, spin-offs) and voluntary (e.g., tender offers, rights offerings, elections). Mandatory actions affect all shareholders; voluntary actions require shareholder participation.

DTC processing: DTC processes corporate actions through its systems. For mandatory actions, DTC handles the exchange of securities and distribution of proceeds. For voluntary actions, DTC facilitates the election and tendering process through ATOP (Automated Tender Offer Program).

Role of transfer agent: The transfer agent works with DTC to set up corporate actions, distribute materials, and process exchanges. Timely and accurate notification to DTC is critical to avoid processing delays.

Source: DTC Corporate Action Services; transfer agent guidelines.

Securities Act of 1933

Federal Statute
Securities Act of 1933

The primary federal law governing the offer and sale of securities in the United States. It requires registration of securities offerings (unless exempt) and prohibits fraud in connection with the sale of securities.

Key provisions: Section 5 requires registration of securities offerings; Section 11 provides liability for misstatements in registration statements; Section 12 provides liability for misstatements in prospectuses; Section 17 prohibits fraud in the offer and sale of securities.

Exemptions: The Act provides exemptions from registration for certain transactions, including private placements (Section 4(a)(2), Regulation D), sales to QIBs (Rule 144A), and offshore transactions (Regulation S).

Enforcement: The SEC enforces the Act and can bring civil actions for violations. Private rights of action also exist for investors who purchase securities in violation of the Act.

Source: 15 U.S.C. § 77a et seq.

Registration Statement

SEC Filing
Registration Statement

A document filed with the SEC to register securities for public offering. Common forms include Form S-1 (initial public offering), Form S-3 (shelf registration), and Form S-8 (employee benefit plans).

Key components: The registration statement includes a prospectus (which is the offering document for investors) and additional information filed with the SEC (Part II). The prospectus contains information about the issuer, the offering, risk factors, use of proceeds, financial statements, and management.

Review process: The SEC staff reviews the registration statement and provides comments to the issuer. The issuer responds to comments and files amendments. The registration is declared "effective" when the SEC completes its review and the issuer requests effectiveness.

Types: Form S-1 (general form for IPOs and other offerings), Form S-3 (shelf registration for seasoned issuers), Form S-4 (merger and acquisition registrations), Form S-8 (employee benefit plans).

Source: Securities Act of 1933; SEC forms.

Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)

Federal Regulator
SEC

The U.S. federal agency responsible for enforcing securities laws, regulating securities markets, and protecting investors. The SEC reviews and declares effective registration statements for public offerings.

Key responsibilities: The SEC reviews registration statements for public offerings; enforces securities laws; regulates securities exchanges (NYSE, NASDAQ); oversees investment advisers and investment companies; and protects investors from fraud.

Divisions: Division of Corporation Finance (reviews public company filings), Division of Enforcement (investigates violations), Division of Investment Management (regulates investment companies and advisers), and Division of Trading and Markets (regulates exchanges and broker-dealers).

Role in DTC: The SEC has oversight of the DTC system. DTC is a registered clearing agency under the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and is subject to SEC rules and oversight.

Source: Securities Exchange Act of 1934; SEC rules.

Public Offering

Securities Sale
Offering

The sale of securities to the public. An Initial Public Offering (IPO) is the first time a company sells shares to the public. Secondary offerings involve the sale of additional shares by the company or existing shareholders.

Types: IPO (first public sale), secondary offering (additional shares from the company or shareholders), follow-on offering (additional shares from the company), and at-the-market offering (continuous sale of shares into the market).

Process: The issuer files a registration statement with the SEC, the SEC reviews and declares it effective, the underwriters price the offering, and the securities are sold to investors. After the offering, the securities are deposited with DTC and become eligible for book-entry trading.

DTC deposit: After the offering, the transfer agent deposits the securities with DTC. The securities become DTC-eligible and are held in book-entry form for trading.

Source: Securities Act of 1933; SEC rules.

Book-Entry System

Record-Keeping
Book-Entry

A system of tracking ownership of securities through electronic ledger entries rather than physical certificates. DTC is the primary book-entry system for U.S. securities.

Key features: In the book-entry system, securities are held electronically at DTC. Ownership is recorded on the books of DTC and participants, not on physical certificates. The system eliminates the need for physical certificates and simplifies trading and settlement.

Benefits: Reduced costs (no physical certificates), faster settlement, reduced risk of loss or theft, and simplified corporate actions processing. The book-entry system is the foundation of modern securities trading.

Role of Cede & Co.: Cede & Co. is the nominee of DTC and is the registered owner of all securities held in DTC's book-entry system. Cede & Co. holds legal title on behalf of DTC participants and their customers.

Source: New York UCC Article 8; DTC rules.

Proxy Voting System

Voting
Proxy System

The system by which beneficial owners (shareholders holding in street name) receive proxy materials and vote through the chain of intermediaries: issuer → DTC → participant → beneficial owner.

How it works: The issuer sends proxy materials to the registered owner (Cede & Co.). DTC forwards the materials to participants (broker-dealers). Participants forward the materials to beneficial owners. Beneficial owners vote their shares through the same chain, with votes aggregated and transmitted back to the issuer.

Key participants: Issuer (solicits proxies), Cede & Co. (registered owner), DTC (forwards materials), participants (broker-dealers), and beneficial owners (voting shareholders). The system is designed to ensure that all shareholders have the opportunity to vote.

NOBO vs OBO: Non-Objecting Beneficial Owners (NOBOs) allow issuers to communicate with them directly. Objecting Beneficial Owners (OBOs) do not. The distinction affects how issuers communicate with shareholders.

Source: SEC Rule 14b-1; DTC Proxy Procedures.

Depository Trust Company (DTC)

Central Securities Depository
DTC

A limited-purpose trust company that holds the majority of U.S. equity and debt securities in book-entry form. DTC provides settlement, custody, and asset servicing for securities held in street name.

Key functions: DTC holds securities in book-entry form, processes securities settlements (T+1 for most equity and many corporate cash trades since May 2024), maintains records of securities positions for participants, facilitates corporate actions and proxy voting, and provides asset servicing (dividends, interest, redemptions).

DTC eligibility: Not all securities are eligible for DTC services. Eligibility requires the issuer to have a transfer agent that participates in FAST, execute a Letter of Representations with DTC, and meet DTC's operational and legal requirements.

Cede & Co.: Cede & Co. is the nominee of DTC and is the registered owner of all securities held in DTC's book-entry system. It holds legal title on behalf of DTC participants and their customers.

Source: Securities Exchange Act of 1934, Section 17A; SEC Rule 17Ad-22 (Clearing Agency Standards); DTC Rules.

Cede & Co.

Nominee Partnership
Cede & Co.

A New York general partnership formed by DTC to serve as the registered owner of securities on the books of issuers. It holds legal title as nominee for DTC participants and their customers.

Role in the DTC system: Cede & Co. is the registered owner of all securities held in DTC's book-entry system. On the issuer's books, Cede & Co. is the only shareholder of record for DTC-eligible securities. This simplifies record-keeping for issuers and allows for efficient processing of corporate actions.

Beneficial ownership: The actual owners of securities — the beneficial owners — are not named on the issuer's books. Their ownership is recorded in the books of DTC participants (brokers, banks) who hold positions in their customers' accounts. This is the street name system.

Legal nature: Cede & Co. is a general partnership formed under New York law. It has no separate existence apart from its role as DTC's nominee. It does not conduct business or have any economic interest in the securities it holds.

Source: DTC Rule 1 (Definitions; Governing Law); DTC Rule 2 (Participants and Pledgees); New York Partnership Law.

DTC Participant

Member Entity
DTC Participant

A broker-dealer, bank, or other financial institution that maintains a securities account at DTC. Participants are the intermediaries through which investors access the depository system.

Key roles: Participants hold securities on behalf of their customers (beneficial owners), process securities trades through DTC's settlement system, receive proxy materials and corporate action notifications from DTC, and forward them to beneficial owners, and facilitate the exercise of voting rights by beneficial owners.

Types of participants: Banks, broker-dealers, custodians, and other financial institutions that meet DTC's membership requirements. Participants must have a clearing agency relationship with DTC and meet regulatory and operational standards.

DTC eligibility: Participants are essential to DTC eligibility — securities must be held through a participant to be traded on the DTC system. The participant chain is how beneficial owners hold securities in street name.

Source: DTC Rule 2 (Participants and Pledgees); DTC Rule 3 (Participants Qualifications).

Issuer

Entity
Issuer

The entity that creates and sells securities to investors. Issuers can be corporations, LLCs, partnerships, trusts, or governments. They are responsible for registration, compliance, and corporate actions.

Key responsibilities: Issuers are responsible for registration of securities offerings (or claiming exemptions), compliance with ongoing reporting and disclosure requirements (for public companies), processing corporate actions (mergers, spin-offs, dividends), and maintaining a transfer agent to handle DTC eligibility and book-entry transfers.

Types of issuers: Corporate issuers (public and private companies), LLCs, partnerships, investment companies (mutual funds, ETFs), and government entities (municipal bonds, Treasury securities). Each type has specific regulatory requirements.

DTC eligibility: Issuers that want their securities to be DTC-eligible must engage a transfer agent that participates in FAST, execute a Letter of Representations with DTC, and meet DTC's operational requirements.

Source: Securities Act of 1933; DTC rules.

Common Stock

Equity Security
Common Stock

The most basic form of corporate ownership. Common stockholders have voting rights (typically one vote per share), receive dividends (if declared), and have a residual claim on assets after creditors and preferred stockholders.

Key features: Common stock represents equity ownership in a corporation. Shareholders have voting rights on major corporate matters, such as electing the board of directors, approving mergers, and amending the corporate charter. They receive dividends when declared by the board, and have a residual claim on assets after debts and preferred stock claims are satisfied in liquidation.

Risks and rewards: Common stock offers the potential for capital appreciation (price increases) and dividend income. However, it is the most junior form of corporate ownership and has the highest risk. In bankruptcy, common stockholders are last in line after creditors and preferred stockholders.

DTC holding: Common stock is the most common type of security held at DTC. Public company common stock is typically DTC-eligible and trades in book-entry form.

Source: Model Business Corporation Act; state corporate statutes.

Preferred Stock

Equity Security
Preferred Stock

A class of stock that has a higher claim on assets and earnings than common stock. Preferred stockholders typically receive dividends before common stockholders and have preference in liquidation, but may have limited or no voting rights.

Key features: Preferred stock has a dividend preference over common stock (usually at a fixed rate). It also has a liquidation preference — preferred stockholders receive their investment back before common stockholders in liquidation. Preferred stock may be convertible into common stock, callable by the issuer, or have other special features.

Types of preferred stock: Cumulative preferred (unpaid dividends accumulate), non-cumulative (unpaid dividends are lost), participating preferred (additional dividends beyond the fixed rate), convertible preferred (convertible into common stock), and callable preferred (issuer can redeem at a specified price).

DTC holding: Preferred stock is typically DTC-eligible and trades in book-entry form. It is often held by institutional investors seeking income and preference rights over common stock.

Source: Model Business Corporation Act; state corporate statutes.
