Overview — Non-Profit Foundations & Securities

Non-profit foundations are a cornerstone of the philanthropic sector, managing billions of dollars in assets dedicated to charitable purposes. Their investment portfolios — often structured as endowments — are typically invested in publicly traded securities, bonds, and alternative assets. Understanding how these securities are held, traded, and accounted for within the DTC/Cede & Co. system is essential for foundation trustees, investment committees, and compliance officers.

This guide explains the legal and operational framework for foundation securities ownership, from 501(c)(3) tax-exempt status and UPMIFA investment rules to the mechanics of holding securities in street name through DTC participants. It also covers donor-advised funds — a rapidly growing vehicle for charitable giving that has unique securities-handling characteristics — and clarifies how the narrower 508(c)(1)(A) church exemption differs from standard 501(c)(3) recognition.

Private Foundation
501(c)(3) — Grantmaking
A non-operating foundation that receives contributions and makes grants to other charities. Typically has an endowment that is invested for long-term growth and payout.
Public Charity
501(c)(3) — Direct Services
A charity that conducts its own programs and receives broad public support. May maintain smaller endowments and often uses securities as a funding source.
Donor-Advised Fund (DAF)
Sponsored Fund
A charitable fund sponsored by a public charity (e.g., Fidelity Charitable, Schwab Charitable, Vanguard Charitable). Donors contribute assets and recommend grants; the sponsor manages the investment pool.
Investment Manager / Custodian
Service Provider
The entity (often a bank or trust company) that manages the foundation's investment portfolio and holds its securities in custody. Custodians are DTC participants.
Church / Religious Organization

Automatically tax-exempt under 501(c)(3) without filing for IRS recognition. Still subject to the same securities, custody, and tax rules as any other 501(c)(3) organization — 508(c)(1)(A) is a filing exemption, not a distinct legal structure.

Endowment Management

A foundation's endowment is the permanent pool of assets that generates investment income to support the foundation's mission. Unlike a for-profit corporation's capital, a foundation's endowment is typically subject to UPMIFA (the Uniform Prudent Management of Institutional Funds Act) — a legal framework that governs how endowments are invested and spent.

Under UPMIFA, foundation trustees have a fiduciary duty to manage endowment funds with the care of a prudent person. They must balance the need for current income (to fund grants and operations) with the preservation of purchasing power over the long term. Most foundations adopt an investment policy statement (IPS) that sets asset allocation targets, risk parameters, and spending policies — typically 4–6% of the average asset value over the trailing 3–5 years.

Concept Map — Endowment Lifecycle

Endowment lifecycle stages: Contributions from donors form the foundation's corpus. The corpus is invested in a diversified portfolio (equities, bonds, alternatives). Investment returns (income + appreciation) grow the portfolio. Spending distributions (typically 4-6% of rolling average) fund grants and operations. The cycle repeats, with the goal of preserving purchasing power.

The endowment cycle: Contributions build corpus → investments generate returns → growth compounds → spending funds grants and operations — with the goal of preserving real purchasing power over time.

Investment Policy Statement (IPS)

A foundation's Investment Policy Statement (IPS) is the governing document for investment decisions. It typically includes:

  • Return Objectives: The target total return (often CPI + 5% to maintain purchasing power).
  • Risk Tolerance: The acceptable level of volatility and drawdown.
  • Asset Allocation: Target percentages for equities, fixed income, real assets, private equity, and alternatives.
  • Spending Policy: The formula for determining annual spending (e.g., 5% of trailing 3-year average market value).
  • Responsible Investing: ESG (environmental, social, governance) considerations, if applicable.

The IPS is approved by the board of trustees and reviewed regularly. It serves as the foundation's "investment constitution" — guiding investment manager selection, performance evaluation, and portfolio rebalancing.

Process Map — Endowment Funding & Investment Flow

Six-step flow: Donor makes a contribution of cash or securities. Foundation deposits assets with its custodian (a DTC participant). Investment manager allocates assets per the Investment Policy Statement. Portfolio generates investment returns (income and appreciation). Assets are revalued and spending is calculated (typically 4-6% of rolling average). Spending funds grants and operations; remaining assets are reinvested.

Step summary (text)
1 · Contribution — Donor contributes cash or securities to the foundation.
2 · Deposit — Foundation deposits assets with its custodian (a DTC participant).
3 · Allocation — Investment manager allocates assets per the Investment Policy Statement.
4 · Returns — Portfolio generates investment income and capital appreciation.
5 · Spending — Spending is calculated (typically 4-6% of rolling average) and funds grants/operations.
Spending Policy Note: The 4–6% spending range widely used today — not a legal requirement, but a widely adopted practice for endowments generally — traces back to the "total return" investing approach popularized by the Ford Foundation's 1969 Advisory Committee on Endowment Management report, which urged trustees to maximize long-term total return rather than focus narrowly on current income. Don't confuse this voluntary convention with the legal 5% minimum distribution requirement that applies specifically to private foundations under IRC § 4942 (which traces to the Tax Reform Act of 1969) — public charities and endowed institutions generally are not subject to that statutory minimum.

Securities Ownership — DTC, Cede & Co. & Foundations

For most foundations, investment securities are held in street name through a custodian that is a DTC participant. This allows for efficient trading, settlement, and corporate action processing. The custodian — often a bank trust department or a brokerage firm — holds the foundation's securities in its DTC account, with Cede & Co. as the registered owner on the issuer's books.

From the foundation's perspective, its securities are "at the custodian" — meaning the custodian maintains the official record of the foundation's positions, sends trade confirmations, collects dividends and interest, and provides periodic account statements. The foundation is the beneficial owner, enjoying all economic rights (dividends, capital appreciation, voting rights) through the custodian.

Concept Map — Foundation Ownership Stack (DTC)

Ownership stack for a foundation's securities: Issuer books show Cede and Co. as the registered owner. Cede is the nominee for DTC. DTC credits the custodian's participant account. The custodian maintains a client account for the foundation. The foundation is the beneficial owner with full economic rights.

Title sits at the top of the stack (Cede & Co.); the foundation's beneficial interest sits at the bottom. The custodian (a DTC participant) connects the two.

Book-Entry vs. Physical Certificates

Foundations almost universally hold securities in book-entry form through DTC. Physical stock certificates are rarely issued or held, and most issuers have eliminated physical certificates entirely. The advantages of book-entry for foundations are significant:

  • Efficiency: Trades settle on T+1 rather than the days or weeks required for physical delivery.
  • Safety: No risk of lost, stolen, or forged certificates.
  • Cost: No certificate issuance, insurance, or secure storage costs.
  • Corporate Actions: Dividends, splits, mergers, and proxy voting are processed automatically.
Process Map — DTC Securities Deposit for Foundations

Five-step process: Foundation authorizes the custodian to deposit securities. Custodian (a DTC participant) submits deposit instructions via DTC's systems. DTC credits the custodian's participant account and reregisters the securities to Cede and Co. The foundation receives confirmation and can view the position online. Dividend and corporate action processing follows the standard DTC pass-through.

Step summary (text)
1 · Authorize — The foundation authorizes its custodian to deposit securities.
2 · Instruct — The custodian (a DTC participant) submits deposit instructions to DTC.
3 · Deposit — DTC credits the custodian's participant account and reregisters the securities to Cede & Co.
4 · Confirm — The foundation receives confirmation and can view the position online.
5 · Service — Dividends, proxies, and corporate actions flow through DTC to the custodian and then to the foundation.
Custodian Risk: When a foundation holds securities through a custodian, it bears custodian risk — the risk that the custodian fails, mismanages assets, or commits fraud. To mitigate this, foundations should use well-capitalized, regulated custodians (typically banks or broker-dealers with strong credit ratings), require regular account reconciliations, and ensure assets are segregated and held in the foundation's name at the custodian level (not commingled with the custodian's own assets).

Donor-Advised Funds (DAFs)

Donor-advised funds have become one of the fastest-growing vehicles for charitable giving in the United States. A DAF is a charitable fund sponsored by a public charity (such as Fidelity Charitable, Schwab Charitable, or Vanguard Charitable). Donors contribute assets — often appreciated securities — to the DAF, receive an immediate tax deduction, and then recommend grants to qualified charities over time.

From a securities ownership perspective, DAFs have several unique characteristics. The sponsoring organization is the legal owner of the assets and is a 501(c)(3) public charity. It maintains investment accounts at custodians, which are DTC participants. Donors do not own the assets — they have a "donor advisory" right to recommend grants, but the sponsoring organization has ultimate legal control. This distinction is important for tax, compliance, and securities law purposes.

Concept Map — Donor-Advised Fund Structure

DAF structure: Donor contributes assets to a DAF Sponsoring Organization (a 501(c)(3) public charity). The sponsoring organization holds assets at a custodian (DTC participant) in book-entry form. The donor advises the sponsoring organization on grant recommendations. The sponsoring organization makes grants to charitable beneficiaries. The donor receives an immediate tax deduction (fair market value of contributed assets).

DAF structure: Donor contributes to the DAF sponsor (a 501(c)(3)), receives an immediate tax deduction, and recommends grants. The sponsor holds assets through a custodian (DTC participant) in book-entry form.

Securities Contributions to DAFs

Donors often contribute appreciated securities to DAFs rather than cash. This is a powerful tax strategy because it:

  • Eliminates capital gains tax — the donor avoids paying tax on the appreciation.
  • Provides a full fair market value deduction — the donor can deduct the full current value of the securities (subject to IRS limits).
  • Increases the charitable impact — the DAF receives the full value of the securities, which can be sold tax-free by the DAF sponsor.

From a securities-processing perspective, the contribution involves transferring the securities from the donor's brokerage account to the DAF sponsor's custodial account. This is typically done through DTC using a DVP (Delivery Versus Payment) transfer or a free delivery (no cash consideration). The DAF sponsor's custodian then sells the securities (often as soon as practical) and invests the proceeds according to the DAF's investment policy.

Process Map — DAF Securities Contribution

Six-step process for contributing appreciated securities to a DAF: Donor initiates transfer from their brokerage account to the DAF sponsor's account. Brokerages coordinate via DTC to move the securities. The DAF sponsor's custodian receives the securities and provides a donation receipt. The sponsor sells the securities (often immediately) and invests the proceeds. The donor receives a tax receipt for the fair market value of the securities on the date of the gift. The donor's charitable fund balance is credited, and the donor can recommend grants.

Step summary (text)
1 · Donor initiates — Donor contacts the DAF sponsor or uses an online portal to initiate the transfer.
2 · Brokerage coordination — The donor's brokerage and the DAF sponsor's custodian coordinate via DTC.
3 · Custodian receives — The DAF sponsor's custodian receives the securities in its DTC account.
4 · Sponsor sells & invests — The DAF sponsor sells the securities (often immediately) and invests the proceeds.
5 · Tax receipt — The donor receives a tax receipt for the fair market value of the securities on the date of the gift.
Tax Tip: For highly appreciated securities, donating them to a DAF can be significantly more tax-efficient than selling them and donating the cash. By donating appreciated securities, you eliminate the capital gains tax that would otherwise be due on the sale, and you receive a charitable deduction for the full fair market value (subject to IRS limits — typically 30% of adjusted gross income for securities, with a 5-year carryforward).
Donor Advisory Rights: Donors to DAFs have the right to advise the sponsor on grants — but the sponsor has ultimate legal authority. The donor cannot direct the sponsor to make a grant to a disqualified person or for a non-charitable purpose. The IRS treats DAF contributions as completed gifts to the sponsor, not as donor-controlled funds.

Regulatory Compliance — Foundations & Securities

Non-profit foundations are subject to a complex web of tax and regulatory requirements that intersect with their securities holdings. Key among them are the Form 990-PF annual return, the Unrelated Business Income Tax (UBIT) rules, and the excise tax on investment income. Understanding how these rules apply to securities transactions is essential for foundation trustees and compliance officers.

Concept Map — Foundation Compliance Framework

Compliance framework for foundations: three pillars — Tax Compliance (Form 990-PF), Investment Compliance (UPMIFA, prudent investor rule, IPS), and Regulatory Compliance (UBIT, excise tax, excess business holdings). Custodians and investment managers support reporting and data provision.

Three compliance pillars: Tax compliance (Form 990-PF), investment compliance (UPMIFA, IPS), and regulatory compliance (UBIT, excise tax, excess business holdings).

Form 990-PF — The Foundation Tax Return

Private foundations (and certain other types of foundations) must file Form 990-PF with the IRS annually. This return requires detailed reporting of:

  • Assets and liabilities: Schedule of investment holdings, including security-level detail for publicly traded securities.
  • Investment income: Dividends, interest, rental income, and capital gains.
  • Distributions: Grants made, administrative expenses, and the required 5% distribution test.
  • Excise tax: Calculation of the flat 1.39% excise tax on net investment income.
  • Unrelated Business Income Tax (UBIT): Income from activities not substantially related to the foundation's charitable purpose.

Foundations rely on their custodians and investment managers to provide accurate and timely data for these filings. The custodian provides monthly or quarterly statements with detailed transaction and position data; the investment manager provides performance and valuation data; and the foundation's tax advisor consolidates and prepares the return.

Unrelated Business Income Tax (UBIT)

Unrelated Business Income Tax (UBIT) is a tax on income generated by activities that are not substantially related to the foundation's charitable purpose. For investment portfolios, UBIT can arise from:

  • Leveraged investments: Income from property acquired with debt financing (e.g., a private equity fund that uses leverage).
  • Qualified partnership income: Income from partnerships that includes UBIT-generating activities.
  • Unrelated business activities: Income from activities conducted in connection with investments (e.g., royalties, advertising income).

Foundations must monitor their investments for UBIT exposure and file Form 990-T if they have $1,000 or more of unrelated business taxable income. Many foundations avoid leveraged investments or invest through vehicles that minimize UBIT exposure.

UBIT Trap: Many foundations are surprised to learn that income from investments in certain alternative assets (like hedge funds or private equity funds) can generate UBIT. Before investing, foundations should review the fund's investment structure and consult with tax advisors to understand UBIT implications.

Excise Tax on Investment Income

Private foundations are subject to an excise tax on net investment income under IRC § 4940, currently a flat rate of 1.39%. This replaced the older two-tier system (2%, reducible to 1% if the foundation met certain distribution requirements) for tax years beginning after December 20, 2019 — the flat rate was adopted specifically to simplify planning and remove the incentive to time distributions around the old reduced-rate test. The tax is calculated on the foundation's net investment income, which includes dividends, interest, rents, royalties, and capital gains (net of losses and expenses). Foreign private foundations are generally subject to a 4% rate instead.

The excise tax is reported and paid on Form 990-PF. Foundations with significant investment portfolios often have substantial excise tax liabilities, which reduce the amount available for charitable grants. Some foundations structure their investments to minimize excise tax exposure (e.g., by using tax-exempt bonds or investing in partnerships that are treated as pass-through entities).

Excess Business Holdings

Private foundations are subject to the excess business holdings rules, which limit the percentage of a business that a foundation and its related disqualified persons can own. Generally, a foundation and its disqualified persons together cannot own more than 20% of the voting stock of a business (or 35% if a non-disqualified person has effective control). This applies to public and private companies alike — the relevant carve-out is the 2% de minimis rule: a foundation is not treated as having excess business holdings in any corporation in which it (together with related foundations) owns 2% or less of both the voting stock and the value of all outstanding shares, regardless of what disqualified persons hold. In practice, most diversified public-equity positions fall under this de minimis threshold, but a concentrated position in a single public company can still trigger the rule.

For foundations that hold concentrated positions in publicly traded companies — or that receive private company stock as a contribution — monitoring excess business holdings is essential. The IRS imposes an initial 10% tax on excess holdings, with additional taxes and penalties if the excess is not corrected within a prescribed period.

Euroclear & Cross-Border Securities for Foundations

While most U.S.-based foundations hold their securities at DTC, many also have international investments — particularly in Eurobonds, foreign equities, and global funds. These are often held through Euroclear, the leading international central securities depository. Understanding the Euroclear infrastructure is essential for foundations with global portfolios.

Euroclear operates Euroclear Bank, an International CSD (ICSD) with a banking license, and a network of national CSDs. For a foundation, holding international securities through Euroclear involves a similar ownership stack to DTC: the foundation is the beneficial owner, the custodian (often a global custodian like Bank of New York Mellon, State Street, or JPMorgan) holds the securities in its Euroclear account, and the legal title is held by Euroclear's nominee (or, in some cases, by the issuer's registrar).

Concept Map — Euroclear for Foundations

Euroclear structure for foundations: Foundation is the beneficial owner. A global custodian (DTC participant for US assets, Euroclear participant for international assets) holds Euroclear securities through Euroclear Bank (ICSD) or a national CSD. Euroclear holds legal title (with its own nominee structure) and provides settlement and asset servicing. T2S provides central bank money settlement for Eurozone securities.

Euroclear for foundations: The foundation is the beneficial owner; a global custodian holds the securities in its Euroclear account; Euroclear Bank or a national CSD provides settlement and asset servicing.

Cross-Border Tax & Reporting Considerations

Foundations with international securities face additional tax and reporting obligations, including:

  • Foreign withholding tax: Many countries impose withholding tax on dividends and interest paid to foreign investors. Foundations may be eligible for reduced withholding tax rates under US tax treaties, but must submit proper forms (e.g., Form W-8BEN-E) to their custodians.
  • Form 8938: Statement of Specified Foreign Financial Assets. This form is generally aimed at individuals and "specified domestic entities" that are closely held by a specified individual (broadly, 80%+ owned by one person) and hold significant passive foreign assets. Because a private foundation has no individual owners in that sense, it typically does not meet the closely-held test and is not required to file Form 8938 — foundations should not assume this filing applies to them without confirming with a tax advisor.
  • FATCA: The Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act — requires foreign financial institutions to report US account holders to the IRS. Foundations must ensure their foreign accounts are FATCA-compliant.
  • PFIC reporting: Investments in Passive Foreign Investment Companies (PFICs) trigger additional reporting and tax treatment under IRS rules.

Foundations with significant international investments often rely on their global custodians and tax advisors to manage these obligations. The custodian typically handles withholding tax reclaims and provides year-end tax reporting data.

Cross-Border Tip: The US has tax treaties with many countries that reduce withholding tax rates on dividends and interest. For example, under the US-UK tax treaty, the withholding tax rate on dividends from UK companies is typically 15% (or 0% for qualified retirement plans). Foundations should work with their custodians to ensure they are claiming the correct treaty benefits.

Non-Profit Foundation

501(c)(3) Organization
Foundation

A tax-exempt charitable organization that typically makes grants to other charities rather than operating its own programs. Private foundations are distinguished from public charities by their funding source (usually a single donor or family) and their governance structure.

Types

  • Private Foundation: Funded by a single source (individual, family, corporation), typically with a small board and no public solicitation. Subject to stricter IRS rules.
  • Public Charity (Operating): Conducts its own charitable programs; receives broad public support. May have an endowment but is not primarily grant-making.
  • Community Foundation: A public charity that pools donor contributions and manages endowments for specific geographic areas or causes.

Key Compliance Obligations

  • Annual filing of Form 990-PF (private foundations) or Form 990 (public charities).
  • Minimum distribution requirement: 5% of average asset value (private foundations).
  • Excise tax on net investment income (1.39% for private foundations).
  • Excess business holdings limitations.
Source: IRS Publication 557; Uniform Prudent Management of Institutional Funds Act (UPMIFA).

Endowment

Asset Pool
Endowment

A permanent pool of assets held by a foundation or other non-profit to generate investment income to support operations and grants. The principal is typically preserved (or grown) while income is spent.

Endowment Types

  • Permanent Endowment: Principal must be preserved in perpetuity; only income may be spent.
  • Quasi-Endowment (Board-Designated): Funds set aside by the board for investment, but the board can reclassify them if needed.
  • Term Endowment: Principal can be spent after a specified term or event.

Investment Strategy

Endowments are typically invested for long-term growth with a diversified portfolio of equities, fixed income, real assets, and alternatives. The goal is to achieve a total return that exceeds the spending rate plus inflation, preserving purchasing power over time. Most endowments target a 5% real return (after inflation) to sustain a 4-5% spending rate.

Source: UPMIFA; NACUBO Endowment Study; CFA Institute.

UPMIFA — Uniform Prudent Management of Institutional Funds Act

Uniform State Law
UPMIFA

A uniform act adopted by most U.S. states that provides guidance on the management and investment of institutional funds (including endowments). It replaced the older Uniform Management of Institutional Funds Act (UMIFA).

Key Provisions

  • Prudent Management: Trustees must manage funds with the care of a prudent person, considering both current and future needs.
  • Spending Rules: Allows spending of both income and appreciation, subject to the prudent investor standard.
  • Donor Intent: Preservation of donor intent is a key consideration; the act provides guidance on interpreting donor restrictions.
  • Appropriation: Provides a framework for determining how much may be spent from an endowment, considering the duration, preservation of purchasing power, and other relevant factors.
  • Release of Restrictions: Allows for release of donor restrictions under certain circumstances, with court approval.
Source: Uniform Law Commission; UPMIFA (2006).

Depository Trust Company (DTC)

Central Securities Depository
DTC

A limited-purpose trust company, central securities depository, and SEC-registered clearing agency. It holds the majority of U.S. equity and debt securities in book-entry form and provides asset servicing and settlement services.

Key Statistics

  • Holds over $100 trillion in securities value.
  • Processes millions of transactions daily.
  • Has approximately 300 participants (broker-dealers and banks).
  • Founded in 1973; headquartered in New York.

Foundation Relevance

Foundations use DTC to hold the vast majority of their public market investments. The DTC system provides the infrastructure for trading, settlement, and corporate action processing. Custodians hold securities at DTC on behalf of foundations, providing the foundation with beneficial ownership while Cede & Co. holds legal title.

Source: DTC Rules; DTCC Operational Arrangements (Necessary for Securities to Become and Remain Eligible for DTC Services).

Cede & Co.

Nominee Partnership
Cede & Co.

A New York general partnership formed by DTC to serve as the registered owner of securities on the books of issuers. It holds legal title as nominee for DTC participants and their customers.

Role in Foundation Holdings

When a foundation holds securities through a custodian that is a DTC participant, Cede & Co. is the registered owner on the issuer's books. The foundation is the beneficial owner, enjoying all economic rights through the custodian. This structure—where the legal title and beneficial ownership are separated—is the foundation of the U.S. securities holding system.

Why This Matters

Because Cede & Co. is the registered owner, the issuer does not know the identity of the beneficial owners. This simplifies record-keeping for issuers, enabling efficient processing of dividends, splits, and other corporate actions. For foundations, this means they can trade and settle securities efficiently without the administrative burden of maintaining direct registration with each issuer.

Source: DTC Rules, Article 1; New York Partnership Law.

Beneficial Owner

Property Law Concept
Beneficial Owner

The person or entity that enjoys the economic benefits of property ownership — dividends, interest, capital appreciation, and voting rights — even though legal title is held by another (a nominee or trustee).

Foundation as Beneficial Owner

For securities held in street name through DTC, the foundation is the beneficial owner. It receives dividends, has voting rights (through its custodian), and can sell or pledge the securities. Cede & Co. holds legal title as nominee. The foundation's status as beneficial owner is recognized under both securities law and the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC) Article 8.

Rights and Responsibilities

  • Economic Rights: Right to receive dividends, interest, and capital appreciation.
  • Voting Rights: Right to vote shares through the proxy system.
  • Right to Transfer: Right to sell or pledge the securities.
  • Liability: Beneficial owners are not liable for the debts of the entity holding legal title.
Source: New York UCC Article 8; SEC Rule 14b-1.

Custodian

Service Provider
Custodian

A financial institution (typically a bank or trust company) that holds securities on behalf of a client, maintains records of positions and transactions, and processes corporate actions and proxy voting.

Role in Foundation Securities

Foundations typically use a custodian (or a bank trust department) to hold their securities. The custodian is a DTC participant, meaning it has a securities account at DTC. The custodian holds the foundation's securities in its DTC account, maintains records for the foundation, and processes transactions and corporate actions. Key custodians for foundations include Bank of New York Mellon, State Street, JPMorgan Chase, and Northern Trust.

Services Provided

  • Safekeeping: Holding securities in a segregated account.
  • Record-Keeping: Maintaining position and transaction records.
  • Settlement: Processing trades and settlements.
  • Corporate Actions: Processing dividends, splits, mergers, and proxy voting.
  • Reporting: Providing monthly and quarterly account statements.
  • Tax Reporting: Providing tax data for Form 990-PF filings.
Source: Bank custody agreements; SEC Rule 17Ad-10.

Custodian Risk

Counterparty Risk
Custodian Risk

The risk that a custodian fails, mismanages assets, commits fraud, or otherwise causes losses to the client. This is a form of counterparty risk specific to the custodial relationship.

Types of Custodian Risk

  • Operational Risk: Failure of internal processes, systems, or people.
  • Credit Risk: The custodian itself becomes insolvent.
  • Fraud Risk: Misappropriation of assets by custodian employees.
  • Legal Risk: Custodian fails to comply with laws or regulations.

Mitigation Strategies

  • Segregation: Ensure assets are held in the client's name (not commingled with the custodian's own assets).
  • Reconciliation: Regularly reconcile custodian statements against internal records.
  • Due Diligence: Review the custodian's financial health, regulatory status, and operational controls.
  • Diversification: Consider using multiple custodians to diversify risk.
  • Insurance: Ensure the custodian has adequate insurance (e.g., SIPC coverage for cash and securities).
Source: SEC Investor Bulletin; OCC trust examination guidelines.

Donor

Contributor
Donor

An individual, family, corporation, or other entity that contributes assets to a non-profit foundation or donor-advised fund. Donors receive tax benefits for their contributions and may have advisory rights (in DAFs) or governance rights (in foundations).

Tax Considerations

  • Income Tax Deduction: Donors can deduct contributions to 501(c)(3) organizations (subject to AGI limits — up to 60% of AGI for cash donations, 30% for securities, with a 5-year carryforward).
  • Capital Gains Avoidance: Donating appreciated securities avoids capital gains tax.
  • Estate Tax Reduction: Charitable bequests reduce the donor's taxable estate.
  • Appraisal Requirements: For donations of property over $5,000, a qualified appraisal is required.

Donor Rights vs. Control

In a private foundation, donors often serve on the board and have governance rights. In a DAF, donors have advisory rights to recommend grants but the sponsor has ultimate legal control. The distinction is critical for tax and compliance purposes.

Source: IRS Publication 526 (Charitable Contributions).

Donor-Advised Fund (DAF)

Charitable Giving Vehicle
Donor-Advised Fund

A charitable fund sponsored by a public charity (such as Fidelity Charitable, Schwab Charitable, or Vanguard Charitable). Donors contribute assets, receive an immediate tax deduction, and recommend grants to qualified charities over time.

Key Characteristics

  • Sponsor: A 501(c)(3) public charity that has ultimate legal control.
  • Advisory Rights: Donors can recommend grants, but the sponsor has final authority.
  • Tax Deduction: Immediate deduction for the fair market value of contributed assets.
  • Growth: Assets can be invested and grow tax-free.
  • Minimal Administration: Sponsors handle all administrative and compliance tasks.

Growth Statistics

As of 2024, DAFs hold over $250 billion in assets, with annual contributions exceeding $70 billion. The number of DAF accounts exceeds 2 million, making DAFs one of the most popular charitable giving vehicles in the U.S.

Source: IRS Notice 2017-73; National Philanthropic Trust DAF Report.

Charitable Grant

Distribution
Grant

A distribution of funds from a foundation or donor-advised fund to a charitable beneficiary. Grants are the primary way foundations achieve their charitable purpose and satisfy IRS distribution requirements.

Types

  • Program-Related Investment (PRI): An investment made by a foundation to further its charitable purpose, with repayment expected. Can be an equity investment, loan, or guarantee.
  • General Operating Support: Unrestricted funds for a charity's operations.
  • Project Support: Funds for a specific program or project.
  • Capital Grant: Funds for capital projects (buildings, equipment, etc.).
  • Emergency Grant: Funds for disaster relief or crisis response.

IRS Requirements

Private foundations must make qualifying distributions equal to 5% of average asset value (minus acquisition debt) each year. This is the "minimum distribution requirement" under IRC § 4942. Grants to qualified charities count toward this requirement; administrative expenses also count.

Source: IRS Form 990-PF instructions; Council on Foundations.

Form 990-PF — Private Foundation Return

Tax Return
Form 990-PF

The annual information return filed by private foundations with the IRS. It reports assets, investment income, grants, and calculates the excise tax on net investment income.

Key Sections

  • Part I: Assets, liabilities, and net worth.
  • Part II: Investment income (dividends, interest, rents, capital gains).
  • Part III: Distributions (grants, administrative expenses).
  • Part IV: Excise tax calculation (1.39% of net investment income).
  • Part V: Distributable amount and qualifying distributions.
  • Part VI: Information about foundation officers and directors.
  • Schedule A: Initial return of a private foundation.
  • Schedule B: Schedule of contributors (large gifts).
  • Schedule C: Distributions and other information.
  • Schedule D: Supplemental financial information.
  • Schedule G: Grants and contributions made.
  • Schedule H: Grants to foreign organizations.
Source: IRS Form 990-PF instructions; Treas. Reg. § 1.509(a)-3.

Unrelated Business Income Tax (UBIT)

Tax
UBIT

A tax imposed on income generated by activities that are not substantially related to the foundation's charitable purpose. UBIT is calculated on Form 990-T and applies when a foundation has $1,000 or more of unrelated business taxable income.

Common UBIT Sources for Foundations

  • Debt-Financed Income: Income from property acquired with debt financing (e.g., leveraged investments). This can include income from private equity funds that use leverage.
  • Partnership Income: Income from partnerships that includes UBIT-generating activities. Foundations should review K-1s for UBIT indicators.
  • Unrelated Activities: Income from activities conducted in connection with investments (e.g., royalties, advertising income, or rent from business tenants).
  • Income from S-Corporations: Since S-Corp income is treated as "unrelated" for 501(c)(3) organizations, foundations often avoid S-Corp investments entirely.

How to Minimize UBIT

  • Invest through taxable corporate structures (C-Corps) rather than pass-through entities.
  • Avoid debt-financed investments where possible.
  • Use tax-exempt bond funds for fixed income.
  • Work with investment managers who understand UBIT and can structure investments to minimize exposure.
Source: IRC § 511-514; Treas. Reg. § 1.512(b)-1.

Excise Tax on Investment Income

Tax
Excise Tax

A tax imposed on private foundations under IRC § 4940 based on their net investment income. Since tax years beginning after December 20, 2019, the rate is a flat 1.39% for domestic private foundations (4% for most foreign private foundations). This replaced the older two-tier system (2%, or 1% if the foundation met a minimum distribution test), which is no longer in effect.

Calculation

The excise tax is calculated on the foundation's net investment income, which includes dividends, interest, rents, royalties, and capital gains (net of losses and expenses). The tax is reported and paid on Form 990-PF, with quarterly estimated payments generally required if the annual tax is expected to be $500 or more.

Practical Implications

For a foundation with a $100 million endowment generating a 5% total return ($5 million), the excise tax at 1.39% would be approximately $69,500. Foundations with large endowments often have substantial excise tax liabilities, which reduce the amount available for charitable grants.

Source: IRC § 4940 (as amended by the Further Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2020); IRS "Tax on Net Investment Income" guidance; Treas. Reg. § 53.4940-1.

Excess Business Holdings

IRS Rule
Excess Business Holdings

A rule limiting the percentage of a business that a private foundation and its related entities can own. Generally, a foundation cannot own more than 20% of the voting stock of a business (or 35% under certain conditions).

Key Provisions

  • 20% Rule: Foundation + disqualified persons cannot own more than 20% of voting stock.
  • 35% Rule: If a non-disqualified person has effective control, the threshold is 35%.
  • 2% De Minimis: Foundation is not subject to the rule if it owns 2% or less of both voting stock and total value.
  • Functionally-Related Businesses: Exemptions exist for businesses that are functionally related to the foundation's purpose.

Penalties

  • Initial Tax: 10% excise tax on excess holdings.
  • Additional Tax: 200% of the value of excess holdings if not corrected within 90 days.
  • Additional Tax (Tier 2): 200% of the value of excess holdings if not corrected within the 90-day period after the initial tax is imposed.
Source: IRC § 4943; Treas. Reg. § 53.4943-1.

Street Name

Industry Term
Street Name

The practice of registering securities in the name of a broker-dealer or its nominee (Cede & Co.) rather than in the name of the beneficial owner. The term derives from Wall Street, where brokers historically held customer securities.

Prevalence

Over 99% of publicly traded U.S. equities are held in street name. Foundations almost universally hold their securities in street name through their custodians, which are DTC participants. Direct registration (holding securities in the foundation's own name) is increasingly rare for public market investments.

Advantages

  • Efficiency: Simplified trading and settlement.
  • Cost: No certificate issuance or transfer fees.
  • Convenience: Consolidated statements and records.
  • Corporate Actions: Automatic processing of dividends, splits, and other actions.
Source: SEC Investor Bulletin: "Holding Your Securities."

Book-Entry System

Record-Keeping Method
Book-Entry

A system of tracking ownership of securities through electronic ledger entries rather than physical certificates. Ownership changes are effected by debiting one account and crediting another.

Advantages for Foundations

  • Speed: Trades settle in T+1 rather than days or weeks.
  • Safety: No risk of lost, stolen, or forged certificates.
  • Cost: No certificate issuance, insurance, or secure storage costs.
  • Efficiency: Corporate actions and proxy voting are processed automatically.
  • Reporting: Account statements provide a clear record of positions and transactions.

How It Works

In the book-entry system, DTC maintains electronic records of securities positions for each participant. When a trade occurs, DTC debits the seller's participant account and credits the buyer's participant account. The issuer's books reflect Cede & Co. as the registered owner, but the beneficial owners are recorded in the DTC system through the participant chain.

Source: New York UCC Article 8; SEC Release No. 33-7496.

Delivery Versus Payment (DVP)

Settlement Mechanism
DVP

A settlement mechanism that ensures the delivery of securities occurs only if payment is made, and payment occurs only if securities are delivered. It eliminates principal risk — the risk of delivering securities without receiving payment, or vice versa.

DVP in Foundation Securities

When a foundation sells securities, the custodian executes a DVP transaction: the securities are delivered to the buyer's custodian simultaneously with the receipt of cash. This ensures the foundation receives payment before the securities are released. DVP is the standard settlement method for most securities transactions and is a key risk-management feature of the DTC system.

DVP vs. Free Delivery

  • DVP: Securities delivered with simultaneous cash payment.
  • Free Delivery: Securities delivered without cash payment (used for gifts and contributions).
Source: CPMI-IOSCO PFMI; DTC Rules, Article 9.

Free Delivery

Transfer Method
Free Delivery

A securities transfer made without simultaneous cash payment. Free deliveries are used for gifts, charitable contributions, and certain transfers between accounts.

Use in Foundation Securities

When a donor contributes appreciated securities to a DAF or a foundation, the transfer is typically processed as a free delivery (no cash consideration). The securities are moved from the donor's brokerage account to the foundation's custodial account via DTC without a cash payment. The foundation's custodian then holds the securities on behalf of the foundation.

Settlement Process

Free delivery transactions are processed through DTC's systems using a "free of payment" code. The transfer is typically completed on T+1 (the same settlement cycle as DVP transactions). The donor's broker instructs DTC to move the securities to the foundation's custodian account without any cash movement.

Source: DTC Participant Guide; SEC Regulation S-T.

Euroclear

Financial Market Infrastructure
Euroclear

A Belgium-based financial market infrastructure group specializing in central securities depository (CSD) services. It operates Euroclear Bank (an International CSD) and national CSDs across Europe, providing settlement, safekeeping, and asset servicing for domestic and cross-border securities transactions.

Key Facts

  • Founded: 1968.
  • Headquarters: Brussels, Belgium.
  • Assets Under Custody: Over €40 trillion.
  • Participants: 2,000+ financial institutions across 90+ countries.
  • Ownership: Owned by its participants (financial institutions).

Foundation Relevance

Foundations with international investments — Eurobonds, foreign equities, global funds — use Euroclear through their global custodians. The Euroclear system provides the same basic functions as DTC: settlement, custody, and corporate action processing, but for international securities.

Source: Euroclear Holding SA/NV Annual Report; Euroclear.com.

Euroclear Bank

International CSD (ICSD)
Euroclear Bank

An ICSD with a banking license, headquartered in Brussels. It settles cross-border transactions in international securities (Eurobonds, global bonds, equities, derivatives, and investment funds) and serves as the domestic CSD for Irish securities.

Key Services

  • Settlement: DVP settlement for international securities in ~50 currencies.
  • Custody: Safekeeping for over 1 million international securities.
  • Collateral Management: Triparty collateral management and securities lending.
  • Fund Services: Fund settlement via Fundsettle and MFEX integration.
  • Tax Services: Withholding tax processing and reclaim services.

Foundation Relevance

For foundations with international portfolios, Euroclear Bank is the primary settlement and custody platform for Eurobonds and other international securities. The foundation's global custodian holds the securities in its Euroclear Bank account, providing the foundation with beneficial ownership and efficient cross-border settlement.

Source: Euroclear Bank; National Bank of Belgium.

National CSD

Financial Market Infrastructure
National CSD

A central securities depository that operates within a single country or currency area, maintaining the definitive record of securities issued in that jurisdiction and settling transactions among domestic participants.

Euroclear's National CSDs

  • Euroclear Belgium, France, Nederland: ESES CSDs on a shared platform.
  • Euroclear UK & International: UK CSD operating CREST.
  • Euroclear Sweden / Finland: Nordic CSDs.

Foundation Relevance

Foundations with holdings in specific European countries use the national CSD for that jurisdiction. For example, a foundation holding shares in a French company (e.g., LVMH) would have those securities held in Euroclear France through its global custodian. The national CSD maintains the definitive record of ownership for domestic securities.

Source: ESMA; CSDR Regulation (EU) No 909/2014.

TARGET2-Securities (T2S)

Eurosystem Infrastructure
T2S

A single, pan-European platform for securities settlement in central bank money, operated by the Eurosystem. T2S allows national CSDs to settle securities transactions on a shared technical platform using central bank accounts.

Key Features

  • Central Bank Money: Cash leg settles in central bank accounts (not commercial bank accounts).
  • Single Platform: Multiple CSDs share one technical infrastructure.
  • DVP Model 1: Real-time gross settlement with delivery versus payment.
  • Launch: Phased rollout 2015-2017; Euroclear ESES CSDs connected in 2016.
  • Currencies: Supports EUR, DKK, and SEK settlement (other currencies use T2S with a link).

Foundation Relevance

For foundations with European investments, T2S provides efficient, low-risk settlement in central bank money. Transactions in T2S settle on a gross basis, reducing systemic risk and improving settlement efficiency compared to previous systems.

Source: European Central Bank; T2S Framework.

Investment Policy Statement (IPS)

Governance Document
Investment Policy Statement

A governing document that sets investment objectives, risk tolerance, asset allocation targets, and spending policies for a foundation's endowment. It is approved by the board of trustees and reviewed regularly.

Key Components

  • Return Objectives: Target total return (e.g., CPI + 5%).
  • Risk Tolerance: Acceptable volatility, drawdown, and liquidity constraints.
  • Asset Allocation: Target percentages for equities, bonds, real assets, private equity, and alternatives.
  • Spending Policy: Formula for annual spending (e.g., 5% of trailing 3-year average market value).
  • Responsible Investing: ESG (environmental, social, governance) considerations, if applicable.
  • Manager Selection: Criteria for selecting and monitoring investment managers.
  • Performance Benchmarks: Indices used to evaluate performance (e.g., S&P 500, Bloomberg Aggregate).

Why It Matters

The IPS is the foundation's "investment constitution." It provides clarity to investment managers, protects against inconsistent decision-making, and demonstrates the board's fiduciary diligence. It should be reviewed at least annually and updated as the foundation's circumstances change.

Source: UPMIFA; Council on Foundations; CFA Institute.

Eurobond

International Bond
Eurobond

A bond issued in a currency other than the currency of the country where it is issued. Eurobonds are typically issued by international entities and traded across borders. They are often settled through ICSDs like Euroclear Bank.

Key Characteristics

  • Currency: Issued in a major currency (e.g., USD, EUR, GBP) outside the home country.
  • Bearers: Often issued in bearer form (though many are now book-entry).
  • Settlement: Typically settled through Euroclear Bank or Clearstream.
  • Investors: Attract international investors seeking diversification.
  • Tax Treatment: Often issued in jurisdictions with favorable tax treatment.

Foundation Relevance

Foundations with fixed-income allocations often hold Eurobonds as a way to diversify credit exposure and currency risk. Eurobonds are typically held through a global custodian in Euroclear Bank, with the foundation as beneficial owner.

Source: ICMA; European Securities and Markets Authority.

DTC Participant

Member Entity
DTC Participant

A broker-dealer, bank, or other financial institution that maintains a securities account at DTC and is a member of NSCC. Participants are the intermediaries through which investors access the depository system.

Role in Foundation Securities

A foundation's custodian is typically a DTC participant. The custodian holds the foundation's securities in its DTC account, maintains records for the foundation, and processes transactions and corporate actions. The participant account is the foundation's "book-entry location" in the DTC system.

Participant Responsibilities

  • Hold securities in book-entry form for clients.
  • Process settlement and corporate actions.
  • Maintain accurate client records.
  • Comply with DTC rules and regulatory requirements.
  • Provide account statements and transaction reports to clients.
Source: DTC Rules, Article 2 (Participant Application).

Investment Growth

Compound Returns
Growth

The increase in the value of an investment portfolio over time due to investment returns (income and capital appreciation) that are reinvested, creating compound growth.

Endowment Growth

Endowments grow through investment returns that exceed spending and inflation. The goal is to preserve purchasing power while funding grants. The 4-6% spending rule is designed to allow for growth that keeps pace with inflation. Over the long term, a well-managed endowment can achieve real returns (after inflation and spending) of 2-4%.

Compound Interest in Action

A $100 million endowment earning a 7% annual return, spending 5% and keeping inflation at 2%, would grow to approximately $200 million in real terms over 20 years. This compounding effect demonstrates the power of long-term investment growth.

Source: NACUBO Endowment Study; CFA Institute.

Spending Policy

Payout Rule
Spending Policy

The policy that determines how much of an endowment's assets are spent each year to fund grants and operations. Most foundations use a percentage of the trailing average market value (typically 4-6%).

Why a Spending Policy Matters

  • Stability: Smoothing spending over market cycles helps avoid drastic changes in grantmaking.
  • Preservation: Limiting spending to a percentage of the average asset value helps preserve purchasing power.
  • Compliance: Private foundations must meet a minimum distribution requirement (5% of average asset value) under IRS rules.

Common Approaches

  • Percentage of Average Value: Spend a fixed percentage (e.g., 5%) of the trailing 3-year average market value.
  • Hybrid Approach: Spend a percentage of average value plus a smoothing adjustment to dampen volatility.
  • Floor/Ceiling: Set a minimum and maximum spending amount to protect against extreme market movements.
Source: UPMIFA; IRC § 4942; NACUBO.

Investment Management

Portfolio Management
Investment Management

The professional management of a foundation's investment portfolio, including asset allocation, security selection, and risk management. Investment managers may be internal (staff) or external (hired firms).

Fiduciary Duties

  • Prudent Investor Rule: Investments must be made with the care of a prudent person, considering the fund's purposes, terms, distribution requirements, and other circumstances.
  • Diversification: Avoiding concentration in any single investment or asset class.
  • IPS Compliance: Adhering to the investment policy statement.
  • Monitoring: Regular review of portfolio performance and risk.
  • Cost Awareness: Managing fees and expenses to maximize net returns.

Typical Asset Classes

  • Public Equities: Domestic and international stocks.
  • Fixed Income: Treasury, corporate, municipal, and international bonds.
  • Real Assets: Real estate, infrastructure, commodities.
  • Private Equity: Venture capital, buyout funds, growth equity.
  • Hedge Funds: Alternative strategies for diversification.
Source: UPMIFA; Uniform Prudent Investor Act; CFA Institute.

501(c)(3) Tax-Exempt Status

IRS Code Section
501(c)(3)

Section of the Internal Revenue Code that provides tax-exempt status to charitable organizations. Organizations that qualify under 501(c)(3) are exempt from federal income tax and donations to them are tax-deductible.

Requirements for 501(c)(3) Status

  • Charitable Purpose: The organization must operate for charitable, religious, educational, scientific, or literary purposes.
  • Non-Profit: The organization cannot have shareholders or distribute profits.
  • No Private Inurement: No part of the organization's income may inure to the benefit of private individuals.
  • Lobbying Limits: Substantial lobbying activities are prohibited.
  • Political Activity: No participation in political campaigns.

Types of 501(c)(3) Organizations

  • Private Foundations: Funded by a single source; subject to excise tax and distribution requirements.
  • Public Charities: Receive broad public support; less restrictive IRS rules.
  • Community Foundations: Public charities that serve a geographic area.
Source: IRC § 501(c)(3); Treas. Reg. § 1.501(c)(3)-1.

508(c)(1)(A) — Church Exemption

IRS Code Section
508(c)(1)(A)

A provision of the Internal Revenue Code stating that churches, their integrated auxiliaries, and conventions or associations of churches are automatically exempt from federal income tax under 501(c)(3) — without needing to apply for or receive an IRS determination letter.

What 508(c)(1)(A) Actually Does

  • Filing exemption only: It waives the requirement to file Form 1023 for recognition of exempt status. A qualifying church is exempt whether or not it ever applies.
  • Same substantive rules apply: A 508(c)(1)(A) church must still meet the same 501(c)(3) requirements as any other charity — organized and operated for exempt purposes, no private inurement, and limits on lobbying and political activity.
  • No effect on securities or custody: Securities donated to or held by a church are still registered in book-entry form through the same DTC/custodian structure described throughout this guide — there is no separate "508" account type or clearing path.
  • Other obligations still apply: Employment tax withholding, unrelated business income tax, and state-law fiduciary duties are unaffected by 508(c)(1)(A) status.

⚠️ Common Misconception

Some promoters market "508(c)(1)(a) trusts" as a special legal entity that provides total financial privacy, exemption from all IRS oversight, or the ability to receive unlimited undeclared donations. This is inaccurate. Section 508(c)(1)(A) does not create a distinct trust or entity type, does not eliminate a church's tax and reporting obligations, and does not provide any special protection from IRS examination. Forming an entity around this misreading of the statute does not change how it is legally treated.

What the IRS Says

The IRS has repeatedly warned about the misuse of 508(c)(1)(A) by promoters who claim it creates a tax-exempt entity without IRS involvement. The IRS has issued guidance clarifying that 508(c)(1)(A) is a filing exemption, not a substance exemption. Churches are still subject to all other tax rules.

Source: IRC § 508(c)(1)(A); IRS Publication 1828, Tax Guide for Churches and Religious Organizations.
