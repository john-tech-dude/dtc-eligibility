Teaching focus: entitlement flow through Cede & Co. and DTC participants—not a full SEC proxy-rule treatise. Pair with the Securities Ownership Wizard for the ownership stack and the eligibility questionnaire for how issues get on deposit.

This guide answers one question: once a security sits at DTC in street name, how do cash, stock, choices, and votes reach the beneficial owner? Eligibility and deposit explain how the position exists. Corporate actions explain what the position earns and decides.

In the modern U.S. system, the legal holder of record for nearly all public equity and many fixed-income issues is Cede & Co., DTC’s nominee. Issuers and transfer agents see Cede—not millions of retail accounts—as the registered owner. Economic entitlements and proxy materials must therefore pass through a layered stack: issuer/agent → DTC → participant → broker or bank custodian → beneficial owner (BO).

Miss a date, mis-route an election, or treat a voluntary offer like a mandatory dividend, and the BO loses value or rights even though the shares never “left” the account. That is the operational heart of post-trade.

Mandatory vs Voluntary Actions

Market practice sorts corporate actions by whether the beneficial owner must elect a response. The same CUSIP can host both types over its life (e.g., a quarterly cash dividend and a later tender offer).

Mandatory

No election required

Positions on the record date automatically receive the entitlement. Brokers still post entries and tax lots, but the BO does not “opt in.”

  • Cash dividends and interest payments
  • Stock splits and reverse splits
  • Spin-offs and many stock dividends
  • Mandatory mergers / cash-out events
  • Name changes and CUSIP replacements
Voluntary

Election required

The BO (or fiduciary) must instruct through the intermediary chain before a deadline. Silence often means default treatment—or forfeiture of the offer.

  • Tender and exchange offers
  • Rights offerings and warrants exercise
  • Optional dividends (cash vs stock)
  • Consent solicitations
  • Conversions and early redemptions (when elective)
Teaching rule of thumb: If the BO must answer a question (“tender or not?”, “cash or stock?”), treat the event as voluntary until you prove otherwise. Process maps and cut-offs differ sharply.
Dimension Mandatory Voluntary
Driver of entitlement Position on record / effective date Valid instruction + eligible position
Typical BO action None (monitor posting) Elect via broker / agent before deadline
Failure mode Posting lag, tax lot error, FX/withholding Missed cut-off, default election, oversubscription

Key Dates Against the Nominee

Date language is where counsel, operations, and retail statements most often talk past each other. Because Cede is holder of record, “who is entitled” is first answered on DTC’s books, then allocated down the chain.

Ownership test

Record date

The date the issuer’s books (or Cede’s position as registered holder) determine who is entitled to a distribution or certain rights. Street-name BOs rely on intermediary records that reconcile to Cede.

Trading convention

Ex-date

The first day the security trades without the upcoming dividend (or similar entitlement). Settlement-cycle conventions align who is the buyer of record relative to the record date—critical under T+1.

Cash / stock move

Pay / payable date

When the issuer/agent pays cash or delivers securities. DTC credits participants; participants credit customer accounts. Posting can lag the legal payable date in edge cases.

Structural change

Effective date

When a merger, split ratio, name change, or similar structural event takes effect. Positions and CUSIPs may convert; elections for voluntary events have separate instruction deadlines.

T+1 interaction: With one-day settlement, ex-date / record-date choreography is tighter than under T+2. Teach learners to check the event announcement and their firm’s cut-offs—not only the issuer press release.

DTC Allocation Chain

For a standard cash dividend, the economic path looks like this. Details vary by instrument and intermediary systems, but the legal geometry is stable: the issuer deals with the registered holder; everyone else is an allocation problem.

  1. Issuer / paying agent funds the event Cash is wired to the agent (or agent draws on issuer arrangements). For securities distributions, the agent coordinates with DTC under depositary arrangements.
  2. Cede is the registered holder The bulk entitlement is calculated against Cede’s position. The issuer does not mail checks to each beneficial owner in street name.
  3. DTC allocates to participants Based on each participant’s position on the relevant date, DTC credits cash or securities to participant accounts (subject to operational calendars and any holds).
  4. Participant allocates to customers Broker-dealers and custodian banks post to end-customer or omnibus sub-accounts, applying house policies, tax lots, and any local withholding.
  5. Beneficial owner sees the result Statement entry, tax form later, and (for votes) proxy materials routed through the communications chain—not a direct issuer envelope in most retail cases.
Concept Map — Entitlement Pass-Through

Flow from issuer and paying agent to Cede and Co at DTC, then to DTC participant, then broker or custodian, then beneficial owner, for cash, stock, and voting rights.

Cash, stock, elections, and votes all travel the nominee stack—but voluntary elections reverse the instruction flow (BO → participant → DTC) before results settle back down.

Mandatory Actions in Practice

Cash dividend (template event)

The cash dividend is the teaching prototype for mandatory actions. Issuer declares amount and dates; Cede’s position determines the bulk claim; DTC and participants allocate. Beneficial owners should see a credit near payable date and later a Form 1099-DIV (U.S. persons) from their broker—not from the issuer directly in street name.

Operational stress points: foreign withholding on ADR/foreign issues, DRIP enrollments that convert cash to stock, and tax-lot accounting when partial shares or return-of-capital components appear.

Stock splits & reverse splits

A forward split multiplies share count and adjusts price; a reverse consolidates. The economic claim is continuous, but CUSIP, lot sizes, and option contracts may change. Intermediaries must restate positions; fractional shares may be paid in cash under firm policy. Reverse splits can force odd-lot handling and delisting-risk narratives—teach the mechanics, not the investment thesis.

Mandatory mergers & CUSIP change

When an event forces conversion into cash, acquirer stock, or a mix without election, positions convert on the effective date. Old CUSIPs stop; new ones appear. “Where did my shares go?” tickets are usually allocation or statement-label issues, not lost certificates—because certificates were never the custody form for street-name holders.

Voluntary Actions & Instruction Paths

Voluntary events invert the information flow: the beneficial owner (or fiduciary) must decide, and that decision must climb the intermediary stack before DTC can protect or deliver the position into the offer.

Process Map — Voluntary Election (Simplified)

Six steps: announcement, broker notifies customer, customer elects, participant aggregates instructions to DTC, DTC interacts with agent or offer, results allocate back to customers.

Retail cut-offs are almost always earlier than headline offer deadlines so firms can aggregate and meet DTC/agent times.

Tender & exchange offers

The BO chooses whether (and sometimes how much) to tender. Shares may be “protected” or delivered into the offer while remaining economically tracked. Proration occurs when the offer is oversubscribed. Partial tenders, guaranteed delivery, and odd-lot preferences are advanced topics—teach the election path and the risk of missing the firm’s cut-off first.

Rights offerings

Rights may trade (when transferable) and expire worthless if unused. The entitlement often depends on a record date position; exercise requires cash and timely instruction. Lapsed rights are a classic “I thought my broker would do it” failure—voluntary means voluntary.

Omnibus Proxy & Beneficial Owner Communications

Voting is a corporate action of rights, not cash. Because Cede is the registered holder, the issuer cannot mail a proxy card to every street-name BO as if they were registered holders. Instead, the industry uses an omnibus proxy and intermediary communications processes so voting authority and materials flow down the stack.

Legal form

Omnibus proxy

DTC (via Cede) executes a proxy covering the bulk position, enabling participants and downstream intermediaries to solicit and process beneficial owner voting instructions under SEC and industry rules.

Communications

NOBO / OBO & vendors

Non-objecting beneficial owners (NOBOs) may be identified to issuers for communications; objecting BOs (OBOs) are not. Vendors and brokers distribute materials and collect voting instructions.

Teaching point for fiduciaries and foundation staff: “I never got the proxy” is often a chain or preferences problem (paperless settings, OBO status, timing)—not proof the issuer failed to hold a meeting. Deep SEC Rule 14a / 14b detail is out of scope here; the ownership stack in the DTC guide is the prerequisite.

Connect modules: Entity titling (trusts, foundations, corps) determines whose instructions and tax ID attach to the account. Street name determines how materials and votes move. Both matter for governance.

Red Flags & Common Failures

  • Using the issuer deadline as the customer deadline — firms need aggregation time; late = default.
  • Treating voluntary like mandatory — no instruction means no tender, not “broker will do the right thing.”
  • Ignoring DRS vs street name — registered holders receive materials differently; mixed holdings confuse clients.
  • Fiduciary accounts without authority maps — who may elect or vote for a trust or foundation must be clear before the cut-off.
  • Assuming CUSIP continuity — mergers and reorganizations retire identifiers; statements need careful reading.
  • Tax lot blindness — corporate actions can scramble lots if systems mis-apply ratios or return-of-capital coding.

Scenario-Based Learning

Work each scenario as if you were ops or counsel advising the account. Tabs switch panels.

Scenario A — “Where is my dividend?”

A retail client holds 500 shares of XYZ in street name. Payable date was yesterday; the account shows no cash. Record date was last week; the client bought 200 of the shares one day before ex-date and 300 two weeks earlier.

Questions for the learner:

  • Which shares are entitled, given ex-date / record-date conventions under T+1?
  • Whom does the client call first—issuer IR, transfer agent, or broker?
  • What would differ if 100 shares were in DRS at the transfer agent?
Key takeaway: Street-name dividends post from the broker’s allocation of DTC credits. Timing complaints start with the intermediary’s CA desk, not the issuer’s shareholder relations line.

Scenario B — Tender cut-off collision

An exchange offer expires Friday 5:00 p.m. New York time at the agent. The client’s broker lists an instruction deadline of Wednesday 2:00 p.m. The client calls Thursday morning after reading the offer press release.

Questions for the learner:

  • Is the broker deadline arbitrary or structurally necessary?
  • What happens if the firm tries a late instruction?
  • How would you document advice to a fiduciary trustee who “just saw the email”?
Key takeaway: Voluntary events are lost on intermediary clocks. Teach clients the firm cut-off as the real deadline.

Scenario C — Foundation never received proxies

A private foundation’s investment committee complains it cannot vote holdings held at a prime broker in street name. The custodian confirms positions at DTC; the foundation is marked OBO on some accounts and paperless on others.

Questions for the learner:

  • Why doesn’t the issuer mail the foundation directly?
  • What is the role of the omnibus proxy?
  • Which preferences (NOBO/OBO, e-delivery) should counsel review?
Key takeaway: Voting rights ride the same nominee stack as dividends. Communications elections and account setup explain most “missing proxy” stories.

Beneficial Owner (BO)

The beneficial owner is the person or entity that ultimately owns the securities and enjoys the economic benefits (dividends, voting rights, etc.), even though the securities may be held in street name through a broker or other intermediary.

Key Characteristics

  • Has economic ownership but not necessarily legal title
  • Receives corporate action entitlements through intermediaries
  • Must make elections for voluntary corporate actions
  • Protected by intermediary agreements and securities regulations
  • May be different from the registered holder on the issuer's books
See also: Street Name, Cede & Co.

Voluntary Corporate Actions

Voluntary corporate actions require the beneficial owner to make an active election to participate. Unlike mandatory actions, if no action is taken, the BO typically receives nothing or defaults to a specific outcome.

Common Voluntary Actions

  • Tender offers (cash or stock exchange)
  • Rights offerings and subscriptions
  • Optional dividend reinvestment plans
  • Stock option exercises
  • Choice between cash and stock consideration

Critical Timing

  • Must respond by specific deadlines
  • Broker cut-offs are earlier than official deadlines
  • Failure to elect may result in lost value or rights
  • Instructions must flow through the intermediary chain
See also: Mandatory Actions, Beneficial Owner

Omnibus Proxy

An omnibus proxy is a legal instrument that allows DTC (acting through its nominee Cede & Co.) to vote shares on behalf of all beneficial owners in street name. It's essential because the issuer only sees Cede as the registered holder, not the millions of actual beneficial owners.

How It Works

  • DTC executes a single proxy covering all street-name shares
  • Participants and intermediaries solicit voting instructions from BOs
  • Voting instructions are aggregated and transmitted to DTC
  • DTC votes the shares according to these instructions
  • Ensures voting rights reach beneficial owners despite street name ownership

Legal Framework

  • Governed by SEC Rule 14a and 14b
  • Industry standard procedures through DTCC
  • Protects beneficial owner voting rights
  • Enables efficient proxy solicitation and voting
See also: Cede & Co., Beneficial Owner

NOBO / OBO Status

NOBO (Non-Objecting Beneficial Owner) and OBO (Objecting Beneficial Owner) refer to a beneficial owner's preference regarding receiving communications directly from the issuer.

NOBO (Non-Objecting)

  • Opted in to receive issuer communications directly
  • May be identified to the issuer for corporate action communications
  • Receives annual reports, proxy materials, and other disclosures
  • Can respond directly to issuer solicitations

OBO (Objecting)

  • Opted out of receiving issuer communications directly
  • Identity is not disclosed to the issuer
  • Relies on broker or intermediary for information
  • May receive communications through the broker's proxy process

Operational Impact

  • Affects how corporate action notices are delivered
  • Influences proxy voting process and communications
  • Can be changed through broker or issuer processes
  • Important for fiduciaries and institutional investors
See also: Omnibus Proxy, Beneficial Owner

Street Name Ownership

Street name ownership refers to the practice where securities are held in the name of a broker-dealer or other nominee rather than in the investor's name. The actual beneficial owner is the investor, but the registered owner on the issuer's books is the broker's nominee.

Characteristics

  • Broker holds securities in nominee name
  • Investor appears as beneficial owner, not registered owner
  • Facilitates trading and settlement
  • Broker provides custody and administrative services
  • Most common form of securities ownership today
See also: Beneficial Owner, Cede & Co.

Cede & Co.

Cede & Co. is DTC's partnership nominee. All securities deposited with DTC are registered in the name of Cede & Co., making it the single largest record holder for most public companies. The issuer and trustee have no knowledge of beneficial owners.

Key Functions

  • Serves as DTC's nominee for book-entry securities
  • Enables street name ownership through brokers
  • Facilitates electronic settlement and transfers
  • Reduces need for physical certificates
  • Acts as the legal holder on issuer records
See also: Beneficial Owner, Street Name

Mandatory Corporate Actions

Mandatory corporate actions are automatically applied to all positions on the record date. Beneficial owners do not need to make an election—they automatically receive the entitlement (cash, stock, etc.) regardless of whether they take any action.

Common Mandatory Actions

  • Cash dividends and interest payments
  • Stock splits and reverse splits
  • Spin-offs and many stock dividends
  • Mandatory mergers and cash-out events
  • Name changes and CUSIP replacements

Operational Characteristics

  • No election required from beneficial owners
  • Automatically processed by intermediaries
  • Cannot opt out (generally)
  • Tax consequences are automatic
  • Simpler processing than voluntary actions
See also: Voluntary Actions, Beneficial Owner
