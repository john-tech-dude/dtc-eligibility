Securities Lending vs Repo

Markets blur the labels. For teaching, separate the economic purpose and the typical legal packaging, then remember both sit on the same book-entry pipes.

Dimension Securities lending Repo (repurchase agreement)
Primary purpose Borrow specific securities (shorts, fails, market making) Borrow/lend cash against securities as collateral
What “moves” Securities out; cash or other collateral in Cash out; securities transferred as collateral (sale + forward repurchase form)
Driver of pricing Specialness / borrow fee for the CUSIP General collateral vs special repo rate
Common docs GMSLA-style master + confirms (varies by market) GMRA / MRA-style master + confirms (varies)

Teaching rule: Ask “are we solving for a security or for cash?” First answer that, then map the legal form and the DTC delivery path.
Securities-driven

Stock loan / stock borrow

  • Locate inventory before short sale
  • Cover settlement fails
  • Lender earns a fee; borrower posts collateral
  • “Specials” when supply is tight
Cash-driven

Repo / reverse repo

  • Finance a long inventory book
  • Invest cash securely overnight/term
  • Rate is the economic headline
  • Collateral schedule and haircuts matter

Why Inventory Moves

In a pure “buy and hold forever in DRS” world, lending markets shrink. In street-name markets they are structural.

  • Short selling: the short seller must deliver shares; borrow supplies the deliverable inventory.
  • Fails: when a seller cannot deliver on settlement date, borrowing can cure the fail (subject to rules and economics).
  • Market making & ETF activity: continuous two-way markets need flexible inventory.
  • Balance-sheet financing: dealers fund positions via repo rather than unsecured borrowing.
  • Corporate actions & recalls: lenders may recall loans; borrowers re-source inventory—ops intensity spikes around events (see Corporate Actions).

Parties & Infrastructure

Names change by desk, but the cast is stable. Beneficial owners often never see the loan ticket; their shares sit in street name at a participant that has (or uses an agent with) lending authority.

Legal Form Through a UCC Article 8 Lens

This is not a full GMSLA or bankruptcy analysis. Teaching goal: connect lending/repo language to the same security-entitlement world as the collateral module.

  • Street-name reality: most “owners” hold security entitlements against intermediaries, not certificates.
  • Delivery matters: book-entry delivery changes who the intermediary shows as entitled—ops must match legal intent.
  • Collateral is not decoration: cash or securities posted against a loan are themselves claims that need clear control and return paths (ACA / control concepts for account-based collateral).
  • Title transfer vs security interest: repo is often structured as sale + repurchase; stock loans use different master terms—read the docs before assuming “just a pledge.”
Counsel note: Characterization (true sale, secured loan, entitlement transfer) is jurisdiction- and document-specific. Teach the questions; do not invent a single global answer.

Rehypothecation & Reuse

Rehypothecation (and related reuse practices) means collateral or inventory posted by a client is used again—by a prime broker or other intermediary—to finance its own book or to lend onward. The collateral guide introduces the term; here it is operational: reuse multiplies claims against the same underlying position stack.

Why firms do it

Balance-sheet efficiency

Funding longs, covering shorts, and lowering external borrow cost. Customer agreements and regulation set the outer fence.

What can go wrong

Chain of reuse

In intermediary stress, customers discover their “held” securities were re-pledged. Teaching cases (e.g., Lehman-era lessons) emphasize documentation, segregation, and who has control—not rumor.

Risk, Fails & Defaults

Risk Plain meaning Ops signal
Counterparty credit Other side fails to return securities or collateral Marks, haircuts, CCP membership, close-out rights
Liquidity / specials Hard-to-borrow spikes fees or freezes locates HTB lists, recall storms
Settlement fail Deliverable not available on settlement date Buy-ins, fail charges, borrow to cover
Operational mismatch Wrong CUSIP, quantity, or account DK / reclaim / break reports
Legal / documentation Master not covering product or entity Onboarding gaps; unauthorized lending

Red Flags

  • “My broker lent my shares without asking” — check account agreements and street-name lending authority; many margin accounts authorize lending.
  • Assuming DRS shares are in the lendable float — DRS is not participant inventory until deposited (see TA / DRS).
  • Ignoring corporate action calendars — recalls and transformations around record dates are normal, not bugs.
  • Collapsing repo and stock loan into one legal story — purpose and documentation differ even when DTC pipes look similar.
  • Treating collateral as free cash — haircuts, segregation, and return rights are the point of the structure.

Scenario-Based Learning

Scenario A — Hard-to-borrow short

A client wants to short a mid-cap equity. The desk reports “HTB — fee elevated.” The client asks why they cannot just “use exchange inventory.”

Questions:

  • What problem is the stock loan solving?
  • Who must deliver on settlement if the short is filled?
  • How does street name at a participant enable the locate?
Key takeaway: Shorts require a deliverable. Borrow supplies inventory; price reflects scarcity, not a DTC “glitch.”

Scenario B — Financing a Treasury long

A dealer holds Treasuries and needs overnight cash. The financing desk “repos out” the bonds. Junior staff call it a “stock loan.”

Questions:

  • Is the primary objective securities or cash?
  • Where might FICC GSD appear in the story?
  • What should junior staff say instead of “stock loan”?
Key takeaway: Cash-driven trades are repo/financing language even when securities move on the same pipes.

Scenario C — Lender recall into a record date

An agent lender recalls a large loan two days before a record date. The borrower must re-source inventory or cover. Ops is flooded with tickets.

Questions:

  • Why might the lender recall ahead of a record date?
  • What corporate-action module concepts apply to who is entitled?
  • What is the borrower’s operational menu?
Key takeaway: Lending life intersects entitlement calendars. Recall + CA is a designed stress, not random noise.

Securities Lending

Securities lending is the temporary transfer of securities from a lender (such as an institutional investor) to a borrower (such as a broker-dealer or hedge fund). The borrower posts collateral (cash or other high-quality securities) to secure the loan and pays a borrow fee.

Key Functions

  • Enables short selling by providing the required deliverable shares
  • Cures settlement fails when a seller cannot deliver on time
  • Generates incremental yield for institutional portfolios (lenders)
  • Marked to market daily to ensure collateral matches exposure
See also: Repo, Collateral

Repurchase Agreement (Repo)

A repurchase agreement (repo) is a transaction where one party sells securities to another against cash, with a simultaneous agreement to buy them back at a specified price and date. Economically, it is a secured cash loan where the seller pays interest (the repo rate) to the buyer.

Core Characteristics

  • Driven by cash financing needs rather than the desire to borrow a specific security
  • Governed by Master Repurchase Agreements (MRA / GMRA)
  • Utilizes general collateral (GC) or specific securities ("specials")
  • Clearing is centralized for U.S. Treasuries through FICC GSD
See also: Securities Lending, Collateral

Rehypothecation

Rehypothecation is the practice where a prime broker or creditor reuse the collateral posted by a client to secure their own borrowings or transactions with third parties. This allows the broker to obtain cheaper funding or facilitate lending to other clients.

Regulatory Framework

  • Subject to strict SEC rules (Rule 15c3-3) to protect customer assets
  • Brokers can only rehypothecate customer assets up to 140% of the customer's debit balance
  • Fully paid-for securities cannot be rehypothecated by the broker
  • Exposes the client to broker counterparty risk in the event of insolvency

Locate Requirement

Under SEC Regulation SHO Rule 203(b), a broker-dealer cannot accept or execute a short sale order in an equity security unless it has "located" the security. This means confirming that the shares are available to borrow and deliver on settlement date.

Key Elements

  • Must be documented prior to executing the short sale trade
  • Prevents "naked short selling" where no shares are available to settle the trade
  • Easy-to-Borrow (ETB) lists can satisfy the requirement for liquid securities
  • Hard-to-Borrow (HTB) issues require a specific locate from a lending desk

Collateral

Collateral represents cash or high-quality securities posted by a borrower to a lender to secure the repayment of a loan or performance of a contract. In securities lending and repo, collateral is critical for mitigating counterparty credit risk.

Key Practices

  • Cash collateral typically earns interest (rebate rate paid to the borrower)
  • Non-cash collateral (such as Treasuries or equities) is held in custody
  • Marked to market daily, triggering margin calls if values diverge
  • Subject to haircuts depending on the credit and liquidity profile of the asset
See also: Haircut

Haircut

A haircut is a percentage reduction applied to the market value of assets pledged as collateral. It acts as a safety buffer for the lender against potential price declines or liquidity issues in the collateral assets before they can be liquidated.

Operational Mechanics

  • Protects the cash lender from market volatility
  • Determined by asset type, maturity, credit quality, and counterparty strength
  • Higher risk or less liquid assets require larger haircuts (e.g., equities vs. Treasuries)
  • Directly affects the leverage available to the borrower in a financing transaction

Settlement Fails

A settlement fail occurs when a trade is executed, but either the seller fails to deliver the securities or the buyer fails to deliver the cash on the contractually agreed settlement date (typically T+1 in the U.S.).

Remediation & Impact

  • Cured by borrowing the security via stock loan to complete delivery
  • Subject to penalties under regulatory frameworks like Regulation SHO
  • Continuous fails may lead to forced close-outs (buy-ins) by clearing agencies
  • Imposes liquidity and operational costs on the failing participant

Cede & Co.

Cede & Co. is the partnership nominee of DTC (The Depository Trust Company). Securities deposited with DTC are registered in the name of Cede & Co. on the issuer's books, making it the single largest record holder for most public companies in the U.S.

Key Points

  • Cede & Co. holds legal title as nominee for DTC participants and their customers
  • Enables electronic, book-entry settlement of trades without physical paper
  • Separates legal registration from beneficial ownership and economic rights

Street Name

Street name ownership refers to the practice where securities are registered on the issuer's books in the name of a broker-dealer or depository (Cede & Co.) rather than the investor's own name. The investor remains the beneficial owner with full economic and voting rights.

Key Benefits

  • Allows for rapid, electronic settlement of buy and sell transactions
  • Allows broker-dealers to easily include positions in client lending pools
  • Streamlines dividend collections and proxy solicitations
