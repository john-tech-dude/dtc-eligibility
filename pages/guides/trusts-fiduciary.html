Overview — Trusts & Fiduciary Structures

A trust is a fiduciary legal arrangement in which a settlor (also called grantor or trustor) transfers legal title to property to a trustee, who holds and manages that property for the benefit of one or more beneficiaries. Trusts are foundational instruments in estate planning, asset protection, and charitable giving. They impose the highest standard of legal care — fiduciary duties — requiring trustees to act with undivided loyalty, prudence, and good faith.

This guide covers the principal trust architectures, the fiduciary responsibilities that govern them, and the mechanics of holding securities in trust through the Depository Trust Company (DTC) and Cede & Co. nominee system. Understanding securities titling — how securities are registered in the name of a trustee in their fiduciary capacity — is essential for compliance with trust instruments, tax reporting, and ensuring that beneficiary rights are properly protected against the trustee's personal creditors.

Settlor / Grantor
Creator of the Trust
The person who transfers assets into the trust and establishes its governing terms. In a revocable trust, the settlor may retain amendment and revocation powers. In an irrevocable trust, the settlor relinquishes all control, which is necessary for estate tax exclusion and asset protection.
Trustee
Fiduciary Manager
The individual or institutional fiduciary (e.g., bank trust department, trust company) that holds legal title to trust assets, manages investments under the Prudent Investor Rule, and administers the trust according to its terms. The trustee owes enforceable fiduciary duties to all beneficiaries.
Beneficiary
Recipient of Trust Benefits
The person or entity entitled to income and/or principal from the trust. Beneficiaries may be current (income beneficiaries) or remainder (principal beneficiaries after a triggering event). Beneficiaries have standing to enforce the trust and demand accountings.
Custodian / DTC Participant
Securities Holder & Record-Keeper
The financial institution (typically a bank trust department or broker-dealer) that is a DTC participant and holds the trust's securities in book-entry form. The custodian's records must reflect the trustee's fiduciary titling to preserve the trust's legal and tax character.

Types of Trusts

Trusts are classified along three principal axes: revocability (revocable vs. irrevocable), timing of creation (inter vivos vs. testamentary), and charitable purpose (charitable remainder vs. charitable lead). Each classification carries distinct legal, tax, and practical consequences. The choice of trust architecture should align with the settlor's goals: control and probate avoidance (revocable living trust), estate tax reduction and asset protection (irrevocable trust), or charitable giving combined with wealth transfer (CRT/CLT).

Charitable Trusts branch into CRT (income to non-charity first, remainder to charity, immediate income tax deduction) vs CLT (charity gets income first, remainder to non-charity, gift/estate tax reduction).

Testamentary vs. Inter Vivos

A testamentary trust is created by language within a will and only becomes effective upon the settlor's death, after probate administration. It is commonly used to provide for minor children, manage assets for spendthrift beneficiaries, or stagger distributions over time. Because it is created by will, a testamentary trust does not avoid probate — the will must be admitted to probate, and the trust is funded through the estate administration process.

An inter vivos (living) trust is created and funded during the settlor's lifetime. It can be either revocable or irrevocable. The primary advantage of an inter vivos trust is that assets properly titled in the trust's name at death pass outside of probate, providing privacy, speed, and cost savings. For incapacity planning, a revocable living trust allows a successor trustee to step in seamlessly if the settlor becomes incapacitated, avoiding the need for a court-appointed guardian or conservator.

Planning Tip: A common and effective strategy is to pair a pourover will with a revocable living trust. The will "pours over" any probate assets into the trust at death, ensuring that all assets are ultimately governed by the trust's terms, even if some were not retitled during life.

Charitable Trusts — CRT & CLT

Charitable trusts are split-interest trusts that satisfy both charitable and non-charitable objectives. They are subject to strict IRS requirements under IRC § 664 and must be carefully drafted and administered:

  • Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT): The trust pays a fixed annuity (CRAT) or unitrust amount (CRUT) to one or more non-charitable beneficiaries for a term of up to 20 years or for life. At the end of the term, the remaining principal passes to a designated qualified charity. The settlor receives an immediate charitable income tax deduction equal to the present value of the remainder interest, subject to AGI limitations. CRTs are particularly effective for donors with highly appreciated assets who want to diversify without immediate capital gains tax.
  • Charitable Lead Trust (CLT): The trust pays income to a qualified charity for a term of years. At the end of the term, the remaining principal passes to non-charitable beneficiaries (typically family members). A CLT is useful for transferring wealth to the next generation with reduced gift or estate tax, because the charitable interest reduces the taxable value of the remainder gift. CLTs can be structured as grantor trusts (income tax deduction to settlor) or non-grantor trusts (no income tax deduction, but estate/gift tax benefits).
CRT/CLT Compliance Warning: Charitable trusts must comply with strict IRS requirements, including the prohibition on self-dealing, minimum payout rates (5% for CRTs), and prohibited investments. Failure to comply can result in loss of tax-exempt status and excise taxes under IRC § 4947. Always work with experienced estate planning counsel and a qualified appraiser for remainder interest valuations.

Fiduciary Responsibilities

A fiduciary duty is the highest standard of care recognized in law. Trustees owe enforceable fiduciary duties to the beneficiaries of the trust, including the duty of loyalty, the duty of care, the duty to act in good faith, and the duty to account. The Prudent Investor Rule — codified in the Uniform Prudent Investor Act (UPIA) and adopted by nearly all states — establishes the investment management standard for trustees.

Duty of Loyalty
Undivided Loyalty to Beneficiaries
The trustee must act solely in the interests of the beneficiaries, avoiding all conflicts of interest and self-dealing. Transactions between the trustee and the trust are presumptively voidable unless expressly authorized by the trust instrument or court order.
Duty of Care
Prudent Administration
The trustee must administer the trust with the care, skill, and caution that a prudent person would exercise in managing similar affairs. This includes proper investment management, diversification, monitoring, and record-keeping. Professional trustees are held to a higher standard.
Prudent Investor Rule
Portfolio-Level Investment Standard
Trust investments must be evaluated in the context of the total portfolio, not individual holdings in isolation. Diversification is required, and risk must be balanced against return objectives. The rule reflects Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT).
Duty to Account
Transparency & Record-Keeping
Trustees must keep accurate records, provide regular accountings to beneficiaries (typically annually), and maintain documentation of all investment decisions, distributions, and trust expenses. Beneficiaries have the right to inspect trust records.
Fiduciary Liability: Trustees who breach their fiduciary duties can be held personally liable for losses to the trust. This includes losses from imprudent investments, failure to diversify, self-dealing, commingling of trust and personal assets, and failure to monitor delegated investment managers. Trustees should maintain comprehensive records, obtain professional advice where appropriate, and consider fiduciary liability insurance.

The Prudent Investor Rule in Practice

The Prudent Investor Rule, as articulated in the Uniform Prudent Investor Act (UPIA) (1994) and adopted in substance by most U.S. jurisdictions, requires trustees to:

  • Diversify investments unless special circumstances (such as a concentrated position required by the trust instrument or tax considerations) warrant a different approach. Undiversified portfolios carry a heightened risk of liability.
  • Consider the total portfolio — risk and return are evaluated at the portfolio level, not for individual securities in isolation. A single risky asset may be prudent if it reduces overall portfolio risk through negative correlation.
  • Balance risk and return — investments must be reasonable in light of the trust's purposes, duration, distribution requirements, and prevailing economic conditions. The trustee must consider inflation, liquidity needs, and tax consequences.
  • Monitor and review — investments must be reviewed regularly and adjusted as circumstances change. A "set it and forget it" approach is a breach of the duty of care.
  • Delegate prudently — trustees may delegate investment management to qualified professionals (investment advisors, mutual funds), but they must select, instruct, and monitor delegates with care. The trustee cannot abdicate responsibility by delegation.
  • Consider costs — trustees must consider the costs of investment management, trading, and custody. Excessive fees that do not produce commensurate benefits may constitute a breach.
Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT): The Prudent Investor Rule explicitly incorporates MPT principles. Under MPT, risk is a function of the entire portfolio's variance, not individual asset volatility. A trustee can prudently hold volatile assets (e.g., emerging market equities, venture capital) if they improve the portfolio's risk-adjusted return through diversification benefits. The key is documentation: the trustee should maintain an Investment Policy Statement (IPS) that articulates the portfolio's objectives, constraints, and risk tolerance.

2 · Open Account — The trustee opens a custodial account with a DTC participant (typically a bank trust department or broker-dealer), providing the trust instrument and tax identification number (EIN).

5 · Manage & Distribute — The trustee manages investments per the trust document, an Investment Policy Statement (IPS), and the Prudent Investor Rule. Distributions are made to beneficiaries per the trust terms.

Securities Titling & the DTC Nominee Structure

For trusts, securities are typically held in a custodial account at a financial institution that is a DTC participant. On the custodian's books, the securities are registered in the name of the trustee in their fiduciary capacity — for example, "John Doe, Trustee of the Smith Family Trust u/a/d [date]." On the issuer's books, however, Cede & Co. remains the sole registered owner, with the custodian's DTC participant account reflecting the trustee's beneficial position.

This layered intermediary structure is identical for individual investors, foundations, and trusts, but the titling is critical for trusts: the trust's name must be accurately reflected on the custodian's records so that all distributions (dividends, interest), proxy materials, corporate actions, and tax reporting (Form 1099, Form 1041) are properly attributed to the trust — not to the individual trustee personally. Incorrect titling can defeat the trust's asset protection, expose assets to the trustee's personal creditors, and create estate tax inclusion issues.

The custodian's records show the trustee's trust account (e.g., "John Doe, Trustee of XYZ Trust"). The Trust is the beneficial owner, entitled to income, principal, and distributions.

Titling Mechanics & Why They Matter

When a trust account is established at a custodian, the account must be titled in the name of the trustee(s) in their fiduciary capacity, with explicit trust identification. The standard format is: 

"John Doe and Jane Doe, Trustees of the Smith Family Trust, dated January 15, 2020"

This precise titling is critical for four reasons:

  • Legal Ownership Distinction: The securities are legally owned by the trustees qua fiduciaries, not in their personal capacity. This distinction prevents the assets from being treated as the trustee's personal property for creditor, divorce, or estate tax purposes.
  • Tax Reporting: The trust's Employer Identification Number (EIN) — not the trustee's Social Security Number — must be used for all tax reporting (Form 1099, Form 1041). Using the trustee's SSN can cause the IRS to attribute trust income to the trustee personally.
  • Succession of Authority: If a trustee dies, resigns, or is removed, the remaining or successor trustee(s) automatically continue to control the account without the need for probate or court intervention, because the account is titled in the fiduciary capacity, not the individual's name.
  • Beneficiary Protection: Proper titling on the custodian's records establishes the trust's beneficial ownership, which protects the beneficiaries' equitable interests and ensures that corporate actions (dividends, stock splits, proxy materials) flow to the trust, not the trustee personally.
Process Map — DTC Securities Transfer into a Trust

Five-step process: 1. Settlor/transferor provides transfer instructions to their broker. 2. Broker and custodian coordinate the DTC transfer (free delivery or DVP). 3. DTC books the transfer from the transferor's account to the trust's custodian account. 4. Custodian credits the trust account with the appropriate fiduciary titling. 5. The trust's investment manager manages the assets per the trust's IPS and Prudent Investor Rule.

Step summary (text)
1 · Instruct — The settlor or transferor provides written instructions to their broker to transfer securities into the trust account, specifying the exact fiduciary titling.
2 · Coordinate — The broker and the trust's custodian coordinate the transfer via DTC, confirming account numbers, CUSIPs, and share quantities.
3 · DTC Transfer — Securities are moved electronically (free delivery or Delivery vs. Payment) from the transferor's DTC account to the trust's custodian account.
4 · Credit — The custodian credits the trust account with the securities, applying the correct fiduciary titling (e.g., "Trustee of XYZ Trust").
5 · Manage — The trustee manages the assets per the trust document, Investment Policy Statement (IPS), and the Prudent Investor Rule.
Titling Error Risk: If securities are transferred into a trust account with incorrect titling (e.g., naming the individual trustee personally rather than in their fiduciary capacity), the assets may be treated as the trustee's personal property, subjecting them to the trustee's personal creditors, divorce claims, estate tax inclusion, and probate. Always verify that the full trust name and "Trustee" or "Trustees" appear in the account title. If an error is discovered, promptly re-register the securities with the correct titling.

Tax Considerations for Trusts

Trusts are subject to a complex web of tax rules. The two primary income tax regimes are grantor trust treatment (where the settlor is taxed on all trust income) and non-grantor trust treatment (where the trust or its beneficiaries are taxed). Estate tax and generation-skipping transfer (GST) tax are critical considerations for irrevocable trusts. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025, permanently raised the estate tax exemption to $15 million per individual ($30 million for married couples) for 2026, with inflation indexing beginning in 2027.

Income Tax — Grantor vs. Non-Grantor Trusts

A grantor trust is a trust in which the settlor retains one or more powers described in IRC §§ 671–679 — such as the power to revoke the trust, the right to income, the power to control beneficial enjoyment, or the power to borrow trust assets without adequate security. Under these rules, the settlor is treated as the owner of the trust assets and is taxed on all trust income on their personal Form 1040. The trust itself does not file a separate income tax return (though it may file an information return).

A non-grantor trust is a trust in which the settlor has relinquished all powers that would trigger grantor trust status. The trust is a separate taxpayer with its own EIN and must file Form 1041 annually. Income distributed to beneficiaries is taxed to the beneficiaries (reported on Schedule K-1), while income retained by the trust is taxed at the trust's highly compressed tax brackets. For 2026, a non-grantor trust reaches the top 37% federal income tax bracket at just $16,000 of taxable income (plus the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax), making distribution planning critical.

2026 Income Tax Brackets for Estates & Non-Grantor Trusts (IRS Rev. Proc. 2025-32):
  • 10% on income up to $3,300
  • 24% on income over $3,300 up to $11,700
  • 35% on income over $11,700 up to $16,000
  • 37% on income over $16,000

These brackets are significantly more compressed than individual brackets, where the 37% rate does not apply until income exceeds $640,600 (single) or $768,600 (married filing jointly) in 2026.

Tax Planning Strategy: Grantor trusts are often intentionally used for their simplicity (no separate trust tax return) and because the settlor's payment of income tax on trust income is effectively a tax-free gift to the beneficiaries — it reduces the settlor's estate without using gift tax exemption. Non-grantor trusts are used when the goal is to shift income to beneficiaries in lower tax brackets, but the trustee must carefully time distributions to avoid the trust's compressed brackets.

Estate Tax & GST Tax Under OBBBA

For irrevocable trusts, one of the primary benefits is the removal of assets from the settlor's estate for estate tax purposes. If properly structured — meaning the settlor retains no powers that would trigger estate tax inclusion under IRC §§ 2036–2038 — the trust assets are not included in the settlor's gross estate at death. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed into law on July 4, 2025, made the following permanent changes effective January 1, 2026:

  • Estate tax exemption: Raised to $15 million per individual ($30 million for married couples with portability), permanently eliminating the scheduled TCJA sunset that would have reduced the exemption to approximately $7 million.
  • Inflation indexing: The exemption will be adjusted annually for inflation beginning in 2027.
  • GST tax exemption: Also raised to $15 million per individual, permanently indexed for inflation from 2027.
  • Annual gift exclusion: Remains at $19,000 per recipient in 2026 ($38,000 for married couples electing gift-splitting).
  • Charitable deduction floor: Individual taxpayers now face a 0.5% of AGI floor for charitable deductions, making trust-level charitable planning more attractive in some cases.

The settlor may allocate GST tax exemption to an irrevocable trust to allow for tax-free transfers to grandchildren and more remote descendants (generation-skipping). Trusts designed to maximize estate and GST tax savings often use "credit shelter" (bypass), "dynasty," or "generation-skipping" trust structures that can perpetually shelter assets from transfer tax across multiple generations.

OBBBA Planning Note: While the $15 million exemption is now permanent (subject to future legislative change), high-net-worth individuals should still consider making strategic lifetime gifts to "lock in" the exemption and remove future appreciation from their taxable estates. Trust structures such as Spousal Lifetime Access Trusts (SLATs), Grantor Retained Annuity Trusts (GRATs), and Intentionally Defective Grantor Trusts (IDGTs) remain highly effective planning tools under the new law.

Advanced Topics

Investment Policy Statements (IPS) for Trusts

A well-drafted Investment Policy Statement (IPS) is the trustee's primary defense against claims of imprudent investment management. The IPS should articulate:

  • Trust objectives: Income production, capital preservation, growth, or total return.
  • Risk tolerance: The acceptable level of volatility and drawdown, considering the trust's duration and distribution requirements.
  • Asset allocation targets: Target percentages for equities, fixed income, alternatives, and cash, with permissible ranges.
  • Constraints: Liquidity needs, time horizon, tax considerations, legal restrictions (e.g., no investments in the settlor's business), and ESG preferences.
  • Benchmarks: Appropriate benchmarks for measuring performance (e.g., a blended benchmark reflecting the target allocation).
  • Review schedule: Quarterly or semi-annual review of the portfolio against the IPS.

Trust Protectors & Directed Trusts

Modern trust drafting often includes a trust protector — an independent third party with limited powers to modify the trust in response to changed circumstances (e.g., tax law changes, family dynamics) without requiring court approval. A directed trust bifurcates trustee duties: an administrative trustee handles custody, record-keeping, and distributions, while an investment advisor or distribution advisor directs investment or distribution decisions. Directed trusts are particularly popular in states with modern trust statutes (e.g., Delaware, South Dakota, Nevada) and can reduce fiduciary liability by allocating responsibility.

Decanting & Trust Modification

Decanting is the process of pouring assets from an old trust into a new trust with modified terms, typically to address changed tax laws, beneficiary needs, or administrative inefficiencies. Most states that have adopted the Uniform Trust Code (UTC) or have specific decanting statutes permit decanting under certain conditions. The UTC also provides for non-judicial settlement agreements and judicial modification when all beneficiaries consent or when the modification is consistent with the trust's material purpose.

While the U.S. trust is a flexible and widely used tool, other jurisdictions have different legal structures for fiduciary holding of assets. For trusts with international investments or non-U.S. beneficiaries, understanding these differences is essential for compliance, tax efficiency, and operational execution.

For U.S.-based trusts holding foreign securities, the titling and tax reporting become significantly more complex. The trust should work with a global custodian that can handle cross-border settlement, withholding tax reclaims (under tax treaties), foreign tax reporting (e.g., FATCA, CRS), and currency conversion. The fundamental principle — that the trust is the beneficial owner — remains the same, but the operational mechanics, regulatory requirements, and tax consequences vary by jurisdiction.

International Planning Tip: When a U.S. trust has non-U.S. beneficiaries or holds foreign assets, consider the interaction of U.S. tax rules with foreign tax regimes. For example, distributions from a U.S. non-grantor trust to a non-resident alien beneficiary may be subject to withholding under IRC § 1441. Conversely, a foreign trust with U.S. beneficiaries may be treated as a "foreign grantor trust" or subject to the throwback rules under IRC §§ 665–668. Specialized cross-border tax counsel is essential.

Trust

Legal Entity / Fiduciary Arrangement
Trust

A legal arrangement where one party (the settlor) transfers property to a trustee, who holds and manages it for the benefit of one or more beneficiaries. The trust is governed by a trust document (or will) and is subject to enforceable fiduciary duties. A trust is not a separate legal entity like a corporation, but it is treated as a separate taxpayer for certain purposes.

Key Elements

  • Settlor (Grantor / Trustor): The person who creates the trust and transfers assets.
  • Trustee: The person or institution that holds legal title and manages the trust.
  • Beneficiary: The person or entity entitled to the trust's income and/or principal.
  • Trust Document: The written instrument that establishes the trust terms, powers, and distribution standards.
  • Corpus / Principal: The assets originally transferred to the trust, plus accumulated gains.
See also: Settlor, Trustee, Beneficiary
Source: Restatement (Third) of Trusts § 2; Uniform Trust Code § 103.

Settlor / Grantor / Trustor

Trust Creator
Settlor

The person who creates a trust by transferring assets to a trustee. The settlor establishes the trust's terms and purposes. In revocable trusts, the settlor may serve as trustee and beneficiary. In irrevocable trusts, the settlor must relinquish control to achieve estate tax and asset protection benefits.

Tax Significance

  • If the settlor retains certain powers (revocation, income rights, control), the trust is a grantor trust and the settlor pays income tax on trust income.
  • If the settlor retains powers that trigger estate tax inclusion (IRC §§ 2036–2038), trust assets will be included in the settlor's gross estate.
Source: Uniform Trust Code § 103(15); IRC §§ 671–679 (grantor trust rules).

Trustee

Fiduciary
Trustee

The person or institution that holds legal title to trust assets and manages them for the benefit of the beneficiaries. The trustee owes fiduciary duties of loyalty, care, and good faith. A trustee can be an individual, a corporate trustee (bank or trust company), or a combination (co-trustees).

Trustee Powers & Duties

  • Invest: Manage trust investments per the Prudent Investor Rule and any IPS.
  • Distribute: Make distributions of income and/or principal to beneficiaries per the trust terms.
  • Administer: Keep records, file tax returns (Form 1041 if non-grantor), and communicate with beneficiaries.
  • Delegate: May delegate investment management to professionals (with due care and monitoring).
  • Account: Provide regular accountings to beneficiaries, typically annually.
Liability: Trustees who breach fiduciary duties can be held personally liable for losses. Professional trustees are held to a higher standard of care than lay trustees.
Source: Uniform Trust Code §§ 801–817 (Trustee Powers); Restatement (Third) of Trusts §§ 76–92.

Beneficiary

Trust Recipient
Beneficiary

The person or entity entitled to receive income and/or principal from a trust. Beneficiaries can be current (receiving income now, also called income beneficiaries) or remainder (receiving principal after a specified event, such as the death of the income beneficiary or the expiration of a term).

Rights of Beneficiaries

  • Right to receive distributions per the trust terms.
  • Right to an accounting and information about the trust's assets, liabilities, and transactions.
  • Right to enforce the trust and seek removal of a trustee for breach of fiduciary duty.
  • Right to challenge a trustee's actions in court.
  • Right to a copy of the trust instrument (in most jurisdictions).
Source: Uniform Trust Code §§ 105, 110, 112 (Beneficiary Rights); Restatement (Third) of Trusts § 82.

Fiduciary Duty

Legal Standard
Fiduciary Duty

The highest standard of care recognized in law. A fiduciary must act in the best interests of the beneficiary with loyalty, care, and good faith. Breach of fiduciary duty can result in personal liability, removal, surcharge (repayment of losses), and in extreme cases, criminal penalties.

Key Components

  • Duty of Loyalty: Act solely in the beneficiary's interest; avoid self-dealing and conflicts of interest.
  • Duty of Care: Act with the care, skill, and caution of a prudent person in similar circumstances.
  • Duty of Good Faith: Act honestly and fairly; do not subordinate beneficiary interests to the trustee's own.
  • Duty to Account: Maintain transparent records and provide regular reports.
Source: Restatement (Third) of Trusts §§ 76–78; Uniform Trust Code §§ 802–804.

Duty of Loyalty

Fiduciary Duty
Duty of Loyalty

The duty to act solely in the interests of the beneficiaries, not for the trustee's personal benefit. Self-dealing, conflicts of interest, and using trust assets for personal gain are prohibited unless specifically authorized by the trust document or court order. Transactions between the trustee and the trust are presumptively voidable.

Prohibited Conduct

  • Selling personal assets to the trust (or buying trust assets) without fair market value and full disclosure.
  • Competing with the trust's business interests.
  • Using trust assets to secure personal loans.
  • Favoring one beneficiary over another without trust document authorization.
Source: Uniform Trust Code § 802 (Duty of Loyalty); Restatement (Third) of Trusts § 78.

Duty of Care

Fiduciary Duty
Duty of Care

The duty to act with the care, skill, and caution that a prudent person would exercise in managing similar affairs. This includes proper investment management, diversification, monitoring, and record-keeping. Trustees with special skills (e.g., professional corporate trustees, attorneys, CPAs) are held to a higher standard commensurate with their expertise.

Practical Applications

  • Investment oversight: Applying the Prudent Investor Rule to portfolio construction, diversification, and periodic review.
  • Property management: Maintaining insurance, paying taxes, and preserving the value of real estate or other tangible trust property.
  • Recordkeeping: Keeping trust assets separate from the trustee's personal assets and maintaining accurate books to support required accountings.
  • Timely administration: Acting within a reasonable time on distributions, tax filings, and beneficiary requests for information.
Source: Uniform Trust Code § 804 (Duty of Care); Uniform Prudent Investor Act § 2; Restatement (Third) of Trusts § 77.

Prudent Investor Rule

Investment Standard
Prudent Investor Rule

A standard for trust investment management codified in the Uniform Prudent Investor Act (UPIA) (1994). It requires trustees to evaluate investments in the context of the total portfolio, diversify, and balance risk and return. It explicitly incorporates Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT) and replaced the older "prudent man" rule that evaluated each investment in isolation.

Key Requirements

  • Portfolio Context: Risk is evaluated for the entire portfolio, not individual holdings in isolation.
  • Diversification: Unless special circumstances warrant, portfolios must be diversified across asset classes, sectors, and geographies.
  • Risk-Return Balance: Investments must be reasonable given the trust's purposes, duration, distribution requirements, and economic conditions.
  • Monitoring: Regular review and adjustment of investments; a "set it and forget it" approach is a breach.
  • Cost Consideration: Trustees must consider the costs of investment management, trading, and custody.
Source: Uniform Prudent Investor Act (1994); Restatement (Third) of Trusts §§ 90–92.

Uniform Trust Code (UTC)

Model State Law
Uniform Trust Code

A comprehensive model state law promulgated by the Uniform Law Commission that provides rules for trust creation, administration, modification, and fiduciary standards. Adopted in whole or in part by roughly 37 U.S. states and the District of Columbia (most recently Oklahoma, in 2025), the UTC modernizes trust law, codifies fiduciary duties, and provides default rules that apply unless the trust instrument provides otherwise. Notable holdouts — including California, Delaware, New York, and Texas — retain their own comprehensive trust statutes that predate the UTC and cover much of the same ground.

Key Provisions

  • Trust Creation: Rules for creating valid trusts, including oral trusts (in limited circumstances) and trusts created by exercise of a power of appointment.
  • Trustee Powers: Default powers of trustees, including the power to invest, sell, lease, and delegate.
  • Fiduciary Duties: Codifies duties of loyalty, care, and good faith.
  • Beneficiary Rights: Rights to information, accounting, and enforcement.
  • Trust Modification & Termination: Rules for non-judicial settlement agreements, decanting, and judicial modification.
Source: Uniform Law Commission; Uniform Trust Code (2000, amended 2005, 2010).

Uniform Prudent Investor Act (UPIA)

Model State Law
Uniform Prudent Investor Act (UPIA)

A model state law promulgated in 1994 that codifies the Prudent Investor Rule for trust investment management. UPIA reflects Modern Portfolio Theory and has been adopted in substance by nearly all U.S. states. It replaced the older "prudent man" rule (from Harvard College v. Amory, 1830) that evaluated each investment in isolation.

Five Core Principles

  • § 2 — Standard of Care: Judges investment decisions in the context of the whole portfolio, not any single holding.
  • § 3 — Diversification: Requires diversification unless the trustee reasonably determines special circumstances make it imprudent.
  • § 5 — Loyalty: Requires investment decisions to be made solely in the interest of the beneficiaries.
  • § 7 — Delegation: Permits trustees to delegate investment functions to qualified agents, subject to a duty to select, instruct, and monitor them.
  • § 9 — Cost Control: Requires trustees to incur only costs that are reasonable and appropriate given the trust's assets and purposes.
See also: Prudent Investor Rule, Duty of Care
Source: Uniform Law Commission; Uniform Prudent Investor Act (1994), §§ 2, 3, 5, 7, 9.

Revocable Trust

Trust Type
Revocable Trust

A trust that can be amended or revoked by the settlor during their lifetime. Also known as a "living trust" when created inter vivos. Assets are included in the settlor's estate for estate tax purposes, and there is no asset protection from creditors. The settlor typically serves as trustee and beneficiary.

Primary Uses

  • Probate avoidance: Assets titled in the trust pass outside of probate, providing privacy and speed.
  • Incapacity planning: A successor trustee steps in automatically if the settlor becomes incapacitated, avoiding guardianship proceedings.
  • Privacy: Trust terms and assets are not part of the public probate record.
  • Ease of administration: Simplifies estate administration by consolidating assets under one instrument.
Source: Uniform Trust Code § 602 (Revocation or Amendment); IRC § 676 (grantor trust — power to revoke).

Irrevocable Trust

Trust Type
Irrevocable Trust

A trust that generally cannot be amended or revoked after it is created. Once assets are transferred, the settlor relinquishes control. Assets are typically excluded from the settlor's estate for estate tax purposes and are protected from the settlor's personal creditors (subject to fraudulent transfer laws). Irrevocable trusts are the cornerstone of advanced estate planning.

Primary Uses

  • Estate tax reduction: Removing assets from the settlor's taxable estate.
  • Asset protection: Shielding assets from creditors, lawsuits, and divorce claims.
  • Charitable giving: CRTs and CLTs are irrevocable.
  • Medicaid planning: In some states, irrevocable trusts can protect assets from Medicaid spend-down requirements (subject to look-back periods).
  • Generation-skipping: Dynasty trusts that shelter assets from transfer tax across multiple generations.
Source: Uniform Trust Code § 602; IRC §§ 2036–2038 (estate tax inclusion); IRC § 2035 (gifts within 3 years of death).

Testamentary Trust

Trust Type
Testamentary Trust

A trust created within a will that becomes effective only upon the death of the settlor, after the will is admitted to probate and the estate is administered. It is often used to provide for minor children, manage assets for beneficiaries over time, or address special family needs. Because it is created by will, it does NOT avoid probate.

Common Applications

  • Minor beneficiaries: Holds and manages an inheritance for a child until they reach an age specified in the will, rather than passing property outright at 18.
  • Staggered distributions: Releases principal in installments (e.g., one-third at 25, one-third at 30, remainder at 35) instead of a single lump sum.
  • Special needs planning: Can be drafted as a supplemental needs trust to provide for a disabled beneficiary without disqualifying them from means-tested government benefits.
  • Pour-over coordination: Often works alongside a "pour-over will," which directs any assets not already titled in a living trust into the trust at death.
Note: Because a testamentary trust arises through probate, its terms become part of the public probate record, unlike an inter vivos trust, which generally remains private.
See also: Inter Vivos Trust, Revocable Trust
Source: Uniform Trust Code § 407 (Trust Created by Will); Restatement (Third) of Trusts § 26.

Inter Vivos Trust

Trust Type
Inter Vivos Trust

A trust created and funded during the lifetime of the settlor. It can be revocable or irrevocable. "Living trust" is a common term for an inter vivos revocable trust. The primary advantage is that properly funded inter vivos trusts avoid probate and provide for seamless incapacity planning.

Funding the Trust

  • Retitling assets: Real estate, brokerage accounts, and business interests must be retitled in the trustee's name to be governed by the trust; assets left outside the trust may still require probate.
  • Beneficiary designations: Assets such as life insurance and retirement accounts typically pass by contract to a named beneficiary and are not retitled into the trust.
  • Certification of Trust: Financial institutions often accept a short "certification of trust" (rather than the full trust instrument) to retitle accounts, preserving privacy.
Common pitfall: An unfunded or partially funded revocable trust does not avoid probate for the assets left outside it — a frequent drafting failure sometimes called the "empty trust" problem.
See also: Revocable Trust, Testamentary Trust
Source: Uniform Trust Code § 401 (Methods of Creating Trust); Uniform Trust Code § 402 (Requirements for Creation).

Charitable Trust

Trust Type
Charitable Trust

A split-interest trust that combines charitable giving with income or estate planning benefits. The two main types are Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRT) and Charitable Lead Trusts (CLT). Both are irrevocable and must comply with strict IRS requirements under IRC § 664.

Why "Split-Interest"

  • The trust's economic value is split between a charitable interest (a stream of payments to charity, or a charity's future remainder) and a non-charitable interest (payments to, or a remainder for, individuals).
  • The settlor receives a partial income or gift/estate tax deduction reflecting only the charitable portion, calculated using IRS actuarial tables and the applicable federal rate under IRC § 7520.
  • Both CRTs and CLTs must be irrevocable and must name a qualified charitable organization under IRC § 501(c)(3) to receive the charitable interest.
See also: Charitable Remainder Trust, Charitable Lead Trust, Charitable Deduction
Source: IRC § 664 (CRT); IRC § 170 (Charitable Deduction); IRC § 7520 (valuation rate); Treas. Reg. § 1.664-1.

Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT)

Charitable Trust
Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT)

A trust that pays income to non-charitable beneficiaries (usually the settlor or family) for a term of up to 20 years or for life. At the end of the term, the remaining principal goes to a designated qualified charity. The settlor receives an immediate charitable income tax deduction equal to the present value of the remainder interest, subject to AGI limitations. CRTs are tax-exempt entities, so appreciated assets can be sold within the CRT without immediate capital gains tax.

Types of CRTs

  • CRAT (Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust): Pays a fixed annuity amount (at least 5% of initial value) each year. The annuity amount does not change.
  • CRUT (Charitable Remainder Unitrust): Pays a fixed percentage (at least 5%) of the trust's fair market value each year, revalued annually. Payments fluctuate with trust value.
IRS Requirements: CRTs must pay at least 5% annually; the remainder interest must be at least 10% of the initial value; no self-dealing; and prohibited investments are restricted.
Source: IRC § 664; Treas. Reg. § 1.664-1.

Charitable Lead Trust (CLT)

Charitable Trust
Charitable Lead Trust (CLT)

A trust that pays income to a qualified charity for a term of years. At the end of the term, the remaining principal goes to non-charitable beneficiaries (usually family members). The CLT is useful for transferring wealth to the next generation with reduced gift or estate tax, because the charitable interest reduces the taxable value of the remainder gift. CLTs can be structured as grantor trusts (income tax deduction to settlor) or non-grantor trusts (no income tax deduction, but estate/gift tax benefits).

Source: IRC § 170(f)(2)(B); Treas. Reg. § 1.170A-6(c).

Form 1041 — Trust Income Tax Return

Tax Form
Form 1041

The U.S. Income Tax Return for Estates and Trusts. Non-grantor trusts must file Form 1041 annually to report income, deductions, gains, losses, and distributions. Income distributed to beneficiaries is reported on Schedule K-1 and is taxed to the beneficiaries. Income retained by the trust is taxed at the trust's highly compressed brackets — the top 37% rate applies at just $16,000 of taxable income in 2026 (IRS Rev. Proc. 2025-32), versus $640,600 for a single individual filer. A 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax applies above that same threshold, producing a combined top federal rate of 40.8% on undistributed investment income — a key reason trustees often distribute income to beneficiaries in lower brackets rather than accumulate it.

Source: IRS Form 1041 instructions; IRC §§ 641–648, 1411; Rev. Proc. 2025-32.

Grantor Trust

Tax Status
Grantor Trust

A trust where the settlor retains one or more powers described in IRC §§ 671–679 (e.g., power to revoke, right to income, power to control beneficial enjoyment, power to borrow without adequate security). Under these rules, the settlor is treated as the owner of the trust assets and is taxed on all trust income on their personal Form 1040. The trust itself does not pay income tax. Many advanced planning trusts (e.g., IDGTs, SLATs, GRATs) are intentionally structured as grantor trusts.

Common Grantor Trust Powers (IRC §§ 671–679)

  • Power to revoke the trust (§ 676).
  • Right to receive trust income (§ 677).
  • Power to control beneficial enjoyment (§ 674).
  • Power to borrow trust assets without adequate security (§ 675).
  • Power to reacquire trust corpus by substituting assets of equal value (§ 675(4)(C)) — commonly used in IDGTs.
Source: IRC §§ 671–679; Treas. Reg. § 1.671-1.

Non-Grantor Trust

Tax Status
Non-Grantor Trust

A trust where the settlor has relinquished all powers that would trigger grantor trust status under IRC §§ 671–679. The trust is a separate taxpayer with its own EIN and must file Form 1041. Income distributed to beneficiaries is taxed to them (Schedule K-1). Income retained by the trust is taxed at the trust's compressed brackets — the 37% rate applies at just $16,000 of taxable income in 2026 (plus the 3.8% NIIT, which kicks in at the same threshold), for a combined top rate of 40.8%.

Source: IRC §§ 641–648, 1411; Treas. Reg. § 1.641(a)-1; Rev. Proc. 2025-32.

Estate Tax

Transfer Tax
Estate Tax

A tax on the transfer of a person's estate at death. Under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025, the federal estate tax exemption is $15 million per individual ($30 million for married couples with portability) for 2026, permanently indexed for inflation beginning in 2027. The top estate tax rate remains 40%. Irrevocable trusts are often used to remove assets from the settlor's estate, reducing or eliminating estate tax liability.

Source: IRC §§ 2001–2056; IRS Publication 559; OBBBA (July 2025).

Generation-Skipping Transfer (GST) Tax

Transfer Tax
GST Tax

A tax on transfers to grandchildren or other beneficiaries who are two or more generations below the transferor ("skip persons"). The GST tax is designed to prevent the avoidance of estate tax by skipping generations. Under OBBBA, the GST exemption is also $15 million per individual for 2026, permanently indexed for inflation from 2027. The GST tax rate is a flat 40%.

Three Taxable Events

  • Direct skip: An outright transfer or transfer in trust to a skip person, such as a grandparent gifting directly to a grandchild.
  • Taxable termination: The termination of an interest in a trust (e.g., the death of a child beneficiary) that leaves only skip persons as beneficiaries.
  • Taxable distribution: Any distribution from a trust to a skip person that is not a direct skip or taxable termination.
See also: GST Exemption, Estate Tax
Source: IRC §§ 2601–2663; Treas. Reg. § 26.2612-1; IRS Form 709.

GST Exemption

Tax Exemption
GST Exemption

The amount that can be transferred free of generation-skipping transfer tax. Under OBBBA, the GST exemption is $15 million per individual for 2026, permanently indexed for inflation from 2027. Trusts can be structured to use the GST exemption to make tax-free transfers to grandchildren and more remote descendants. "Dynasty trusts" are designed to perpetually shelter assets from GST tax across multiple generations.

Source: IRC § 2631; Treas. Reg. § 26.2631-1.

Charitable Deduction

Tax Deduction
Charitable Deduction

An income tax deduction for contributions made to qualified charitable organizations. For CRTs, the deduction is based on the present value of the charity's remainder interest. For CLTs, the deduction depends on whether the CLT is a grantor trust (income tax deduction to settlor) or non-grantor trust (no income tax deduction, but estate/gift tax benefits). Under OBBBA, individual taxpayers face a 0.5% of AGI floor for charitable deductions, making trust-level charitable planning more attractive in some cases.

Source: IRC § 170; Treas. Reg. § 1.170A-6; OBBBA (July 2025).

Trust Securities Titling

Fiduciary Titling
Trustee Titling

The correct format for titling securities in a trust account is: "[First Name] [Last Name], Trustee of the [Trust Name] Trust, dated [Date]". For example: "John Doe, Trustee of the Smith Family Trust, dated January 1, 2020." For co-trustees: "John Doe and Jane Doe, Trustees of the Smith Family Trust, dated January 1, 2020."

Why Proper Titling Matters

  • Legal Ownership: Establishes that assets are held in a fiduciary capacity, not personally.
  • Tax Reporting: Ensures the trust's EIN and tax status are properly applied (Form 1099, Form 1041).
  • Succession: Clarifies who has authority when a trustee dies or resigns.
  • Asset Protection: Protects trust assets from the trustee's personal creditors and divorce claims.
  • Estate Tax: Prevents inclusion of trust assets in the trustee's personal estate.
Source: Custodian account opening procedures; Uniform Trust Code § 801.

Depository Trust Company (DTC)

Central Securities Depository
DTC

A limited-purpose trust company and the primary central securities depository for U.S. equity and debt securities. DTC holds the majority of U.S. securities in book-entry form, eliminating the need for physical certificates. DTC is a subsidiary of DTCC (Depository Trust & Clearing Corporation). Settlement for DTC-eligible securities is T+1 (one business day after trade) as of May 28, 2024.

Why It Matters for Trustees

  • Immobilization: Because DTC holds a global certificate (or tracks positions electronically) rather than issuing certificates to each owner, securities transfer by book entry between participant accounts rather than by physical delivery.
  • Regulatory status: DTC is chartered as a limited-purpose New York trust company and is a member of the Federal Reserve System; it is also registered with, and regulated by, the SEC as a clearing agency.
  • Where the trustee's asset lives: A trustee never holds a DTC account directly — the trust's custodian, as a DTC participant, holds the position on the trustee's behalf, one layer below DTC itself.
See also: Cede & Co., DTC Participant, Custodian
Source: DTC Rules; SEC Release No. 34-96930 (T+1 adoption); DTCC website.

Cede & Co.

Nominee Partnership
Cede & Co.

A New York general partnership formed by DTC to serve as the registered owner of securities on the books of issuers. It holds legal title as nominee for DTC participants and their customers, including trusts, individuals, and institutions. Cede & Co. does not have beneficial ownership — it is purely a nominee. The beneficial ownership chain runs through the DTC participant (custodian) to the ultimate beneficial owner (the trust, via the trustee).

The Ownership Chain

  • Issuer's books: List Cede & Co. as the single registered ("record") holder of essentially all shares held through the DTC system, regardless of how many actual beneficial owners exist.
  • DTC's books: Track how those shares are divided among thousands of DTC participants (banks and broker-dealers).
  • Participant's books: Track how each participant's DTC position is divided among its own customers — including a trust's custodian account, titled in the trustee's fiduciary capacity.
  • Practical effect: Because Cede & Co. is a nominee rather than a beneficial owner, it has no economic interest in the securities and exercises voting or other rights only as directed by the underlying beneficial owners, relayed up through the participant and DTC.
Source: DTC Rules, Article 1; New York Partnership Law; SEC Concept Release No. 34-69225 (proxy plumbing).

DTC Participant

Member Entity
DTC Participant

A broker-dealer, bank, or other financial institution that maintains a securities account at DTC. The trust's custodian is typically a DTC participant, holding the trust's securities in its participant account. DTC participants are responsible for maintaining accurate records of their customers' positions and for settling trades on a T+1 basis.

Participant Obligations

  • Eligibility: Must meet DTC's financial responsibility and operational capability standards, and remain subject to ongoing DTC oversight.
  • Sub-accounting: Maintains its own books identifying which underlying customer — such as a trust's custodial account — owns each position within the participant's aggregate DTC holding.
  • Corporate actions: Passes through dividends, interest payments, tender offers, and proxy materials received from DTC to the beneficial owners it services.
  • Net settlement: Settles trades against other participants through DTC's continuous net settlement system, which nets offsetting obligations to reduce the number and value of actual securities movements.
Source: DTC Rules, Article 2 (Participants); DTC Rules, Article 8 (Settlement).

Custodian

Service Provider
Custodian

A financial institution (typically a bank, trust company, or broker-dealer) that holds securities on behalf of a client (such as a trust). The custodian is a DTC participant and maintains records of the trust's positions, processes transactions (trades, corporate actions), provides reporting, and ensures proper fiduciary titling. The custodian does not make investment or distribution decisions — those are trustee functions.

Custodian vs. Trustee: Different Roles

  • Custodian: Safekeeps assets, settles trades, collects income, and reports positions — an administrative and record-keeping function, typically governed by a separate custody agreement.
  • Trustee: Exercises discretion over investment strategy and distributions and bears fiduciary responsibility to the beneficiaries under the trust instrument and state trust law.
  • Overlap: A corporate trustee (e.g., a bank trust department) may serve as both trustee and custodian for the same trust, but the two functions remain legally distinct, and the fiduciary standards described elsewhere in this guide attach to the trustee role, not the custodial function itself.
See also: DTC Participant, Trustee
Source: Bank custody agreements; SEC Rule 17Ad-10; Uniform Trust Code § 816 (Specific Powers of Trustee).
