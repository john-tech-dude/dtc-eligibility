  • ≥ 6 business days before closing: Participant submits the eligibility package via UW SOURCE or UWC — preliminary offering document, lead underwriter identity, CUSIP(s), principal/share amounts, and interest rates/maturities as applicable (OA Exhibit B). Late surcharges may apply per the DTC Fee Schedule. Municipals: also plan NIIDS trade/settlement data dissemination in UW SOURCE (Exhibit B Note 2; § I.C.3) — separate from eligibility.
  • BEO LOR — U.S. issuers: Final executed PDF of the Letter of Representations (BLOR/ILOR) at least 3 business days prior to closing (Exhibit B).
  • BEO LOR — Non-U.S. issuers: Draft LOR at least 10 business days prior; original signed hard copy by 3 business days prior (Exhibit B).
  • Business day before closing, by 12:00 noon ET: Physical security certificate for inspection, or on closing date by noon ET: Agent FRAC FAST balance confirmation (OA § II.B / Exhibit B).
  • Closing Date: Underwriter and Agent notify DTC of closing by 1:15 p.m. ET (extension requests for ≥ $100M issues by 1:00 p.m. ET) (Exhibit B).
  • Within 10 business days after closing: Agent supplies Agent Confirmation by email to DTC Underwriting for UW SOURCE/UWC issues (OA § I.A.1) — confirms features/attributes vs. underwriter data and offering document.
Note: OA Important Legal Information: DTC may extend deadlines/timeframes it sets, and times applicable to DTC are standards and not deadlines (processing may vary). Still plan to the published standards; late filings can draw surcharges and delay eligibility.
To make a new issue of securities DTC eligible, a DTC Participant (or approved correspondent) must submit required issuer/security data and offering documents through UW SOURCE or Underwriting Central (UWC) — OA § I.A.1 requires submission through those systems at a minimum for full-service eligibility. Standard underwriting time frames appear in OA Exhibit B (at least six business days prior to the Closing Date for the core package).

The Agent for the issue must have a completed Operational Arrangements Agent Letter on file with DTC before the security becomes eligible (OA § I introduction). Agents of new issues must complete a confirmation of securities features/attributes supplied by the underwriter; for UW SOURCE/UWC issues that Agent Confirmation is emailed to DTC Underwriting and must be provided within ten (10) business days after the Closing Date (OA § I.A.1).

Signatures (OA § I.A.6): DTC may, at its option, rely on electronic signatures in many circumstances. Documents from non-U.S. entities are often required with original manual signatures; legal opinions must be hard-copy with original manual signatures.

OFAC: Issuers, Agents, and Underwriters must not conduct transactions through DTC that violate OFAC sanctions. Securities of issuers on the SDN list or otherwise OFAC-sanctioned are not DTC eligible; transfer-agent Agents provide OFAC certifications DTC may rely on (OA § I.D).

Red Flags: DTC's Discretionary Powers

Absolute Discretion on Counsel

DTC reserves absolute discretion to approve or reject the counsel providing a legal opinion. Even a well-known firm can be rejected if DTC determines it lacks sufficient securities expertise or has a conflict of interest.

Deadline Extensions Are Discretionary

OA Important Legal Information: times applicable to DTC are standards and not deadlines, and DTC may extend timeframes it sets. Still plan to Exhibit B standards (e.g., ≥ 6 BD UW SOURCE/UWC package). Late filings can draw surcharges and delay or derail a closing.

Indemnity Letters for Restricted Issuers

Ownership-threshold issuers (REITs, NOL structures, maritime/comms, etc.): OA § I.B.4 requires written assurances that restrictions will not be enforced against DTC/Cede & Co./holders through DTC; DTC may also require an indemnity letter in its discretion. Certain regulated regimes (e.g., gaming) must provide a prescribed indemnity letter. Not always on the standard questionnaire checklist.

OFAC Sanctions Compliance

Issuers, Agents, and Underwriters must not conduct any transaction through DTC that violates OFAC sanctions (OA Important Legal Information + § I.D). If the issuer is on the SDN list, formed in a sanctioned country, or otherwise OFAC-sanctioned, the securities are not DTC eligible. Participants represent screening; transfer-agent Agents provide OFAC certifications DTC may rely on.

Certificate Sizing >$500M

If any single maturity exceeds $500 million, the issuer must provide multiple certificates (one per $500M tranche). A single oversized certificate will be rejected.

Foreign Issuer Legal Opinions

For non-U.S. issuers, DTC may require a foreign legal opinion confirming that DTC's rights as nominee are enforceable under local law. This can add significant time and cost.

DTC Can Discontinue Services Anytime

DTC reserves the right to discontinue providing depository services for any security at any time by giving reasonable notice. This is not merely a theoretical risk — DTC has exercised this right for securities with persistent operational failures, compliance violations, or issuer insolvency.

Non-U.S. Currency Requires Special Rider

OA § I.B.1.b: LOR riders are required for securities denominated or having payments in non-U.S. currencies (list all CUSIPs on the rider). That is separate from the UK/Ireland issuer rider — a euro bond from a French issuer still needs the currency rider even without a UK rider. Settlement of non-USD principal/income has additional OA payment rules (e.g., § I.C.5 / § IV).

Corporate UK Entity Rule 144A + Reg S BEO Put Feature

Scenario A: British Telecom plc — Dual 144A/Reg S Notes with Put Option

The Offering: £400 million notes issued by a UK-incorporated entity, eligible for resale under both Rule 144A to QIBs and Regulation S to non-U.S. persons. Book-Entry-Only with a 3-year put option. Lead underwriter is a DTC Participant. Transfer Agent is Euroclear Bank.

Questionnaire Walkthrough:

  • UK Entity: Check "Yes." OA § I.B.1.b requires a UK/Ireland LOR rider for each U.K./Ireland issuer security (list all CUSIPs on the rider).
  • Article 8 Security: Yes — notes are clearly securities under NY UCC Article 8.
  • Registration: Check BOTH "Eligible for resale under Rule 144A" AND "Eligible for resale under Regulation S." Dual eligibility is common for global issuers.
  • BEO: Yes — Book-Entry-Only (no investor certificates; Cede & Co. holds the entire balance via DTC/FAST). Requires fully executed BEO LOR (BLOR or ILOR) on DTC’s form before eligibility (OA § I.B.1.a).
  • Put/Tender: Yes — 3-year put. The questionnaire form calls for a Tender LOR for put/tender features on certificated issues. This deal is BEO — do not assume Tender LOR is automatic; confirm put-processing rules with DTC (OA special rules for daily/weekly puts) and tender-agent setup.
  • LOR package (OA § I.B.1): (1) Standard Issuer LOR/BLOR, (2) UK/Ireland rider (required), (3) non-U.S. currency rider (required for GBP). List CUSIPs on each rider. For 144A/Reg S: distinct restricted CUSIP(s) (OA § II.A.1.b); OA expressly requires LOR riders for older 144A/Reg S issues — use current DTCC forms for new issues.
  • Signatures / timing: Non-U.S. issuer — original manual signatures commonly required (§ I.A.6); Exhibit B: draft LOR ≥ 10 BD, original hard copy by 3 BD pre-close.
  • CUSIP: Restricted 144A/Reg S CUSIP(s) must be distinct from unrestricted British Telecom notes of the same class.
  • Currency: £ (GBP) — OA requires the non-U.S. currency rider in addition to the UK/Ireland rider (not optional).
  • Agent Letter + Confirmation: Euroclear Bank (as Agent) must have an executed Operational Arrangements Agent Letter on file before eligibility, understand BEO mechanics, and supply Agent Confirmation by email within 10 business days after closing (OA § I.A.1).
  • OFAC: Screen issuer/principals; sanctioned issuers are not DTC eligible (OA § I.D). Transfer agents also provide OFAC certifications.
Key Takeaway: Maximum complexity under OA § I.B: UK/Ireland rider and non-U.S. currency rider are both required for a GBP UK issuer; BEO needs BLOR/ILOR on DTC’s form with Exhibit B timing; 144A/Reg S needs distinct restricted CUSIP(s). Tender LOR is a certificated-form concept — don’t bolt it onto BEO without checking. Start LOR/rider packages early (non-U.S. original signatures and 10 BD draft LOR).
Corporate Rule 144A Non-Investment Grade

Scenario B: Acme Corp. 8.500% Senior Notes due 2030 (Rule 144A)

The Offering: $500 million high-yield notes sold only to QIBs under Rule 144A. Distinct CUSIP from any registered notes. Lead underwriter is a DTC Participant.

Questionnaire Walkthrough:

  • Issue Type: Check "Corporate."
  • Registration: Check "Eligible for resale under Rule 144A of the '33 Act."
  • CUSIP table: One row (term bond, type "T"). At deposit in Cede & Co., securities must be Legally or Contractually Restricted with a distinct CUSIP/CINS from unrestricted securities of the same class (OA § II.A.1.b).
  • LOR / riders: Use BLOR or ILOR for BEO as applicable. OA § I.B.1.b expressly requires LOR riders for older 144A and Reg S issues (list all CUSIPs on the rider). Confirm current DTCC LOR/rider forms for new 144A offerings; issuer also makes OA restricted-security representations.
  • PORTAL: Historically required for non-IG 144A issues. PORTAL is retired; private QIB venues replaced it. Distinct CUSIP remains mandatory.
  • Electronic Submission: UW SOURCE/UWC package per Exhibit B (≥ 6 BD pre-close).
  • Certificate Sizing: $500 million exactly — one certificate is acceptable; the multi-certificate rule applies when a maturity exceeds $500M (OA § I.B.1.d).
Key Takeaway: For Rule 144A, never reuse an unrestricted CUSIP. Distinct restricted identification is non-negotiable under the OA; treat LOR riders carefully for older 144A deposits and always use current DTCC forms for new issues.
Corporate UK Entity SEC Registered

Scenario C: Thames Industries plc — SEC-Registered Notes

The Offering: UK-incorporated issuer doing a registered offering of $300 million notes under Form F-3. Lead underwriter is a DTC Participant. Transfer Agent is Computershare.

Questionnaire Walkthrough:

  • UK Entity: Check "Yes." OA § I.B.1.b requires a UK/Ireland LOR rider (list CUSIPs on the rider).
  • Registration: Check "Registered with the SEC."
  • Foreign issuer: OA § I.B.3 — DTC may require special representations and/or a foreign legal opinion under the issuer’s organizational law (and securities governing law if different).
  • LOR: BEO LOR (BLOR/ILOR) plus UK/Ireland rider. USD registered offering: no currency rider; no 144A/Reg S restricted-CUSIP split unless also sold under those exemptions.
  • Signatures / timing: Non-U.S. — original manual signatures commonly required (§ I.A.6); Exhibit B non-U.S. LOR draft ≥ 10 BD, original hard copy by 3 BD pre-close.
  • Agent Letter + Confirmation: Computershare must have an OA Agent Letter on file before eligibility and email Agent Confirmation of issue attributes within 10 business days after closing (OA § I.A.1).
  • OFAC: Screen issuer/principals; OA § I.D bars eligibility for OFAC-sanctioned issuers. Agents that are transfer agents provide OFAC certifications.
Key Takeaway: Foreign issuers add layers. The UK rider, potential foreign legal opinion, and original signature requirement can each add days to the timeline. Start the LOR process early.
Municipal Serial Maturities BEO

Scenario D: State of Franklin General Obligation Bonds, Series 2025A

The Offering: $250 million GO bond with serial maturities from 2026–2035, issued as Book-Entry-Only. Lead underwriter is a DTC Participant. Transfer Agent is U.S. Bank.

Questionnaire Walkthrough:

  • Issue Type: Check "Municipal." Do NOT check "Limited Underwriting" because principal exceeds $1 million.
  • Article 8 Security: Yes — municipal bonds are clearly securities under NY UCC Article 8.
  • UK Entity: No — issuer is a U.S. state.
  • Registration: Municipal securities are exempt from SEC registration under Rule 3(a)(2). Check "Exempt under Rule 3(a)(2)."
  • CUSIP Table: You need 10 rows (one per serial maturity). Each gets its own CUSIP, interest rate, and maturity date. Type code = "S" for Serial.
  • BEO: Yes — BEO LOR required (OA § I.B.1.a). Check for an existing BLOR under the exact issuer legal name (name change → new BLOR and possibly opinion).
  • Serial CUSIPs: OA § II.A.1 — each municipal serial/term needs a distinct CUSIP printed on the certificate(s).
  • NIIDS (muni data path): In UW SOURCE, the underwriter/dissemination agent must input NIIDS Data Elements and select Disseminate Security Information so key trade/settlement data reach NIIDS subscribers (OA § I.C.3; Exhibit B Note 2). This is separate from the eligibility request — finishing NIIDS does not finish eligibility, and vice versa.
  • Put/Tender: No — standard GO without optional put/tender (no Tender LOR analysis).
  • Agent Confirmation: U.S. Bank (Transfer Agent) emails confirmation of securities features/attributes within 10 business days after closing (OA § I.A.1); Agent Letter must already be on file for eligibility.
Key Takeaway: Serial maturities multiply CUSIP workload; each maturity is its own DTC security. Confirm BLOR name match, run the eligibility package on Exhibit B timing, and complete NIIDS dissemination in UW SOURCE for MSRB/market data — three parallel tracks, not one form.
Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

❌ Missing the Agent Confirmation (or confusing it with the Agent Letter)

Two separate requirements: (1) The Agent must have a completed Operational Arrangements Agent Letter on file before the security can become eligible. (2) For UW SOURCE/UWC new issues, the Agent must email an Agent Confirmation of securities features/attributes to DTC Underwriting within ten (10) business days after the Closing Date (OA § I.A.1). Do not teach a pre-closing Agent Confirmation deadline — that is not the June 10, 2026 OA rule.

❌ Wrong Certificate Size for Large Maturities

If a single maturity exceeds $500 million, you must provide multiple certificates (one per $500M tranche plus remainder). A single $750M certificate will be rejected. This applies to both BEO and certificated issues.

❌ Assuming a BLOR Covers a New Issuer Name

If the issuer's legal name on the offering document differs from the name on the existing Blanket LOR (e.g., due to a merger or reorganization), DTC will require a new LOR or legal opinion. Always verify the exact name match.

❌ Treating NIIDS Dissemination as “Done” for DTC Eligibility

OA § I.C.3 is explicit: NIIDS Data Elements support muni trade reporting, matching, and confirmation; a DTC eligibility request may require the same, different, or additional data. Completing NIIDS input/dissemination in UW SOURCE does not complete eligibility, and completing eligibility does not disseminate NIIDS data. For munis, plan both tracks (Exhibit B Note 2).

❌ Using the Same CUSIP for 144A and Registered Securities

DTC requires distinct CUSIPs for restricted and unrestricted securities of the same class. Using the same CUSIP will cause eligibility rejection and may create settlement failures.

❌ Ignoring Ownership-Restriction Documentation (REIT / NOL / Regulated)

OA § I.B.4: for tax-driven ownership caps (REIT/NOL, etc.), the issuer must give written assurances restrictions will not be enforced against DTC/Cede & holders through DTC; an indemnity letter is often required at DTC’s discretion. Gaming and similar regimes require a prescribed indemnity letter. Don’t assume the blank questionnaire form lists every special letter.

❌ Electronic Signature for Non-U.S. Entities

While the June 2026 OA permits electronic signatures in many cases, non-U.S. entities and legal opinions still require original manual signatures. Using DocuSign for a UK issuer's LOR will result in rejection.

Other Eligibility Paths

The standard new-issue questionnaire is not the only path to DTC eligibility. These alternative paths apply to specific situations.

Older Issue Eligibility

For already issued and outstanding securities (OA § I.A.1 “Older Issue Eligibility Request”), the Participant must present:

  • Completed Older Eligibility Questionnaire
  • Copy of the physical certificate(s) representing the securities
  • Agent Attestation form
  • Submission through UW SOURCE as an “Eligibility Only” request
  • Offering documentation for review (required for all eligibility requests, including older issues)
Further documents (opinions, riders, etc.) may be required after DTC’s review. Common for secondary listings and legacy issues seeking full-service eligibility.

Custody-Only Services

OA footnote 3: securities may be eligible for limited custody rather than full book-entry/nominee service when, for example, they are subject to transfer restrictions and not part of a fungible bulk:

  • Not eligible for Cede & Co. nominee registration or free book-entry services in the usual sense
  • Physical / restricted holdings patterns differ from full-service eligibility
  • This OA document primarily addresses full book-entry eligibility
See DTCC Custody Service materials for operational detail. Custody-only is not a substitute for full-service underwriting eligibility.

Money Market Instruments (MMI)

OA § I.A.2: the general OA arrangements apply to MMI, but MMI are processed differently under the DTC Rules and service guides:

  • Commercial paper, BAs, institutional CDs, short-term notes, and related instruments
  • Securities maturing in ≤ 30 calendar days from issuance/eligibility date are made eligible only under the MMI Program (not as non-MMI)
  • MMI use dedicated BLOR / form letters and IPA pairing rules (see dtcc.com/legal/issue-eligibility)
  • Underwriting help line: (866) 724-4402 (OA-published)
Do not teach “MMI ignores the OA.” Teach OA + Rules/guides + MMI-specific forms.

Segregation Account 100 (Seg 100)

OA § I.B.4: for equity issues with legal/regulatory ownership percentage limits (e.g., Shipping Act / FCC-style foreign ownership caps), Participants should use DTC’s elective Seg 100 tool:

  • DTC does not monitor compliance — it makes the tool available
  • Issuer must provide a Seg 100 letter for equity issues with such limitations
  • Distinct from REIT/NOL tax-threshold assurances / indemnity path (also § I.B.4)
Seg 100 is a post-eligibility compliance tool, not a separate underwriting questionnaire. See DTC Deposits Service Guide for Seg 100 detail.

Retail Certificates of Deposit (E-CD)

OA § I.C.1: Retail (brokered) CDs have additional eligibility requirements; electronic master certificates are optional via UWC:

  • Issuer books must show deposit held for Cede & Co. as exclusive registered owner (FDIC-compatible records)
  • Electronic path: UWC System E-CD Templates → underwriter populates → issuer e-signs prior to closing → vault storage at DTC
  • Non-conforming CDs need a physical master certificate delivered pre-close (noon ET day before per Exhibit B)
  • E-CD BLOR + associated Rider for each base CUSIP (forms: dtcc.com/legal/issue-eligibility)
Contact DTC Underwriting customer help line (866) 724-4402 for E-CD/MMI questions (OA). Do not rely on old 212-855-xxxx extensions without verifying.

Unit Securities — Separation Rules

OA § I.C.2: a Unit has two or more components (debt, equity, and/or warrants) and may separate on or after closing:

  • Immediately separable on closing: Reviewed as separate components; separate CUSIPs per component; separate UW SOURCE eligibility requests; Unit CUSIP not required if immediately separable
  • Separable only after closing: Unit CUSIP + component CUSIPs; eligibility request for Unit and each component; indicate mandatory vs voluntary separation
  • Voluntary post-close separation: Unit/components in FAST; Agent processes separations via DWAC
  • Mandatory post-close separation: Follow OA notice timeframes (see § VI.B.2)
Coordinate CUSIPs and UW SOURCE submissions before closing. Separation design drives eligibility packaging.

NIIDS — Municipal New-Issue Data (?)

OA § I.C.3: the New Issue Information Dissemination Service (NIIDS) is built into UW SOURCE so muni underwriters (and other authorized Dissemination Agents) can meet MSRB expectations for disseminating key new-issue data elements:

  • Agent inputs NIIDS Data Elements in UW SOURCE and selects Disseminate Security Information
  • That action authorizes DTC to send the data to NIIDS Subscribers (market participants who subscribe)
  • If elements are not input or dissemination is not selected, data are not disseminated
  • NIIDS records who input the data and when, and discloses that contact info to subscribers for questions
Critical distinction (OA): NIIDS data support trade reporting, matching, and confirmation of munis. A DTC eligibility request may use the same, different, or additional fields. Completing NIIDS ≠ completing eligibility (and vice versa). Exhibit B Note 2: for munis, required trade/settlement-eligible data are submitted via NIIDS — see § I.C.3. DTC is a conduit only (“as is”); it does not validate NIIDS content.

The Depository Trust Company (DTC)

Definition
The Depository Trust Company

The world's largest securities depository, holding over $100 trillion in assets. (DTC surpassed this milestone in June 2025; the exact figure moves quarter to quarter.) Founded in 1973 and headquartered in New York City, DTC is a subsidiary of The Depository Trust & Clearing Corporation (DTCC).

DTC provides custody and book-entry transfer services for eligible securities. When securities are "DTC-eligible," they can be held, transferred, and settled electronically through DTC's systems rather than via physical certificate delivery.

DTC's nominee, Cede & Co., is the registered owner of record for all securities held in DTC's depository. Beneficial owners hold their interests through DTC Participants (broker-dealers, banks, and other financial institutions).

DTC was created to solve the "paperwork crisis" of the late 1960s and early 1970s, when the surge in trading volume overwhelmed the manual, certificate-based settlement system.

Source: Securities Exchange Act of 1934, §17A(a); DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I. dtcc.com

The Depository Trust & Clearing Corporation (DTCC)

Parent Organization
DTCC

The parent company of DTC and other critical financial infrastructure organizations. A systemically important financial market utility overseen by the Federal Reserve, SEC, and CFTC.

DTCC's subsidiaries include:

  • DTC — Depository Trust Company (securities custody & book-entry)
  • NSCC — National Securities Clearing Corporation (equity trade clearing)
  • FICC — Fixed Income Clearing Corporation (bond clearing)
Source: 17 CFR §240; SEC Oversight. dtcc.com

What does "DTC Eligible" mean?

Eligibility Standard
DTC Eligible

A security that DTC has accepted for its book-entry delivery, settlement, and depository services, enabling electronic trading and settlement.

DTC eligibility is the gateway to the U.S. clearing and settlement infrastructure. Without it, trades would require manual handling of physical certificates, dramatically reducing liquidity and increasing counterparty risk.

Generally, issues eligible for DTC services are those issued in a transaction that: (i) has been registered with the SEC under the Securities Act of 1933; (ii) was exempt from registration under a Securities Act exemption that does not involve transfer or ownership restrictions; or (iii) permits resale pursuant to Rule 144A or Regulation S.

To be DTC-eligible, issues must generally: Be registered with the SEC or qualify for an exemption without transfer restrictions · Not have ownership restrictions conflicting with DTC holding as nominee · Have proper documentation (Letter of Representations) · Have valid CUSIP numbers · Meet special requirements for the security type
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.1. dtcc.com

UW SOURCE / Underwriting Central (UWC)

Electronic Submission Platform
UW SOURCE / UWC

DTC's online underwriting systems — Securities Origination, Underwriting and Reliable Corporate Action Environment (UW SOURCE) and Underwriting Central (UWC). OA § I.A.1 requires Participants to submit eligibility requests through UW SOURCE or UWC at a minimum for full-service eligibility.

What Changes with Electronic Submission

  • Faster processing: Electronic submissions are routed directly into DTC's underwriting workflow, reducing manual data entry delays.
  • Document management: Offering statements, prospectuses, and legal opinions can be uploaded as PDFs rather than mailed or faxed.
  • Status tracking: Submitters can track the status of their eligibility request in real time.
  • Required channel: OA § I.A.1 requires eligibility requests through UW SOURCE or UWC at a minimum for full-service eligibility (not a paper-first process).
  • Exhibit B timing: Standard package (offering document, underwriter, CUSIPs, amounts, rates/maturities as applicable) at least six business days prior to closing; late surcharges may apply.
  • Municipal NIIDS: For munis, UW SOURCE also hosts NIIDS data entry and dissemination (§ I.C.3) — a separate track from eligibility.
Access: UW SOURCE and UWC are available to DTC Participants and authorized users. Contact your DTC relationship manager or DTCC Client Center for access credentials.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements as of June 10, 2026, § I.A.1, § I.C.3, and Exhibit B. bundled OA PDF

NIIDS — New Issue Information Dissemination Service

Municipal Data Service (OA § I.C.3)
NIIDS

An automated system incorporated into DTC’s underwriting system, UW SOURCE, that helps underwriters and other authorized representatives (Dissemination Agents) of newly issued municipal securities meet MSRB requirements for disseminating key data elements of those securities (NIIDS Data Elements).

How dissemination works

  1. The Dissemination Agent inputs applicable NIIDS Data Elements into UW SOURCE.
  2. The agent selects the Disseminate Security Information function.
  3. That selection authorizes DTC to disseminate the data to market participants that subscribe (NIIDS Subscribers).
  4. If the agent does not input the elements or does not choose dissemination, the NIIDS Data Elements are not disseminated.

NIIDS also collects the Dissemination Agent’s name, contact information, and input time, and discloses that information to subscribers so they can follow up with questions.

What NIIDS Data Elements are for

Per the OA, NIIDS Data Elements are certain data elements needed for trade reporting, matching, and confirmation of municipal securities. The detailed element list and procedures are published on DTCC.com (Underwriting / Asset Services). Exhibit B Note 2: for municipal issues, required trade and settlement–eligible data are submitted via NIIDS (refer to § I.C.3).

Eligibility vs NIIDS — separate requests

OA warning: A DTC eligibility request may require the same, different, or additional data elements than NIIDS. Although data can overlap, the requests are separate and distinct. Completion of one does not mean completion of the other.

DTC’s role (liability notice, condensed)

DTC is a conduit of information disseminated via NIIDS. It does not validate or confirm the accuracy of NIIDS Data Elements. Data are provided “as is”; DTC disclaims warranties and liability for adequacy, quality, accuracy, completeness, or timeliness (see full Important Legal Notice in OA § I.C.3).

Desk practice: For a muni closing, calendar (1) Exhibit B eligibility package in UW SOURCE/UWC, (2) BEO LOR if applicable, (3) NIIDS input + Disseminate Security Information, and (4) Agent Letter / post-close Agent Confirmation. Different owners may touch different steps — don’t assume one person “did DTC.”
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements as of June 10, 2026, § I.C.3 (New Issue Eligibility Requirements for Municipal Securities) and Exhibit B Note 2; bundled OA PDF; element list on DTCC.com Underwriting / Asset Services.

Agent Confirmation

OA § I.A.1 (as of June 10, 2026)
Agent Confirmation

For securities issued via UW SOURCE and UWC, the Agent must email DTC's Underwriting Department a confirmation of the securities features and attributes that the underwriter provided to DTC. Agreement among underwriter data, agent confirmation, and the offering document supports accurate asset servicing. This confirmation must be provided within ten (10) business days after the Closing Date.

Do not confuse with the Agent Letter

The Operational Arrangements Agent Letter must be on file with DTC before a security can become eligible (OA § I introduction). Agent Confirmation is a separate post-close attribute confirmation for UW SOURCE/UWC issues — not a 3-business-day pre-close deadline.

What the Agent Confirms

  • Securities features and attributes match what the underwriter supplied to DTC.
  • Alignment with the offering document for accurate ongoing servicing.
Action item: Ensure the Agent Letter is executed and on file well before closing. Calendar the post-close Agent Confirmation email for no later than 10 business days after closing.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements (as of June 10, 2026), § I.A.1; bundled pdf/operational-arrangements.pdf.

Euroclear Bank (as Agent)

ICSD · Transfer / Paying Agent
Euroclear Bank

Belgium-based International Central Securities Depository (ICSD) with a banking license. On cross-border or dual-market offerings it may also serve as Transfer Agent / Paying Agent for a DTC-eligible issue. In that role it is an “Agent” under DTC’s Operational Arrangements — subject to the same Agent Letter and Agent Confirmation rules as any other agent.

Why Scenario A names Euroclear Bank

In Scenario A (UK dual 144A/Reg S notes), Euroclear Bank is the named Transfer Agent. DTC still needs a U.S. book-entry path (Cede & Co. / FAST or physical deposit mechanics as applicable), but the issuer’s Agent for register and confirmation duties can be Euroclear Bank. Eligibility fails if that Agent has not met OA prerequisites.

Three OA requirements (do not skip any)

  • Operational Arrangements Agent Letter (pre-eligibility): Euroclear Bank must have a completed OA Agent Letter on file with DTC before the security can become eligible (OA § I introduction). No letter → no eligibility, regardless of underwriter package quality.
  • BEO mechanics: For Book-Entry-Only issues, the Agent must understand BEO deposit, Cede & Co. registration, and (where used) FAST balance / FRAC confirmation. Investor certificates are not available; the Agent services the book-entry position against DTC’s systems.
  • Agent Confirmation (post-close): For UW SOURCE/UWC issues, the Agent emails DTC Underwriting a confirmation of securities features/attributes supplied by the underwriter — within ten (10) business days after the Closing Date (OA § I.A.1). This is separate from the Agent Letter and is not a pre-close deadline.

Agent Letter vs. Agent Confirmation

  • Agent Letter → executed agreement on file before eligibility.
  • Agent Confirmation → attribute email ≤ 10 business days after closing.
Common mistake: Treating Euroclear only as “the European depository” and forgetting its Agent duties on a DTC eligibility package. ICSD status does not waive the OA Agent Letter or the 10-BD post-close confirmation.
Action item: Confirm early that Euroclear Bank’s OA Agent Letter is current and on file; calendar the post-close confirmation email to Underwriting no later than 10 business days after close. For a deeper ICSD overview, see the Securities Ownership Wizard — Euroclear.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements (as of June 10, 2026), § I introduction and § I.A.1; bundled pdf/operational-arrangements.pdf. Euroclear Bank institutional overview: euroclear.com.

Department
Underwriting Department

Closing Date

Transaction Milestone
Closing Date

The date on which the issuer delivers the securities to the underwriter and receives the proceeds of the offering. All DTC eligibility documentation must be completed before this date.

OA Exhibit B underwriting standards are measured backward from the Closing Date (e.g., ≥ 6 BD for the UW SOURCE/UWC package; LOR and noon-ET certificate/FRAC rules; closing notice by 1:15 p.m. ET). Agent Confirmation of attributes is due within 10 BD after closing for UW SOURCE/UWC issues.

Critical: If the closing date changes (a "delayed closing"), notify DTC's Underwriting Department immediately. DTC may need to adjust processing, and documentation may need re-dating. Per OA Important Legal Information, DTC may extend timeframes it sets — do not plan on extensions.

CUSIP — Committee on Uniform Securities Identification Procedures

Identifier System
CUSIP Number

A unique 9-character alphanumeric identifier assigned to each security issue, enabling standardized tracking across the U.S. financial system. Administered by CUSIP Global Services (CGS), a division of S&P Global.

For DTC eligibility, CUSIP number(s) (and principal/share amounts, rates, and maturities as applicable) form part of the standard UW SOURCE/UWC package under OA Exhibit B — due at least six business days prior to the Closing Date. Late surcharges may apply. Municipal issues also interact with NIIDS trade/settlement data requirements referenced in the OA.

CUSIP Format
9-character code (e.g. "037833100")

First 6 characters: Issuer code · Characters 7–8: Security type and issue identifier · Character 9: Check digit

CUSIP in the Eligibility Package

Per OA Exhibit B (as of June 10, 2026):

  • Standard package (≥ 6 BD pre-close): CUSIP number(s), principal/share amount per CUSIP, interest rates and maturity dates as applicable
  • Legacy form wording: Older questionnaires said 10 BD package / 7 BD CUSIP or corporate “upon pricing” — teach Exhibit B as current
  • MMI Securities: Special CUSIP/MMI program rules apply
  • Rapid Issuance: Expedited assignment paths may exist for programmatic programs

Special CUSIP Requirements

Certain security types have special CUSIP assignment requirements:

  • Rule 144A Securities: Must use different CUSIP/CINS from unrestricted securities of same class
  • Regulation S Securities: Must use different CUSIP/CINS from U.S. registered securities
  • Dual 144A/Reg S: May require single or multiple CUSIPs depending on structure
  • Serial Maturities: Each maturity requires separate CUSIP (e.g., 10-year serial = 10 CUSIPs)
  • Unit Securities: Components may have separate CUSIPs from the unit itself

CUSIP Assignment Timing

  • Standard Assignment: Typically 2-3 business days after application to CUSIP Services
  • Expedited Assignment: Available for urgent issuances (may incur additional fees)
  • Program Assignment: Pre-assigned CUSIP ranges for frequent issuers
  • MMI Assignment: Special procedures for money market instruments

CUSIP Information Required

For DTC eligibility, the following CUSIP information must be provided:

  • Full CUSIP Number: Complete 9-character identifier
  • Security Description: Full name of security as it will appear on certificate
  • Issue Amount: Principal or par amount of the issue
  • Maturity Dates: For serial bonds, all maturity dates
  • Interest Rates: Coupon rates for each maturity/CUSIP
Operational Requirement: CUSIP information that is incomplete, incorrect, or late can result in eligibility denial or processing delays. For Rule 144A and Regulation S securities, failure to use distinct CUSIPs from registered securities is a common cause of rejection. See Operational Arrangements Section II.A.1
Assignment Tip: For complex issues with multiple maturities or features, create a CUSIP schedule early in the process. Verify CUSIP distinctness for restricted securities before submitting eligibility questionnaire.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.1.b. dtcc.com

Debt obligations issued by states, cities, counties, and other governmental entities to fund public projects. Typically exempt from federal income tax under Section 103 of the Internal Revenue Code.

Corporate Securities

Security Type
Corporate Securities

Debt (bonds, notes, debentures) and equity (common stock, preferred stock) issued by private and public corporations.

Corporate issues must comply with SEC registration requirements or qualify for an exemption. If issued under Rule 144A or Regulation S, additional riders to the Letter of Representations are required, and the securities must carry a distinct CUSIP from any registered securities of the same class.

ABS — Asset-Backed Securities

Structured Product
Asset-Backed Securities (ABS)

Financial instruments collateralized by a pool of underlying assets — auto loans, credit card receivables, student loans, or mortgages. Cash flows from these assets service payments to ABS investors.

ABS are typically structured through a special purpose vehicle (SPV) or trust that isolates the assets from the originator's balance sheet. For DTC eligibility, ABS must meet the same general criteria as other securities: SEC registration or a qualifying exemption, and compliance with DTC's Operational Arrangements.

Source: SEC Regulation AB; DTC Operational Arrangements. dtcc.com

CMO — Collateralized Mortgage Obligation

Structured Product
Collateralized Mortgage Obligation (CMO)

A mortgage-backed security that pools mortgage loans and repackages them into tranches with varying risk profiles, maturities, and payment priorities. Created in 1983 by Freddie Mac and Salomon Brothers.

Each tranche receives principal and interest payments in a specific order (the "waterfall"), with senior tranches paid first and junior tranches absorbing prepayment risk. For DTC eligibility, CMOs must be issued by a qualified issuer (typically a government-sponsored enterprise or a REMIC trust).

Source: SEC Regulation AB; DTC Operational Arrangements. CMOs are governed by the Tax Reform Act of 1986 (REMIC provisions). dtcc.com

Article 8 of the NY UCC — "Security" Definition

Legal Framework
Article 8 — Investment Securities

The New York Uniform Commercial Code article governing investment securities. Defines what constitutes a "security" and provides the legal basis for the indirect holding system used by DTC.

Article 8 defines a "security" as an obligation of an issuer or a share, participation, or other interest in an issuer or in property or an enterprise of an issuer that:

  • Is represented by a security certificate in bearer or registered form, or transferable upon books maintained by or on behalf of the issuer;
  • Is one of a class or series of shares, participations, interests, or obligations; and
  • Is, or is of a type, dealt in or traded on securities exchanges or securities markets, or is a medium for investment.

This definition is critical because DTC's book-entry system operates under Article 8. If an instrument is not a "security" under Article 8, it cannot be held in DTC's depository system. New York is the governing jurisdiction because DTC is a New York corporation and the UCC Article 8 framework validates the indirect holding system (where Cede & Co. is the registered owner).

Practical guidance: Answer "Yes" for virtually all debt and equity instruments. Answer "No" only if the instrument does not meet the UCC Article 8 definition (rare).
Source: NY UCC Article 8 (1994 Revised Article 8); DTC Operational Arrangements. cornell.edu (UCC Article 8)

New York Uniform Commercial Code (NY UCC)

State Law
New York Uniform Commercial Code

New York's codification of the Uniform Commercial Code, the body of law governing commercial transactions. Article 8 (Investment Securities) provides the legal foundation for DTC's book-entry system.

Article 8 of the NY UCC was substantially revised in 1994 to accommodate the indirect holding system, where securities are held through intermediaries rather than directly by beneficial owners. Under this system, DTC's nominee (Cede & Co.) is the registered owner on the issuer's books, while beneficial owners hold their interests through DTC Participants. This is legally validated by NY UCC Section 8-503 and related provisions.

Source: NY UCC Article 8 (1994 Revised); DTC Operational Arrangements. cornell.edu (UCC Article 8)

United Kingdom Entity

Jurisdictional Classification
United Kingdom Entity

An issuer incorporated or organized under the laws of the United Kingdom. UK and Ireland issuers require a special rider to the Letter of Representations.

DTC requires a specific rider to the Letter of Representations for issuers organized under the laws of the United Kingdom or Ireland. This rider addresses unique legal and operational considerations, including potential conflicts of law issues and the applicability of UK/Irish insolvency laws.

The UK entity designation also affects how securities are cleared and settled, particularly for cross-border transactions involving Euroclear and Clearstream. A UK legal opinion confirming DTC's rights may also be required.

Securities Act of 1933

Federal Statute
Securities Act of 1933

The federal law governing the initial issuance and registration of securities. Requires issuers to register offerings with the SEC and provide full disclosure through a prospectus. Often called the "'33 Act."

Enacted after the 1929 stock market crash, the Act requires that a registration statement be filed with the SEC before securities can be sold to the public. Several exemptions exist, including Rule 144A, Regulation S, and Rule 3(a)(2).

Source: Securities Act of 1933 (15 U.S.C. §77a et seq.); SEC Division of Corporation Finance. sec.gov

Investment Company Act of 1940

Federal Statute
Investment Company Act of 1940

The federal law regulating companies that engage primarily in investing, reinvesting, and trading in securities. Often called the "'40 Act." Provides exemptions for private funds under Sections 3(c)(1) and 3(c)(7).

Private investment funds (hedge funds, private equity) can avoid registration under:

  • Section 3(c)(1): Limits beneficial owners to 100 persons (or 250 for qualifying venture capital funds)
  • Section 3(c)(7): No ownership limit, but all owners must be "qualified purchasers" ($5M for individuals, $25M for entities)
Source: Investment Company Act of 1940 (15 U.S.C. §80a-1 et seq.); SEC Division of Investment Management. sec.gov

SEC Registered Securities

Registration Status
SEC Registered

Securities issued pursuant to an effective registration statement filed with the SEC under the Securities Act of 1933. The cleanest path to DTC eligibility — no transfer restrictions, single CUSIP, no special LOR riders required.

The registration process involves filing a registration statement (Form S-1, S-3, F-1, etc.), SEC review and comment, and declaration of effectiveness by the SEC before any sales can occur.

Rule 144A — Private Resale to QIBs

SEC Safe Harbor
Rule 144A

A safe harbor under the Securities Act of 1933 that permits the resale of restricted securities to Qualified Institutional Buyers (QIBs) — institutions that own and invest on a discretionary basis at least $100 million in securities of unaffiliated issuers — without SEC registration.

Key Requirements for DTC Eligibility (OA)

  • At initial registration in Cede & Co., securities must be Legally or Contractually Restricted and eligible for transfer under Rule 144A (OA § II.A.1.b).
  • Must carry a distinct CUSIP or CINS different from unrestricted securities of the same class (OA § II.A.1.b).
  • LOR riders: OA § I.B.1.b expressly requires riders for all older issues of Rule 144A (and Reg S) securities — list all CUSIPs on the rider. Confirm current DTCC LOR/rider templates for new issuances; issuer restricted-security representations still apply.
  • PORTAL is retired; private QIB venues facilitate secondary trading. No separate PORTAL application is a DTC eligibility step.
Source: SEC Rule 144A; DTC Operational Arrangements as of June 10, 2026, §§ I.B.1.b and II.A.1.b.

Regulation S — Offshore Offerings

SEC Safe Harbor
Regulation S

A safe harbor under the Securities Act of 1933 for offers and sales of securities outside the United States. Designed to facilitate access to international capital markets without full SEC registration.

Distribution Compliance Period

Regulation S prohibits offers and sales to U.S. persons during the distribution compliance period. For debt securities, this is typically 40 days. For equity securities, the period is one year for non-reporting issuers or six months for reporting issuers. During this period, beneficial interests may only be held through Euroclear and Clearstream as DTC participants.

Restriction
U.S. Person Prohibition

During the compliance period, securities cannot be purchased or held by U.S. persons, except in limited circumstances. This includes direct purchases and indirect holdings through U.S. intermediaries.

Key Requirements for DTC Eligibility (OA)

  • Distinct CUSIP/CINS from unrestricted securities of the same class when held as restricted under Reg S / 144A (OA § II.A.1.b).
  • Distribution compliance period market practice (often ~40 days debt; equity periods longer) constrains U.S. person holdings during the period — coordinate with offering docs and intermediaries.
  • LOR riders: OA § I.B.1.b requires riders for older Reg S (and 144A) issues; list CUSIPs on the rider. Use current DTCC forms for new Reg S deals.
  • Dual 144A/Reg S: Common structure; still needs correct restricted CUSIP identification and applicable jurisdiction/currency riders.
Source: SEC Regulation S; DTC Operational Arrangements as of June 10, 2026, §§ I.B.1.b and II.A.1.b.

Section 3(c)(7) of the Investment Company Act

Exemption
Section 3(c)(7)

An exemption from registration as an "investment company" for issuers whose outstanding securities are owned exclusively by "qualified purchasers" — individuals with $5M+ in investments, entities with $25M+.

Unlike Section 3(c)(1) (which caps beneficial owners at 100), Section 3(c)(7) has no ownership limit but restricts ownership to qualified purchasers. Popular for private equity funds, hedge funds, and other pooled investment vehicles targeting ultra-high-net-worth investors.

OFAC Compliance — Sanctions Screening

Treasury Department
OFAC (Office of Foreign Assets Control)

U.S. Treasury agency that administers and enforces economic and trade sanctions based on U.S. foreign policy and national security goals. OFAC maintains the Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) list and other sanctions programs.

OFAC Requirements for DTC Eligibility (OA)

Issuers, Agents, and Underwriters must not conduct OFAC-violating transactions through DTC. If the issuer is on the SDN list, formed in a sanctioned country, or otherwise OFAC-sanctioned, the securities are not DTC eligible (OA § I.D). Compliance Steps
Practical Screening Checklist

Before sponsoring eligibility: (1) Screen the issuer against the SDN list and other OFAC programs, (2) Diligence ownership/control parties as required, (3) Confirm the Agent can provide required OFAC certification, (4) Document results. Sanctioned issuers cannot be made eligible.

Red Flags

  • High-risk jurisdictions: Issuers from countries under comprehensive U.S. sanctions (e.g., Iran, North Korea, Syria, Russia)
  • Name variations: Issuers with names similar to sanctioned entities require additional due diligence
  • Complex ownership: Shell companies or opaque ownership structures may hide sanctioned individuals
  • Political exposure: Foreign government officials or their family members may be subject to sanctions

Section 3(a)(2) — Government & Bank Securities Exemption

Exemption — Securities Act of 1933
Section 3(a)(2) — Not "Rule 3(a)(2)"

A statutory exemption from SEC registration for securities issued or guaranteed by governmental entities and regulated banking institutions. Note: The DTC questionnaire uses the informal shorthand "Rule 3(a)(2)," but the actual legal provision is Section 3(a)(2) of the Securities Act of 1933 — it is a statutory exemption, not an SEC-promulgated rule.

Securities Covered by Section 3(a)(2)

  • U.S. government securities: Obligations issued or guaranteed by the United States or any U.S. territorial government.
  • State and municipal securities: Obligations issued by any state, territory, or political subdivision thereof (including municipal bonds).
  • Regulated bank securities: Securities issued by national banks (OCC-chartered), state member banks (Federal Reserve-supervised), state non-member insured banks (FDIC-supervised), or savings associations regulated by the OCC.
  • Federally-supervised common trust funds: Interests in collective trust funds maintained by banks for tax-qualified retirement plan assets.
Commonly confused exemptions: Short-term commercial paper (≤9 months) is exempt under Section 3(a)(3), not 3(a)(2). Securities of non-profit religious, educational, and charitable organizations are exempt under Section 3(a)(4). These are separate exemption categories.

A broker-dealer, bank, or other financial institution that has been approved for membership in DTC and maintains an account with DTC. Participants are the direct holders of securities in DTC's book-entry system.

Cede & Co. — DTC's Nominee

Nominee Name
Cede & Co.

The registered nominee of The Depository Trust Company. Cede & Co. appears as the registered owner of record on the issuer's books for all securities held in DTC's depository.

Why Cede & Co. Exists

The indirect holding system — where intermediaries hold securities on behalf of beneficial owners — requires a single registered owner on the issuer's transfer records. Rather than registering millions of individual beneficial owners (which would be administratively impossible), DTC uses its nominee, Cede & Co., as the sole registered owner.

This structure was legally validated by the 1994 revisions to Article 8 of the NY UCC, which explicitly recognized the indirect holding system and the rights of beneficial owners through their intermediaries.

How the Chain Works

  1. Issuer's books: Show "Cede & Co." as the registered owner of 100% of the issue.
  2. DTC's records: Show which DTC Participants hold what positions.
  3. Participant's records: Show which beneficial owners (customers) hold what positions.
  4. Beneficial owner: Holds the economic interest but is not on the issuer's register.

Legal Rights

  • Cede & Co. holds legal title but has no beneficial interest. It cannot vote, receive payments for its own benefit, or exercise put/tender rights except as directed by DTC Participants.
  • DTC Participants (broker-dealers, banks) are the "direct participants" in DTC's system. They hold securities in their DTC account and allocate positions to their customers.
  • Beneficial owners (investors) hold their interests through their broker or bank. They receive proxy materials, income payments, and corporate action notices through their intermediary.

Practical Implications

  • Dividends & interest: The Paying Agent remits to DTC in the name of Cede & Co. DTC then allocates to Participants, who allocate to beneficial owners.
  • Proxy voting: Issuers send proxy materials to Cede & Co. DTC forwards to Participants, who forward to beneficial owners. Votes flow back up the chain.
  • Corporate actions: Tender offers, exchanges, and redemptions are processed through DTC's systems (ATOP, ASOP, etc.) with Cede & Co. as the formal counterparty.
  • Legal proceedings: In class actions or bankruptcies, notice to Cede & Co. constitutes notice to all beneficial holders under NY UCC §8-503.
Key point for issuers: When you sign a Letter of Representations, you agree to recognize Cede & Co. as the sole registered owner and to make all payments and send all notices through DTC. You do not deal directly with beneficial owners for book-entry securities.
Source: NY UCC Article 8 (1994 Revised), §8-503; DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.B.1.d. cornell.edu (UCC Article 8) | dtcc.com

  • Debt securities (bonds, notes): Typically leave blank or enter "N/A" — the "Principal / Offering Amount" field captures the par value. However, if the debt is issued in certificated form with a specific number of certificates, you may enter that number here.

Relationship to Other Fields

  • Share Quantity × Offering Price per Share = Gross Proceeds (for equity).
  • For unit securities (debt + equity combined), enter the number of units. The debt and equity components should each have their own CUSIP and row in the CUSIP Information table.
  • For ADR/GDR programs, enter the number of depositary receipts, not the underlying shares.
Tip: If your offering includes both debt and equity (e.g., convertible bonds), fill in "Share Quantity" only for the equity component. The convertible feature is described in the Issue Description and captured in the CUSIP table.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.C.2 — Unit Securities; DTC Eligibility Questionnaire instructions. dtcc.com

Closing Date — Form Field

Form Field
Closing Date

The scheduled date on which the issuer will deliver the securities to the underwriter and receive the offering proceeds. This is the anchor date from which all DTC deadlines are calculated.

How to Determine the Closing Date

  • Firm commitment underwriting: Closing is typically 3–5 business days after pricing, allowing time for document finalization and settlement.
  • Best efforts / continuous offerings: Closing may be a rolling date or the final date of the offering period.
  • Municipal bond closings: Often scheduled to align with project funding needs and may involve multiple closings ("draw-down" structures).
  • Rule 144A / Reg S: Closing may occur simultaneously with or shortly after the initial sale to QIBs or offshore purchasers.

What Happens on Closing Date

  1. Issuer delivers executed securities (or master certificate for BEO) to DTC or the designated DTC Participant.
  2. Underwriter wires offering proceeds to the issuer.
  3. DTC credits the securities to the Participant's account specified on the questionnaire.
  4. Securities become available for book-entry trading and settlement among DTC Participants.
Critical: If the closing date changes (a "delayed closing"), notify DTC's Underwriting Department immediately. DTC may need to adjust its processing schedule, and the Letter of Representations may need to be re-dated.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.2 — Standard Time Frames; NABL "Demystifying DTC" (2023). dtcc.com

Lead Underwriter — Form Field

Form Field
Lead Underwriter

The investment bank or financial institution managing the offering and acting as the primary liaison with DTC for eligibility processing. This entity is typically responsible for submitting the questionnaire.

Who Qualifies as Lead Underwriter

  • Manager / Bookrunner: The firm that structures the offering, sets pricing, and allocates securities to investors.
  • Sole underwriter: In smaller offerings, a single firm may act as both lead and sole underwriter.
  • Syndicate lead: In larger offerings, multiple underwriters form a syndicate; the "lead" or "left lead" is the managing underwriter with primary responsibility.
  • Placement agent: In private placements (e.g., Rule 144A), the placement agent performs a similar role but does not purchase securities for resale in the same way a traditional underwriter does.
  • Initial purchaser: In 144A/Reg S offerings, the initial purchaser buys from the issuer and resells to QIBs or offshore investors.

DTC Requirements

  • The Lead Underwriter (or its clearing DTC Participant) must be a DTC Participant or have an approved correspondent relationship with one. Only Participants can request eligibility.
  • The Lead Underwriter is responsible for the accuracy of all information on the questionnaire.
  • The Lead Underwriter / Participant must ensure the BEO Letter of Representations meets OA Exhibit B timing (U.S. final PDF ≥ 3 BD pre-close; non-U.S. draft ≥ 10 BD, original by 3 BD).
  • For municipal offerings, the MSRB requires underwriters to comply with DTC's time frames.
If the Lead Underwriter is NOT a DTC Participant: You must identify a Clearing DTC Participant (below) who will handle the book-entry settlement. The clearing participant becomes the direct counterparty with DTC, even though the lead underwriter manages the offering.

Lead Underwriter Contact

The specific individual at the Lead Underwriter who DTC should contact with questions about the eligibility request, document submissions, or closing coordination.

Who to Name

  • Operations / settlement desk: The person who handles DTC eligibility submissions and closing logistics. This is often a Vice President or Director in the underwriter's Operations, Settlement, or Corporate Trust group.
  • Capital markets / syndicate desk: In some firms, the syndicate manager coordinates with DTC.

  • Direct phone number (not a general desk line)
  • Email address
  • Backup contact (optional but recommended for time-sensitive closings)
Best practice: Provide a direct line, not a general operations desk. DTC Underwriting works on tight deadlines and needs immediate access to someone with authority to answer questions and make decisions.

Issuer's Counsel — Form Field

Form Field
Issuer's Counsel

The law firm representing the issuer in the offering. This firm is responsible for drafting the offering documents, ensuring regulatory compliance, and providing legal opinions that may be required by DTC.

Role in DTC Eligibility

  • Opinion of counsel: DTC may require an opinion from Issuer's Counsel confirming that the securities are properly registered or exempt, that they qualify for DTC eligibility, and that the issuer has authority to issue the securities. This opinion must be from outside counsel (not in-house) licensed to practice in the relevant jurisdiction.
  • Letter of Representations: Issuer's Counsel often reviews and advises on the LOR before execution, ensuring the issuer understands its obligations to DTC.
  • Foreign legal opinions: For issuers organized outside the U.S., Issuer's Counsel may need to engage local counsel to provide a foreign legal opinion confirming DTC's rights under foreign law.
  • Indemnity letters: For securities with ownership restrictions (e.g., REITs, maritime issues), Issuer's Counsel drafts the indemnity letter protecting DTC and its Participants.

DTC's Requirements for Counsel

  • Must be engaged in independent private practice — in-house counsel opinions are generally not accepted.
  • Must be licensed and in good standing in the relevant jurisdiction.
  • Must be an experienced securities practitioner familiar with DTC eligibility requirements.
  • Must not have a beneficial ownership interest in the securities and must not be an officer, director, or employee of the issuer.
  • DTC reserves absolute discretion to approve or reject the counsel providing the opinion.

Information to Provide

  • Law firm name
  • Specific attorney name and title
  • Direct phone number
  • Email address
Pro tip: If DTC requests a legal opinion, do not wait until the last minute. Drafting, reviewing, and delivering a securities opinion can take several days. Build this into the Exhibit B package window (standard ≥ 6 business days pre-close) and any non-U.S. LOR timing.

CUSIP Number — Column Explained

Column Field
CUSIP Number

The unique 9-character identifier for this specific security issue. Every distinct tranche, maturity, or series of an offering receives its own CUSIP.

Why This Matters for DTC

  • DTC uses CUSIP numbers as the primary key for all book-entry records. Without a valid CUSIP, DTC cannot create the security in its system.
  • Each row in this table represents a distinct CUSIP — so a single bond issue with 5 different maturities requires 5 rows.
  • For Rule 144A and Regulation S securities, the restricted CUSIP must differ from any unrestricted CUSIP of the same class.
  • CUSIPs are assigned by CUSIP Global Services (CGS), a division of S&P Global. Issuers or their agents must request assignment before submitting this questionnaire.
Pro tip: Verify your CUSIP with CGS before submitting. An invalid or duplicate CUSIP will delay eligibility approval. The check digit (9th character) should be mathematically valid.

Format

9 alphanumeric characters: XXXXXX XX X (6 issuer + 2 issue + 1 check digit). Example: 037833100 (Apple Inc. common stock).

Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.1.b; CUSIP Global Services. dtcc.com

Interest Rate — Column Explained

Column Field
Interest Rate

The annual coupon rate (stated interest rate) payable on the security, expressed as a percentage of par value.

What to Enter

  • Fixed-rate bonds: Enter the stated coupon (e.g., "5.25%" or "5.250%").
  • Variable-rate demand obligations (VRDOs): Enter the initial rate or "Variable" and provide the remarketing agent details in the Contact Information section.
  • Zero-coupon / capital appreciation bonds: Enter "0.000%" or "N/A — Zero Coupon." These instruments accrete in value rather than paying periodic interest.
  • Floating-rate notes: Enter the spread over the reference index (e.g., "SOFR + 125 bps") plus any rate cap or floor.
  • Step-up / step-down bonds: List all applicable rates with their effective dates in the description field.

DTC Processing

DTC uses this rate to calculate income payments distributed through its income collection system. The Paying Agent must remit payments based on this rate. If the rate changes (e.g., for VRDOs or step-ups), the issuer or agent must notify DTC promptly to update its records.

Common mistake: Do not confuse the interest rate with the yield to maturity or the initial offering yield. Enter only the stated coupon rate from the indenture or offering document.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section III.C — Payment Instructions; DTC Underwriting FAQ. dtcclearning.com

Final Maturity / Expiration Date — Column Explained

Column Field
Final Maturity / Expiration Date

The date on which the principal amount of the security becomes due and payable in full. For options or warrants, this is the expiration date.

What to Enter

  • Term bonds: Single maturity date (e.g., "06/15/2035").
  • Serial bonds: Multiple maturity dates within a single issue — each maturity gets its own CUSIP row.
  • Callable bonds: Enter the final maturity date, not the call date. Use the "Type of Issue" column to note if callable.
  • Puttable bonds: Enter the final maturity. The put dates are captured separately in the questionnaire's put/tender feature question.
  • Perpetual securities: Enter "Perpetual" or "No Maturity."
  • Warrants / rights: Enter the expiration date.

DTC Processing

DTC tracks maturity dates to trigger redemption notifications to Participants. On the maturity date, DTC instructs the Paying Agent to remit principal plus any final interest payment. DTC then distributes proceeds to Participants' accounts. If a maturity is extended or amended, the issuer must provide DTC with an indemnity letter authorizing the change.

Important: If the maturity date is amended after eligibility (e.g., via consent solicitation or restructuring), the issuer must notify DTC in writing and may need to provide an indemnity letter. DTC cannot unilaterally change maturity dates on its records.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section V.A — Redemptions; Section I.B.4.b — Revisions of Eligible Securities. dtcc.com

Principal / Offering Amount — Column Explained

Column Field
Principal / Offering Amount

The par value or face amount of this specific CUSIP being offered. This is the amount on which interest is calculated and the amount due at maturity.

What to Enter

  • Fixed principal: The total par amount for this CUSIP (e.g., "$50,000,000").
  • Accreted value bonds (CABs): Enter the initial offering amount (par at maturity). DTC tracks accretion separately.
  • Unit securities: Enter the aggregate principal amount of the debt component. The equity component is listed separately if it has a different CUSIP.
  • Reopened issues: Enter only the new principal amount being added, not the total outstanding.

Relationship to Total Offering

The sum of all "Principal / Offering Amount" values across all CUSIP rows should equal the Issue Principal / Offering Amount stated in the Issue Information section above. DTC uses this figure to verify that the total deposited certificates match the offering size.

Certificate Sizing

Per DTC's Operational Arrangements, if the aggregate principal amount of any single maturity exceeds $500 million, the issuer must provide multiple certificates: one for each $500 million tranche plus one for the remainder. Each certificate is registered in the name of Cede & Co.

Formatting: Use full dollar amounts with commas (e.g., "$100,000,000"). Do not abbreviate (e.g., avoid "$100MM"). DTC's systems parse these values for payment calculations.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.B.1.d.i — Certificate Deposit Requirements; Section I.A.3 — Market Values. dtcc.com

Type of Issue — Column Explained

Column Field
Type of Issue

A single-letter code indicating the structural feature of the security. This helps DTC's systems apply the correct processing rules.

Valid Codes

  • C = Capital Appreciation — The security is issued at a discount and accretes to par at maturity. No periodic interest payments; the return is the difference between purchase price and redemption value. Common in municipal zero-coupon bonds.
  • P = Put — The security contains a put feature allowing holders to demand repayment before maturity on specified dates. Requires a Tender Letter of Representations if certificated.
  • S = Serial — The issue contains multiple maturity dates (serial maturities), with principal amortizing over time. Each serial maturity typically receives its own CUSIP and row in this table.
  • T = Term — The entire issue matures on a single date. The simplest structure; principal is repaid in full at maturity.

Combinations

Some securities may combine features (e.g., a serial bond with a put option). In such cases, list all applicable codes or describe the structure in the Issuer Name / Issue Description field. When in doubt, contact DTC's Underwriting Department before submission.

DTC Processing Impact

The type code determines which DTC processing modules are activated:

  • C (Capital Appreciation): DTC suppresses income payment processing until maturity.
  • P (Put): DTC enables tender/put processing workflows and requires coordination with the Tender Agent.
  • S (Serial): DTC sets up multiple maturity events and sinking fund schedules.
  • T (Term): Standard single-maturity processing.
Tip: If your issue does not fit neatly into one category (e.g., a VRDO with a mandatory tender), describe the structure in detail in your offering documents and contact DTC Underwriting for guidance on the correct code.
Source: DTC Eligibility Questionnaire; DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.C.2 — Unit Securities; Section VII — VRDOs. dtcc.com

Initial Offering Price (To the Public) — Column Explained

Column Field
Initial Offering Price (To the Public)

The price at which the security is first sold to investors, expressed as a percentage of par value (e.g., "99.50%" or "100.00%"). Also called the "public offering price" or "reoffering price."

What to Enter

  • Par pricing: "100.00%" or "$100.00" — the security is sold at face value.
  • Discount pricing: "99.25%" or "$99.25" — the security is sold below par; investors receive the difference as additional yield.
  • Premium pricing: "101.50%" or "$101.50" — the security is sold above par; typical for high-coupon issues in a low-rate environment.
  • Capital appreciation bonds: Enter the initial accreted value (e.g., "$45.00" for a bond that accretes to $100.00 at maturity).
  • Unit securities: Enter the combined offering price of the unit (debt + equity/warrant).

DTC Processing

DTC uses the initial offering price to establish a baseline market value for the security in its systems. This value is posted to Participants' accounts and serves as the starting point for:

  • Position valuation: Participants use this to report holdings values to beneficial owners.
  • Margin calculations: Clearing firms use DTC's posted values for margin and collateral purposes.
  • Corporate action processing: Exchange ratios and proration calculations may reference the offering price.

If no offering price is provided, DTC will post a price of $0.00 until the underwriter provides an initial indicative bid price.

Underwriter Obligation

Per DTC's Operational Arrangements, underwriters should provide DTC with an initial indicative bid price in U.S. dollars prior to the Closing Date. This is separate from the offering price but serves a similar function for secondary market tracking.

Formatting: Use percentage of par (preferred) or dollar price per $100 par. Be consistent across all CUSIPs in the same offering. Include two decimal places (e.g., "99.50" not "99.5").

Qualified Institutional Buyer (QIB)

Institutional Investor Category
QIB

An institution that owns and invests on a discretionary basis at least $100 million in securities of unaffiliated issuers. QIBs include banks, insurance companies, investment companies, and certain registered investment advisers.

Rule 144A Requirements

  • Safe harbor: Rule 144A permits resale of restricted securities to QIBs without SEC registration.
  • $100 million threshold: Must own and invest at least $100 million in non-affiliated securities (excludes affiliates' holdings).
  • Qualified purchasers: Similar concept under the Investment Company Act — entities with $5+ million in investments.

Types of QIBs

  • Banks: With at least $100 million in securities and audited financial statements.
  • Insurance companies: Meeting the $100 million threshold and state regulatory requirements.
  • Investment companies: Registered under the Investment Company Act of 1940.
  • Investment advisers: Registered with the SEC, managing at least $100 million for QIB clients.
  • Employee benefit plans: With at least $100 million in assets.
DTC Impact: For Rule 144A issues, QIB trading occurs on private trading venues (successors to PORTAL). DTC requires a distinct CUSIP and LOR rider for 144A-eligible securities, but no separate PORTAL eligibility verification.
Source: SEC Rule 144A (17 CFR 230.144A); Securities Act of 1933. sec.gov

Blanket Letter of Representations (BLOR)

DTC Agreement
BLOR

Per OA § I.B.1.a: a BLOR is issuer-specific and, except with respect to MMI securities, applicable to all DTC-eligible securities (debt and/or equity) of the same issuer. Once a BLOR is on file, a new BLOR is not required for future non-MMI issuances unless the issuer’s name changes (an opinion of counsel may also be required).

How BLOR Differs from ILOR

  • BLOR: Covers the issuer’s DTC-eligible securities (non-MMI) on an ongoing basis until name change / replacement.
  • ILOR: Discrete issuance only (e.g., trusts). MMI uses dedicated BLOR forms with IPA pairing rules.

BLOR Advantages

  • Efficiency: No need to execute new LOR for each issuance.
  • Speed: Reduces closing timeline — BLOR already on file.
  • Cost: Avoids repeated legal counsel fees for LOR execution.

When BLOR Doesn't Apply

  • Issuer name change requires new BLOR.
  • Change in corporate structure may require update.
  • DTC may request new BLOR if material circumstances change.
  • Some specialized securities (MMI, E-CD) use dedicated forms.
Verification: Before closing, verify with DTC whether the issuer has a BLOR on file. If not, an ILOR will be required. Check that the issuer name matches exactly — even minor discrepancies (e.g., "Inc." vs "Inc") can invalidate BLOR coverage.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements as of June 10, 2026, § I.B.1.a; DTCC issue-eligibility BLOR templates.

Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT)

Corporate Structure
REIT

A company that owns, operates, or finances income-generating real estate. REITs are structured to avoid corporate income tax if they distribute at least 90% of taxable income to shareholders.

Ownership Restrictions

To maintain tax-advantaged status, REITs must comply with ownership concentration limits under the Internal Revenue Code:

  • 5-50 rule: No five shareholders can own more than 50% of the REIT's shares.
  • Diversity requirement: Must have at least 100 shareholders.
  • Asset tests: At least 75% of assets must be in real estate, cash, or government securities.
  • Income tests: At least 75% of income must come from real estate sources.

DTC Eligibility Impact

Because REITs have ownership restrictions to preserve tax status, DTC requires an indemnity letter from the issuer. This letter protects DTC and its Participants in case the REIT's ownership restrictions are violated through DTC's book-entry system.

Seg 100 vs tax thresholds: Seg 100 (OA § I.B.4) is aimed at equity ownership-percentage regimes (e.g., shipping/FCC-style caps) and requires a Seg 100 letter. REIT/NOL tax thresholds use assurances and may use an indemnity letter — don’t conflate the two tools.
Source: Internal Revenue Code §856; DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.B.4. irs.gov | dtcc.com

Net Operating Loss (NOL) Carryforwards

Tax Attribute
NOL Carryforwards

Tax losses that a company can carry forward to offset future taxable income. NOLs are valuable tax assets that can be jeopardized if ownership changes trigger limitations under Section 382 of the Internal Revenue Code.

Section 382 Limitations

  • Ownership change: If one or more 5% shareholders increase their ownership by more than 50 percentage points over a 3-year period, an ownership change occurs.
  • NOL limitation: After an ownership change, annual NOL usage is limited to a formula based on the company's value.
  • Preservation concern: Companies with large NOLs may restrict ownership changes to preserve tax benefits.

DTC Eligibility Impact

Issuers with NOL preservation provisions often have ownership restrictions similar to REITs. DTC requires an indemnity letter because:

  • DTC's book-entry system could facilitate prohibited ownership concentrations.
  • The indemnity letter protects DTC if NOL limitations are inadvertently violated.
  • Ownership restrictions are not always visible in standard questionnaire responses.
Identification: When reviewing issuer materials, check for references to "Section 382," "ownership limitations," or "change of control provisions." These indicators suggest NOL preservation concerns and the need for an indemnity letter.
Source: Internal Revenue Code §382; DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.B.4. irs.gov

Indemnity Letter

Legal Protection Document
Indemnity Letter

A letter in which the issuer agrees to indemnify (compensate) DTC and its Participants for losses arising from the issuer's ownership restrictions or other special requirements. The letter protects DTC from liability when processing restricted securities.

When Required (OA § I.B.4)

For tax-driven ownership thresholds (e.g., REIT/NOL), the issuer must provide written assurances that restrictions will not be enforced against DTC, Cede & Co., or parties holding through DTC. DTC may also require an indemnity letter protecting DTC/Participants/customers, in its sole discretion. Certain regulated regimes (e.g., gaming) must provide a prescribed indemnity letter. Templates are on DTCC’s issue-eligibility site.

  • REIT / NOL: Assurances required; indemnity letter often requested depending on facts.
  • Maritime / FCC / citizenship caps: Often use Seg 100 and/or prescribed letters — confirm path with DTC.
  • Gaming and similar: Prescribed indemnity letter as condition of eligibility.
  • Foreign issuers: May need foreign legal opinions (§ I.B.3) separate from indemnity letters.

Content of Indemnity Letter

  • Indemnification clause: Issuer agrees to indemnify DTC and Participants for losses.
  • Ownership restriction acknowledgment: Issuer confirms restrictions exist.
  • DTC protection: DTC is held harmless for processing through book-entry system.
  • Counsel involvement: Typically drafted by issuer's counsel.
Not on Standard Questionnaire: The indemnity letter requirement is not explicitly listed on the DTC eligibility questionnaire. DTC may request it based on issuer type, industry, or review of offering documents. Always check with DTC if the issuer has ownership restrictions.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section I.B.4; DTC Underwriting FAQ. dtcc.com

Commercial Paper

Short-Term Debt Instrument
Commercial Paper

Unsecured, short-term debt instrument issued by corporations to meet short-term liabilities. Maturities typically range from 1 to 270 days. Commercial paper is usually issued at a discount from face value.

Characteristics

  • Unsecured: Not backed by collateral — relies on issuer's creditworthiness.
  • Short maturity: Usually 270 days or less (to avoid SEC registration).
  • Discount pricing: Sold at discount, redeemed at face value.
  • Low cost: Interest rates typically lower than bank loans.

DTC Processing (OA § I.A.2)

Commercial paper runs in DTC’s MMI Program. The general OA arrangements apply, and MMI are also processed under dedicated DTC Rules and service guides:

  • MMI BLOR / form letters: Use MMI-specific BLOR and IPA forms from dtcc.com/legal/issue-eligibility (not standard corp/muni LOR templates).
  • ≤ 30 calendar days: Maturities this short are made eligible only under the MMI Program (not as non-MMI).
  • Help line: (866) 724-4402 (OA-published Underwriting customer help).
  • Banker's acceptances / short-term notes: Also typically MMI-program instruments.
Registration exemption: Commercial paper with maturity ≤ 270 days often relies on Securities Act exemptions (e.g., § 3(a)(3) commercial paper concepts) — confirm with counsel; DTC eligibility is separate from SEC registration analysis.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements as of June 10, 2026, § I.A.2; DTC Rules / MMI guides; SEC commercial-paper exemption analysis.

Banker's Acceptance

Short-Term Credit Instrument
Banker's Acceptance

A time draft drawn on and accepted by a bank, representing the bank's obligation to pay the stated amount at maturity. Used primarily in international trade to finance transactions.

How It Works

  • Import transaction: Buyer's bank accepts a time draft from the exporter.
  • Bank guarantee: The bank's acceptance makes it a bank obligation, not just a corporate obligation.
  • Discounting: The exporter can sell the acceptance in the secondary market at a discount.
  • Maturity: Typically 30 to 180 days.

DTC Processing

Like commercial paper, banker's acceptances are processed under DTC's Money Market Instruments (MMI) program:

  • MMI forms: Specialized documentation requirements.
  • Bank involvement: The accepting bank must be a DTC Participant or have arrangements with one.
  • Trade finance focus: Common in letters of credit and trade transactions.
Credit quality: Banker's acceptances are considered high-quality short-term instruments because they carry the bank's credit rather than just the corporate credit. This makes them attractive to money market funds.
Source: DTC MMI Guidelines; Uniform Commercial Code Article 3. dtcc.com (MMI)

FAST Agent

DTC Program Participant
FAST Agent

A transfer agent that has executed the necessary agreements with DTC to participate in the FAST (Fast Automated Securities Transfer) program, allowing electronic immobilization and transfer of securities without physical certificate movement.

FAST Program Requirements

To act as a FAST Agent, the transfer agent must have on file with DTC:

  • Operational Arrangements Agent Letter: Basic agreement to process DTC-eligible securities.
  • Balance Certificate Agreement: Agreement regarding certificate balances and reconciliation.
  • Operational Criteria for FAST Transfer Agent Processing: Technical and operational requirements.

How FAST Works

  • Immobilization: Physical certificates are held at the FAST Agent or DTC.
  • Book-entry transfer: Securities move electronically between DTC and FAST Agent.
  • No physical movement: Eliminates risk and delay of certificate shipping.
  • BEO eligibility: Book-Entry-Only securities typically use FAST Agents for certificate immobilization.
Coordination: The Transfer Agent listed on the eligibility questionnaire must be a FAST Agent (or have executed the necessary agreements) for the issue to be eligible for FAST processing. If not, DTC may require physical certificate delivery.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.B; DTC FAST Program Guidelines. dtcc.com

Serial Maturities

Bond Structure
Serial Maturities

A bond structure where principal is repaid in multiple installments over time, with different maturities for each installment. Each serial maturity typically has its own CUSIP and may have different interest rates.

Serial vs. Term Bonds

  • Serial bonds: Multiple maturity dates (e.g., 2026, 2027, 2028...2035). Principal amortizes over time.
  • Term bonds: Single maturity date. Entire principal repaid at once (bullet maturity).

DTC CUSIP Requirements

Each serial maturity receives its own CUSIP number and row in the CUSIP Information table:

  • Type code "S": Used for serial maturities in the CUSIP table.
  • Individual interest rates: Each maturity may have its own interest rate.
  • Separate principal amounts: Each maturity specifies the principal repayment amount.
  • Example: A 2026–2035 serial issue requires 10 CUSIPs and 10 table rows.
Municipal bonds: Serial structures are common for municipal bonds to match revenue streams with debt service. Corporate bonds typically use term structures, but serial bonds are used for project financing where cash flows match amortization schedules.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.2; CUSIP Services Manual. cusip.com

Term Bond

Bond Structure
Term Bond

A bond with a single maturity date when the entire principal amount is repaid. Unlike serial bonds, which amortize over time, term bonds are bullet maturities with one principal repayment date.

Characteristics

  • Single maturity: Entire principal due at one specified date.
  • Type code "T": Used in DTC's CUSIP table for term bonds.
  • One CUSIP: Term bonds typically require only one CUSIP (unless multiple tranches).
  • Corporate bonds: Most corporate bonds are term structures.

Advantages and Disadvantages

  • Issuer advantage: No principal payments until final maturity — full use of proceeds.
  • Investor advantage: Known repayment date and full principal at maturity.
  • Refinancing risk: Large balloon payment at maturity creates refinancing risk.
  • Serial comparison: Serial bonds reduce refinancing risk but require ongoing principal payments.
DTC efficiency: Term bonds are simpler for DTC processing — one CUSIP, one row in the CUSIP table, one set of certificates. Serial bonds multiply administrative workload because each maturity is treated as a separate security.
Source: DTC Operational Arrangements, Section II.A.2; Municipal Securities Rulemaking Board. msrb.org

Beneficial Owner

Ownership Concept
Beneficial Owner

The person or entity that enjoys the benefits of ownership (economic interest, voting rights, dividend entitlement) even though the security may be registered in the name of another (such as Cede & Co. or a broker).

Indirect Holding System

Under the modern securities system (post-1994 UCC Article 8 revisions):

  • Registered owner: Cede & Co. appears on issuer's books as owner of record.
  • Intermediaries: DTC Participants (brokers, banks) hold positions with DTC.
  • Beneficial owners: Investors hold economic interests through their intermediaries.
  • Chain of title: Issuer → Cede & Co. → DTC → Participants → Beneficial owners.

Rights of Beneficial Owners

  • Economic benefits: Entitled to dividends, interest, and principal payments.
  • Voting rights: Can vote through their intermediary (proxy voting chain).
  • Corporate actions: Can participate in tender offers, exchanges, and rights offerings.
  • Sale proceeds: Can sell their position through their broker.
Legal protection: The 1994 revisions to NY UCC Article 8 specifically validated the rights of beneficial owners through intermediaries. Notice to Cede & Co. constitutes notice to all beneficial owners under §8-503.
Source: NY UCC Article 8 (1994 Revised), §8-503. cornell.edu

Nominee

Legal Concept
Nominee

A person or entity that holds legal title to securities on behalf of another, who is the beneficial owner. The nominee has no beneficial interest and holds the securities only as a matter of convenience or legal structure.

Cede & Co. as Nominee

DTC's nominee, Cede & Co., is the classic example:

  • Legal title: Cede & Co. is registered owner on issuer's books.
  • No beneficial interest: Cede & Co. cannot benefit from ownership.
  • Administrative convenience: Eliminates need to register millions of individual owners.
  • Indirect holding system: Made possible by 1994 UCC Article 8 revisions.

Other Nominee Arrangements

  • Street name registration: Brokers hold securities in "street name" (nominee) for customers.
  • Trust nominees: Trust companies may hold assets as nominees for trusts.
  • Custody nominees: Custodians may hold securities as nominees for asset managers.

Foreign Issuers

For non-U.S. issuers, DTC may require a foreign legal opinion confirming that:

  • DTC's rights as nominee are enforceable under local law.
  • The indirect holding system is recognized.
  • Beneficial owners' rights through intermediaries are protected.
Legal distinction: The nominee holds legal title but not equitable title. Beneficial owners hold equitable title (economic interest). This separation is fundamental to modern securities holding systems.
Source: NY UCC Article 8 (1994 Revised); Uniform Commercial Code. cornell.edu
