Bill of Exchange (Draft)

Negotiable Instrument
Bill of Exchange / Draft

An unconditional written order from one party (the drawer) to another (the drawee), directing the drawee to pay a specified sum of money to a third party (the payee) either on demand or at a determinable future date.

Key Characteristics

  • Unconditional: The order to pay must not depend on any other event or condition.
  • In writing: Must be a physical or electronic document with the required formal elements.
  • Three parties: Drawer (seller), Drawee (buyer/buyer's bank), and Payee (usually the drawer or their bank).
  • Negotiable: Can be transferred by endorsement, making it a liquid instrument in trade finance.

Types of Drafts

  • Sight Draft: Payable on demand, upon presentation to the drawee. Used in D/P (Documents Against Payment) collections.
  • Time Draft / Usance Draft: Payable at a specified future date (e.g., 30, 60, 90 days after sight or after date). Used in D/A (Documents Against Acceptance) collections.
Trade Finance Tip: In a D/P collection, the buyer cannot take possession of the shipping documents (and thus the goods) until they pay the sight draft. This gives the seller significant security.
Source: Uniform Customs and Practice for Documentary Collections (UCP 600), ICC Publication No. 600; UNCITRAL Convention on International Bills of Exchange and International Promissory Notes (1988).

Zions Bank — International Trade Finance

Collecting Bank / Remitting Bank
Zions Bank

A U.S. regional bank headquartered in Salt Lake City, Utah, providing international trade finance services including documentary collections, letters of credit, and foreign exchange.

Role in Documentary Collection

  • Remitting Bank: The bank that receives the collection documents from the seller (drawer) and forwards them to the buyer's bank (collecting bank) in the buyer's country.
  • Agent: Acts solely as an intermediary — assumes no responsibility for payment. The bank's role is limited to presenting documents and collecting funds.
Important: Under UCP 600, banks deal in documents, not goods. Zions Bank is not responsible for the quality, quantity, or condition of the underlying merchandise. Its obligation is strictly to follow the collection instructions.

Drawer (Customer / Seller)

Party to the Draft
Drawer

The party (typically the seller/exporter) who creates and signs the Bill of Exchange, ordering the drawee to pay a specified amount. The drawer is also the beneficiary of the collection.

Responsibilities of the Drawer

  • Prepares and signs the Bill of Exchange and collection instructions accurately.
  • Provides complete and correct shipping documents to the remitting bank.
  • Ensures the draft amount matches the invoice and contract terms.
  • Specifies clear instructions on how documents are to be released (D/P or D/A).

Relationship to Other Parties

  • Drawer → Remitting Bank: Submits documents and draft for collection.
  • Drawer → Drawee: The underlying commercial relationship (sale of goods).
  • Drawer as Payee: Often the drawer is also the payee on the draft — the party to whom payment is made.
Risk Note: In a D/P collection, the drawer retains control of the goods until payment. In a D/A collection, the drawer releases documents against acceptance of a time draft, bearing the credit risk of the buyer until maturity.
Source: UCP 600, Article 2 (Definitions); Uniform Commercial Code (UCC) Article 3 (Negotiable Instruments).

Drawee (Buyer)

Party to the Draft
Drawee

The party (typically the buyer/importer) on whom the Bill of Exchange is drawn — the party ordered to make payment. In trade collections, this is the importer who purchased the goods.

Drawee's Role in D/P vs. D/A

  • D/P (Documents Against Payment): The drawee must pay the sight draft in full before receiving the shipping documents and taking possession of the goods.
  • D/A (Documents Against Acceptance): The drawee accepts (signs) the time draft, promising to pay at a future date, and receives the documents immediately. Payment is due later.

Drawee Bank

The drawee's bank (buyer's bank) acts as the collecting bank or presenting bank — the bank in the buyer's country that presents the documents and draft to the drawee for payment or acceptance.

Credit Risk: D/P is safer for the seller because the buyer cannot obtain the goods without paying. D/A is riskier for the seller but more convenient for the buyer, who can sell the goods before payment is due.
Source: UCP 600, Articles 7-8; ICC Uniform Rules for Collections (URC 522).

Drawee Bank (Buyer's Bank)

Collecting Bank / Presenting Bank
Drawee Bank

The bank in the buyer's country that receives the collection documents from the remitting bank, presents them to the drawee (buyer), and collects payment or acceptance.

Functions of the Drawee Bank

  • Receipt: Receives the collection order, draft, and documents from the remitting bank.
  • Presentation: Presents the draft and documents to the buyer for payment (D/P) or acceptance (D/A).
  • Collection: Collects funds from the buyer and remits them to the remitting bank.
  • Document Release: Releases documents to the buyer only upon payment or acceptance, per the collection instructions.

Liability

Under URC 522, the collecting bank acts as an agent only and assumes no liability for payment unless it has specifically guaranteed payment (avalized the draft). Its duty is to follow the collection instructions in good faith and with reasonable care.

Practical Note: The seller should verify that the drawee bank is reputable and has a correspondent relationship with the remitting bank. Using a bank known to the buyer can facilitate smoother collection.
Source: URC 522 (Uniform Rules for Collections), ICC Publication No. 522, Articles 1-26.

Draft — Document in Collection

Collection Document
Draft

The Bill of Exchange included in the documentary collection. It serves as the formal demand for payment and is typically accompanied by shipping documents (Bill of Lading, Invoice, etc.).

Why the Draft Matters

  • Legal instrument: Creates an enforceable obligation to pay under negotiable instruments law.
  • Collection trigger: The collecting bank presents the draft to the buyer for payment or acceptance.
  • Evidence of debt: An accepted time draft becomes a trade acceptance — a marketable instrument that the seller can discount or sell.
Document Check: The draft must be properly signed by the drawer, show the correct amount, and specify the drawee. Mismatches between the draft and invoice will cause delays or rejection by the collecting bank.
Source: URC 522, Article 4 (Form of Collection); UCC Article 3, Section 3-104.

Bill of Lading (BoL)

Transport Document
Bill of Lading

A document issued by a carrier to a shipper, acknowledging receipt of cargo for shipment. It serves as a contract of carriage, a receipt for goods, and — in its original form — a document of title enabling the holder to take possession of the goods.

Types of Bills of Lading

  • Original BoL: A document of title. The buyer must present the original to claim goods at the destination port. This is the most secure form for the seller.
  • Non-Negotiable (N/N) BoL: A receipt only, not a document of title. Named consignee can claim goods without presenting the original. Less secure for the seller.
  • Sea Waybill: Similar to N/N BoL but used for sea transport. The consignee is named and no original is needed for delivery.
  • Air Waybill: Used for air freight. Always non-negotiable; the consignee is named at issuance.

Role in Documentary Collection

The Bill of Lading is the most critical shipping document in a collection. The seller retains control of the goods by retaining the original BoL until payment is received. The collecting bank releases the original BoL to the buyer only upon payment (D/P) or acceptance (D/A).

Security Tip: Always use an Original (negotiable) Bill of Lading for D/P collections. The N/N BoL does not give the seller control over the goods once they arrive at the destination.
Source: URC 522, Article 4; Hague-Visby Rules (Carriage of Goods by Sea).

Tenor — Payment Timing

Draft Term
Tenor

The period of time after which a draft becomes due and payable. It determines when the drawee must pay.

Common Tenor Expressions

  • "At sight" — Payable immediately upon presentation. Used in D/P collections. No delay; the buyer pays to get the documents.
  • "30/60/90 days after sight" — Payable 30, 60, or 90 days after the drawee has seen (accepted) the draft. The clock starts when the buyer acknowledges the draft.
  • "30/60/90 days after date" — Payable 30, 60, or 90 days after the date written on the draft. The clock starts from the draft date, regardless of when the buyer sees it.
  • "On [specific date]" — Payable on a fixed calendar date.

Practical Impact

The tenor determines the seller's cash flow and the buyer's financing period. A 90-day usance draft gives the buyer three months to sell the goods before payment is due, but exposes the seller to 90 days of credit risk.

Best Practice: For new buyers or high-risk markets, use "at sight" (D/P). For established relationships, a 30-60 day usance draft (D/A) may be acceptable. Always align the tenor with the underlying sales contract.
Source: URC 522, Article 7 (Release of Documents); UCC Article 3, Section 3-108.

TENOR — Collection Form Field

Form Field
TENOR

The field on the collection form where the payment timing is specified. Must match the tenor stated on the Bill of Exchange.

What to Enter

  • Sight: Enter "SIGHT" or "AT SIGHT" for D/P collections.
  • Usance: Enter the number of days and basis, e.g., "60 DAYS AFTER SIGHT" or "90 DAYS AFTER DATE".
  • Fixed date: Enter "PAYABLE ON [DATE]" for maturity-date drafts.
Consistency Check: The tenor on the collection form, the tenor on the draft, and the payment terms in the sales contract must all match. Any discrepancy will cause the collecting bank to seek clarification, delaying the collection.
Source: URC 522, Article 4 (Form of Collection).

Documents Against Acceptance (D/A)

Collection Type
Documents Against Acceptance (D/A)

A documentary collection arrangement where the collecting bank releases the shipping documents to the buyer upon the buyer's acceptance (signature) of a time draft, rather than upon immediate payment. The buyer pays at maturity.

How D/A Works

  1. Seller ships goods and sends documents + time draft to remitting bank.
  2. Remitting bank forwards to collecting bank in buyer's country.
  3. Collecting bank presents draft to buyer for acceptance.
  4. Buyer signs (accepts) the draft, promising to pay at maturity.
  5. Collecting bank releases documents to buyer immediately upon acceptance.
  6. Buyer can now clear customs and take possession of goods.
  7. At maturity, buyer pays the accepted draft amount.
  8. Collecting bank remits funds to remitting bank, who credits the seller.

Risk Analysis

  • Seller Risk: HIGH. Once documents are released, the seller has no control over the goods. If the buyer defaults at maturity, the seller must pursue legal remedies abroad.
  • Buyer Benefit: The buyer gets goods immediately and can sell them before paying, effectively using trade credit.
When to Use D/A: Only for trusted, long-standing buyers in stable markets. Consider requiring a bank aval (guarantee) on the accepted draft for additional security. For new buyers, always use D/P.
Source: URC 522, Article 7; ICC Guide to Documentary Collections.

Credit Our Commercial Account

Settlement Instruction
Credit Our Commercial Account

The instruction specifying where the remitting bank (Zions Bank) should deposit the collected funds once payment is received from the buyer. This is the seller's (drawer's) bank account.

Required Information

  • Account Name: The legal name on the bank account. Should match the drawer's name.
  • Bank Name: The full name of the depository institution.
  • Bank Address: The bank's physical address (may be required for international wires).
  • Account Number: The specific account where funds should be deposited.
  • ABA / Bank Transit Number: The 9-digit routing number for U.S. banks. For international transfers, a SWIFT/BIC code may also be needed.
Wire Transfer Tip: For international collections, ensure the account can receive foreign currency wires or confirm with your bank that USD wires are accepted. Provide the SWIFT/BIC code if the bank is outside the U.S.
Source: URC 522, Article 19 (Interest, Charges and Expenses); Zions Bank Wire Transfer Guidelines.

ABA / Bank Transit Number

Bank Identifier
ABA / Bank Transit Number

A 9-digit routing number used to identify U.S. financial institutions. It tells the Federal Reserve and other banks where to send funds during wire transfers and ACH transactions.

Structure

  • Digits 1-4: Federal Reserve routing symbol (identifies the Federal Reserve district and processing center).
  • Digits 5-8: Institution identifier (unique to the bank or credit union).
  • Digit 9: Check digit (calculated using a checksum algorithm to detect errors).

For International Transfers

For collections from overseas buyers, the ABA number is used to route the incoming wire to the correct U.S. bank. The seller may also need to provide:

  • SWIFT/BIC Code: An international bank identifier used for cross-border wires.
  • IBAN: International Bank Account Number (used mainly in Europe and other regions).
  • Intermediary Bank: A correspondent bank that handles the transfer if the seller's bank does not have a direct relationship with the foreign bank.
Verification: Always verify the ABA number with your bank before submitting. An incorrect routing number will cause wire delays or returns. You can verify ABA numbers at the Federal Reserve's official directory.
Source: American Bankers Association; Federal Reserve Bank Services.

UNCITRAL Convention on International Bills of Exchange

International Convention
UNCITRAL Convention (1988)

The United Nations Convention on International Bills of Exchange and International Promissory Notes, adopted by the UN Commission on International Trade Law (UNCITRAL) in 1988. It provides a uniform legal framework for negotiable instruments used in international trade.

Key Articles Referenced on the Bill

  • Articles 1-7: Scope of application, definitions, and interpretation of the Convention.
  • Articles 11-13: Form and content requirements for an international bill of exchange.
  • Articles 46(1)-(3): Rules governing acceptance of a bill — when, how, and the legal effect of acceptance.
  • Articles 47(1)-(4): Rules governing payment — the drawee's obligation to pay at maturity and discharge upon payment.
  • Articles 51-53: Rules governing endorsement — transfer of rights, liability of endorsers, and conditions of endorsement.

Why It Matters

The Convention harmonizes the law of bills of exchange across signatory countries, reducing legal uncertainty in international trade. By referencing the Convention on the bill, the parties agree that the instrument is governed by these uniform rules rather than purely domestic law.

Note: The U.S. has signed but not ratified the UNCITRAL Convention. In practice, bills of exchange in U.S. trade are typically governed by the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC) Article 3. The Convention reference on this form is a standard Zions Bank template provision.
Source: UNCITRAL Convention on International Bills of Exchange and International Promissory Notes (1988); U.S. UCC Article 3 (Negotiable Instruments). UNCITRAL Website

Drawer — Bill of Exchange

Bill of Exchange Party
Drawer

The person or entity who creates and signs the Bill of Exchange, ordering the drawee to pay. On this form, the drawer is the seller (customer) who is exporting goods and seeking payment.

Required Signature

The drawer must sign the Bill of Exchange in the space provided. An unauthorized signature or a signature by an agent without disclosed authority may make the bill non-enforceable.

Location

The "Location" field indicates where the drawer is situated (city, state/country). This may be relevant for determining applicable law and jurisdiction in case of disputes.

Signature Requirement: The Bill of Exchange must be signed by the drawer to be valid. An unsigned draft is not a negotiable instrument and cannot be enforced. The signature should match the name in the "Drawer" field exactly.
Source: UCC Article 3, Section 3-401 (Signature); UNCITRAL Convention, Article 11.

Air Waybill (AWB)

Transport Document
Air Waybill

A transport document issued by an airline or air freight forwarder acknowledging receipt of goods for air shipment. Unlike a Bill of Lading, it is not a document of title and is always non-negotiable.

Key Characteristics

  • Non-negotiable: The consignee named on the AWB can claim the goods without presenting the original document. The AWB is typically sent directly to the consignee upon arrival.
  • Receipt and contract: Serves as a receipt for the goods and evidence of the contract of carriage between the shipper and the carrier.
  • Speed: Air freight is faster than sea transport, so AWBs are used when time is critical.

Role in Documentary Collection

For air shipments, the AWB is included in the collection documents. However, since it is non-negotiable, the seller has less control than with a negotiable Bill of Lading. The buyer may be able to claim goods before payment unless special arrangements are made.

Security Note: For high-value air shipments, consider using a bank aval or letter of credit instead of documentary collection, as the non-negotiable nature of AWBs reduces the seller's leverage.
Source: IATA Air Waybill Rules; URC 522, Article 4.

Non-Negotiable Bill of Lading (N/N BoL)

Transport Document
N/N BoL

A Bill of Lading marked "non-negotiable" that serves as a receipt for goods and contract of carriage but is not a document of title. The named consignee can claim the goods without presenting the original.

How It Differs from Original BoL

  • Original BoL: Document of title. Must be presented to claim goods. Gives seller control over goods until payment.
  • N/N BoL: Receipt only. Named consignee claims goods by identity. Seller loses control once goods are shipped.

When Used

N/N BoLs are typically used when the buyer and seller have a trusted relationship, or when the goods are shipped to a branch office or affiliate of the seller. They may also be used for domestic shipments where control is less critical.

Risk Warning: In documentary collections, avoid N/N BoLs for new buyers or high-risk markets. Once the carrier releases goods to the named consignee, the seller has no recourse if payment is not received.
Source: URC 522, Article 4; Hague-Visby Rules.

Commercial Invoice

Trade Document
Invoice

A commercial document issued by the seller to the buyer, detailing the goods or services provided, their quantities, prices, and payment terms. It serves as a demand for payment and is used for customs valuation.

Required Information

  • Seller and buyer details: Complete names and addresses of both parties.
  • Goods description: Clear description of the merchandise, including HS codes if required.
  • Quantity and unit price: Number of units and price per unit.
  • Total amount: The final amount due, typically in the currency of payment.
  • Payment terms: How and when payment is due (e.g., "Net 30 days").

Role in Documentary Collection

The invoice is a key document in the collection. The collecting bank presents it to the buyer along with other documents. The draft amount must match the invoice total. Discrepancies can cause payment delays or rejection.

Accuracy Check: Ensure the invoice amount matches the draft amount exactly. Banks are strict about discrepancies — even minor differences can cause the collection to be rejected.
Source: URC 522, Article 4; ICC Guide to Export-Import.

Packing List

Trade Document
Packing List

A document that provides detailed information about the shipment's packaging, including the number of packages, their contents, weights, and measurements. It complements the commercial invoice.

Key Information

  • Package count: Total number of boxes, crates, pallets, or containers.
  • Contents per package: What each package contains.
  • Weight and dimensions: Gross and net weight, plus measurements of each package.
  • Marks and numbers: Shipping marks on each package for identification.

Purpose in Trade

The packing list helps customs officials verify the shipment and assists the buyer in checking the goods upon arrival. It is particularly important for partial shipments or when goods are packed in multiple containers.

Verification: The packing list should match the commercial invoice in terms of quantity and description. Any discrepancies may cause customs delays or questions from the buyer.
Source: URC 522, Article 4; ICC Guide to Export-Import.

Certificate of Origin

Trade Document
Certificate of Origin

A document certifying that the goods in a particular export shipment are wholly obtained, produced, manufactured, or processed in a specific country. It is often required for customs clearance and to determine tariff rates.

Why It Matters

  • Customs duty: Determines the tariff rate applied — preferential rates may apply under free trade agreements.
  • Trade restrictions: Some countries restrict imports from certain origins; the certificate verifies compliance.
  • Quota management: Used to track import quotas from specific countries.

Who Issues It

Certificates of Origin are typically issued by chambers of commerce, trade associations, or government authorities. The exporter must provide supporting documentation to prove the origin of the goods.

Free Trade Agreements: If shipping under a free trade agreement (e.g., USMCA, EU agreements), a specific certificate format may be required to claim preferential duty rates. Check the destination country's requirements.
Source: World Trade Organization; ICC Certificate of Origin Guidelines.

Insurance Certificate / Policy

Trade Document
Insurance Certificate

A document evidencing that insurance coverage has been obtained for the goods during transit. It specifies the risks covered, the insured value, and the duration of coverage.

Types of Coverage

  • All-risk: Covers all physical loss or damage from external causes, with specific exclusions.
  • With average: Covers partial losses; general average contribution may apply.
  • Free of particular average (FPA):strong> Covers only total losses or specified partial losses.

When Required

Insurance documents are typically required when the sales contract specifies CIF or CIP terms (Cost, Insurance and Freight), where the seller is responsible for obtaining insurance. Under other terms (FOB, EXW), insurance may be optional or the buyer's responsibility.

Coverage Amount: The insured value should typically be at least 110% of the CIF value to cover expected profit and incidental costs. Check the sales contract for specific requirements.
Source: Institute Cargo Clauses; Incoterms 2020 Rules.

Courier Receipt

Transport Document
Courier Receipt

A receipt issued by a courier service (e.g., FedEx, DHL, UPS) acknowledging receipt of documents or small packages for delivery. It provides tracking information and proof of shipment.

Purpose in Trade

  • Document delivery: Used to send original documents (BoL, drafts, etc.) to banks or buyers.
  • Proof of dispatch: Establishes when documents were sent and provides tracking for security.
  • Sample shipments: Used for small parcel shipments that don't require full freight documentation.

Bank Requirements

In documentary collections, banks may require courier receipts as evidence that documents have been dispatched to the appropriate parties. The receipt should show the date of dispatch and the recipient's details.

Tracking: Always retain the courier tracking number and receipt copy. If documents are delayed or lost, the tracking number is essential for tracing and making claims.
Source: URC 522, Article 4; ICC Guide to Documentary Collections.

Documents Against Payment (D/P)

Collection Type
Documents Against Payment (D/P)

A documentary collection arrangement where the collecting bank releases the shipping documents to the buyer only upon payment of the draft. The buyer cannot take possession of the goods without paying.

How D/P Works

  1. Seller ships goods and sends documents + sight draft to remitting bank.
  2. Remitting bank forwards to collecting bank in buyer's country.
  3. Collecting bank presents documents and draft to buyer for payment.
  4. Buyer pays the draft amount in full.
  5. Collecting bank releases documents to buyer only after payment is received.
  6. Buyer can now clear customs and take possession of goods.
  7. Collecting bank remits funds to remitting bank, who credits the seller.

Security for Seller

D/P provides good security for the seller because the buyer cannot obtain the goods without paying. The seller retains control through the documents until payment is received.

Best Practice: D/P is recommended for new buyers or high-risk markets. For trusted buyers with established relationships, D/A (Documents Against Acceptance) may be appropriate to offer financing terms.
Source: URC 522, Article 7; ICC Guide to Documentary Collections.

Sight Draft / At Sight

Draft Type
Sight Draft

A draft payable immediately upon presentation to the drawee. No waiting period — payment is due when the draft is presented (at "sight").

Characteristics

  • Immediate payment: The drawee must pay when the draft is presented.
  • No credit period: No days-after-sight or days-after-date calculation.
  • Used with D/P: Sight drafts are the standard for Documents Against Payment collections.

Sample Wording

"At sight" or "Payable at sight" or "Upon presentation". The draft should clearly indicate that payment is due immediately.

Advantage for Seller: Sight drafts provide immediate payment (subject to bank processing times) and are the most secure form of draft for documentary collections. The buyer cannot delay payment by accepting a time draft.
Source: UCC Article 3, Section 3-108; URC 522, Article 7.

Protest (of a Draft)

Legal Action
Protest

A formal legal declaration made by a notary public or other authorized official that a draft or bill of exchange was presented for payment or acceptance and was refused. It creates legal evidence of the refusal.

Purpose of Protest

  • Evidence of dishonor: Provides official proof that the draft was not paid or accepted.
  • Legal recourse: Required in some jurisdictions to preserve the right to sue the drawer or endorsers.
  • Holder protection: Protects the holder's rights against prior parties to the instrument.

When Not Required

Many collection instructions specify "DO NOT PROTEST" because protest is expensive, time-consuming, and may not be required under modern law or banking practices. Banks typically handle dishonor through formal notices rather than protest.

Modern Practice: In most international trade, protest is rarely used. Banks prefer to handle dishonor through swift communication and notice procedures. The "DO NOT PROTEST" instruction is standard to avoid unnecessary costs.
Source: UCC Article 3, Section 3-507; URC 522, Article 16.

Place of Drawing

Bill of Exchange Field
Place of Drawing

The location (city, state/country) where the Bill of Exchange is created and signed by the drawer. This field identifies the legal jurisdiction of the instrument's creation.

Legal Significance

  • Governing law: May determine which country's law applies to interpretation of the bill.
  • Jurisdiction: Relevant for disputes and legal proceedings.
  • UNCITRAL: For international bills, the place of drawing is one factor in determining whether the Convention applies.

What to Enter

Enter the city and country where the drawer is located when creating the bill. For example: "New York, USA" or "London, UK".

Consistency: The place of drawing should match the drawer's location and the location where the bill is actually signed. Inconsistencies may raise questions about the bill's validity.
Source: UNCITRAL Convention, Article 1; UCC Article 3.

Pay To The Order Of

Negotiable Instrument Language
Pay To The Order Of

The standard wording used on negotiable instruments (bills of exchange, checks, promissory notes) to indicate that payment is to be made to a specific person or entity, or to that person's order (meaning it can be transferred to others by endorsement).

Why This Wording Matters

  • Negotiability: "To the order of" makes the instrument negotiable — it can be transferred by endorsement.
  • Identification of payee: Clearly specifies who is entitled to receive payment.
  • Legal requirement: Under negotiable instruments law, this wording helps establish the instrument as negotiable.

Alternatives

  • "Pay to the order of [Name]": Standard negotiable form.
  • "Pay to bearer": Payable to whoever holds the instrument — rare in trade finance due to security concerns.
  • "Pay to [Name]": May be non-negotiable depending on jurisdiction.
Payee Identification: The payee name should match the name on the account where funds will be deposited. In documentary collections, the payee is typically the drawer (seller) or the remitting bank.
Source: UCC Article 3, Section 3-104; UNCITRAL Convention, Article 11.

Value Received

Bill of Exchange Clause
Value Received

A standard clause on bills of exchange indicating that the drawer has received value (goods, services, or other consideration) in exchange for the instrument. It confirms that the bill is supported by an underlying transaction.

Legal Purpose

  • Consideration: Establishes that the bill is given for value, making it a valid negotiable instrument.
  • Defense against claims: Helps prevent claims that the bill was given without consideration.
  • Standard practice: Traditional wording that confirms the underlying trade transaction.

What It Means

"Value Received" means the drawer has received something of value (typically the goods being exported) in return for the buyer's promise to pay. It links the bill to the underlying commercial transaction.

Modern Practice: While traditional, some modern bills omit this clause. However, including it maintains standard form and provides clarity about the underlying transaction.
Source: UCC Article 3, Section 3-303; Bills of Exchange Act 1882 (UK).

Aval

Bank Guarantee
Aval

A guarantee by a bank (or other party) on a bill of exchange, whereby the bank undertakes to pay the bill if the drawee defaults. The bank's signature on the face of the bill constitutes the aval.

How Aval Works

  • Bank signature: The bank signs the bill, adding words like "Good as Aval" or "Per Aval".
  • Guaranteed payment: The bank becomes jointly and severally liable for payment.
  • Enhanced security: The seller can rely on the bank's credit rather than the buyer's.

Benefits

An aval transforms a trade acceptance (buyer's promise to pay) into a bank-guaranteed instrument. The seller can discount or sell the avalized draft more easily, as banks are more creditworthy than most commercial buyers.

When to Use: Avals are particularly useful in D/A collections where the seller is concerned about the buyer's creditworthiness. The aval provides bank-backed security while still offering the buyer credit terms.
Source: UNCITRAL Convention, Articles 46-48; UCC Article 3, Section 3-419.

Endorsement

Negotiable Instrument Action
Endorsement

The signature of the holder of a negotiable instrument (bill of exchange, draft, or check) on the back of the instrument, which transfers ownership or rights to another party.

Types of Endorsement

  • Blank endorsement: Signature only — makes the instrument payable to bearer (risky).
  • Special endorsement: Signature + "Pay to the order of [Name]" — transfers to a specific payee.
  • Restrictive endorsement: Limits further transfer (e.g., "For deposit only").
  • Conditional endorsement: Includes conditions (may not be valid in all jurisdictions).

Role in Documentary Collection

When a seller endorses a draft to their bank, they transfer the right to collect payment. The bank then presents the endorsed draft to the buyer's bank for collection. Proper endorsement is essential for the chain of title.

Bank Endorsement: When remitting documents to your bank, ensure endorsements are properly completed. An improperly endorsed draft may be rejected by the collecting bank, causing delays.
Source: UCC Article 3, Sections 3-204 to 3-206; UNCITRAL Convention, Articles 51-53.

Account Name

Bank Account Field
Account Name

The legal name of the account holder as registered with the bank. This must match exactly the name on the account to ensure proper credit of funds.

Requirements

  • Exact match: Must match the bank's records exactly, including spacing and punctuation.
  • Business or personal: Can be either a business name or individual's name, depending on account type.
  • International accounts: For international transfers, use the name as it appears on SWIFT documentation.

What to Enter

Enter the complete legal name of the account holder. For businesses, use the full corporate name. For individuals, use first and last name as registered with the bank.

Accuracy Critical: Incorrect account names can cause payment delays or rejection. Banks may reject transfers with name mismatches under anti-money laundering regulations.
Source: International Banking Standards; SWIFT MT202 Field 50A/K.

Account Number

Bank Account Field
Account Number

The unique identifier for a specific bank account, used to route funds to the correct account within a financial institution.

Format Variations

  • US accounts: Typically 8-17 digits, varies by bank.
  • IBAN: International Bank Account Number, used in Europe and other regions (up to 34 alphanumeric characters).
  • UK accounts: 8-digit account number plus 6-digit sort code.

What to Enter

Enter the complete account number without spaces or dashes. For international payments, include the IBAN if required by the destination country.

Verification: Always verify account numbers with the bank before initiating international transfers. Errors can result in misdirected payments that are difficult to recover.
Source: SWIFT Standards; ISO 13616 (IBAN); Federal Reserve Regulation J.

Bank Name

Bank Account Field
Bank Name

The official legal name of the financial institution where the account is held. This identifies the receiving bank in international transfers.

Importance in International Transfers

  • SWIFT identification: Bank name must match SWIFT directory listings.
  • Correspondent banking: Some banks use different legal names for various operations.
  • Branch identification: Large banks may require specific branch names for proper routing.

What to Enter

Enter the full official bank name as it appears on bank statements and SWIFT documentation. Avoid abbreviations unless they are part of the official legal name.

Official Names: Use "JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A." not "Chase Bank". Use "Bank of America, N.A." not "BOA". Exact official names prevent routing errors.
Source: SWIFT Directory; Financial Institution Standards.

Bank Address

Bank Account Field
Bank Address

The physical location of the bank branch where the account is maintained. Required for international wire transfers and correspondence.

Address Requirements

  • Street address: Required for most international transfers.
  • City and country: Essential for routing through correspondent banks.
  • Postal code: Required for many countries' banking systems.

International Format

Use standard international addressing format: Street address, City, State/Province (if applicable), Postal Code, Country. Country name should be in English.

Head Office vs. Branch: For large banks, use the specific branch address where the account is held, not the bank's headquarters address, unless instructed otherwise by the bank.
Source: SWIFT Messaging Standards; Universal Postal Union guidelines.

Bank ABA/Transit Number

Bank Routing Identifier
ABA/Transit Number

A 9-digit code used to identify financial institutions in the United States. Also known as Routing Transit Number (RTN) or ABA Routing Number.

Structure and Purpose

  • 9 digits: First 4 digits Federal Reserve routing, next 4 ABA institution identifier, last digit check digit.
  • Domestic transfers: Required for all U.S. bank-to-bank transfers (ACH, wire transfers).
  • Check processing: Printed on checks (bottom left, MICR line).

International Context

For international transfers to U.S. accounts, the ABA number works with SWIFT codes to route funds through correspondent banking networks.

Verification: ABA numbers can be verified on the Federal Reserve's ABA Routing Number lookup website. Always verify before initiating transfers to avoid errors.
Source: American Bankers Association; Federal Reserve Regulation J; SWIFT Standards.

Other Documents

Trade Document Category
Other Documents

A catch-all category for any additional documents required by the documentary collection that don't fit into standard document types like invoices or bills of lading.

Common Examples

  • Certificates of analysis: For chemicals, pharmaceuticals, or food products.
  • Quality certificates: Inspection reports certifying product quality.
  • Weight lists: Detailed breakdown of shipment weights.
  • Phytosanitary certificates: For agricultural products.
  • Export licenses: Required for controlled goods.

When to Use

Use this category when the collection instructions specify documents not listed in the standard table, or when special certificates are required by the importer's country regulations.

Country-Specific Requirements: Many countries have unique documentation requirements. Always verify what documents are required for the specific destination country before shipment.
Source: ICC Uniform Rules for Collections (URC 522); Country-specific import regulations.

Originals

Document Classification
Original Documents

The primary, authoritative versions of trade documents that represent legal title to goods and are required for customs clearance and payment collection.

Critical Original Documents

  • Bill of Lading: Originals represent title to goods - typically 3 originals issued.
  • Drafts: Signed originals required for negotiation and payment.
  • Commercial invoices: Original may be required for customs purposes.
  • Certificates of origin: Often required as originals for preferential tariff treatment.

Documentary Collection Requirements

In documentary collections, banks require specified numbers of originals to present to the drawee. Original negotiable documents (like bills of lading) are essential because only originals convey title to goods.

Safe Handling: Original documents are valuable and should be handled carefully. Loss of original bills of lading can require expensive court bonds to obtain replacement documents.
Source: ICC Uniform Rules for Collections (URC 522) Articles 4-5; Standard Trade Documentation Practices.

Duplicates (Copies)

Document Classification
Duplicates/Copies

Reproductions of original documents used for record-keeping, informational purposes, or as non-negotiable copies when originals are not required.

Uses for Duplicates

  • Record keeping: Seller's files and internal documentation.
  • Customs copies: Some countries accept copies for certain customs procedures.
  • Informational: Advance copies sent to buyers for shipment planning.
  • Non-negotiable: Copies that cannot transfer title to goods.

Collection Instructions

The documentary collection instructions specify how many copies of each document are required. Banks may present copies when originals are not needed or when multiple parties require documentation.

Marking: Duplicates should be clearly marked "COPY" or "DUPLICATE" to avoid confusion with originals. However, ensure such markings don't interfere with required document formatting.
Source: ICC Uniform Rules for Collections (URC 522); International Trade Documentation Standards.

Authorized Representative

Legal Authority
Authorized Representative

An individual who has been granted legal authority by a company, organization, or principal to act on their behalf in signing documents, making commitments, and conducting transactions.

Role in Trade Finance

  • Signature authority: Can legally bind the company to obligations through their signature.
  • Drawing authority: Authorized to sign bills of exchange, drafts, and other negotiable instruments.
  • Bank mandates: Named in bank resolution documents as having authority to operate accounts.
  • Legal representation: Acts as the company's agent in documentary collections and trade transactions.

Requirements for Validity

An authorized representative must have proper documentation demonstrating their authority, such as a board resolution, power of attorney, or corporate secretary certificate. Banks typically verify this authority before accepting signed documents.

Bank Verification: When signing on behalf of a company, ensure your bank has current documentation of your authorization. Outdated or missing authorization documents can cause rejection of transactions.
Source: UCC Article 3; Corporate Law Principles; UNCITRAL Convention on Bills of Exchange.
